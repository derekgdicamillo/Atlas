# 02 — Clinical Presentation

## 1. The Multi-System Symptom Picture

Parasitic infection is a great mimic. A single organism can produce GI, dermatologic, neurologic, hematologic, immune, and metabolic symptoms simultaneously. The functional medicine clinician should suspect parasites in any chronic, multi-system, treatment-refractory presentation, especially one with eosinophilia, IgE elevation, sleep disturbance at full moon, anal/nasal itching, or animal/water exposure.

## 2. Symptoms by System

### 2.1 Gastrointestinal
- Bloating, gas, abdominal cramping (often after meals or in evening)
- Alternating constipation and diarrhea
- Steatorrhea (Giardia, Strongyloides — fat malabsorption)
- Mucus or undigested food in stool
- Visible worms or proglottids (Ascaris, Taenia, Enterobius)
- Anal itching, especially nocturnal (Enterobius, Taenia)
- Right upper quadrant pain (Fasciola, Clonorchis, Ascaris in biliary tree, Echinococcus cyst)
- Rectal prolapse in heavy Trichuris infection (children)
- Reflux, early satiety, gastroparesis-like (Anisakis, gastric Strongyloides)
- Weight loss with normal or increased appetite
- New food sensitivities post-GI illness

### 2.2 Dermatologic
- Chronic urticaria (Blastocystis, Strongyloides, Anisakis, Giardia)
- Eczema and atopic dermatitis (especially worsening with full moon)
- Larva currens — serpiginous, migrating urticarial track (Strongyloides)
- Cutaneous larva migrans — creeping eruption (animal hookworm)
- Cercarial dermatitis ("swimmer's itch") — Schistosoma
- Pruritus ani (pinworm in children, taeniasis in adults)
- Pruritus nasi
- Periorbital edema (Trichinella, loiasis)
- Rosacea, acne rosacea-like rash (Demodex, possibly Helicobacter overlap)
- Hives, angioedema, dermatographism

### 2.3 Neurologic and Psychiatric
- Brain fog, poor concentration
- Insomnia, especially around full moon
- Anxiety, irritability ("worm-like restlessness")
- Mood swings, depression
- Headaches, migraines
- Bruxism (teeth grinding) — classic functional sign though weak literature
- Vertigo
- Seizures (neurocysticercosis, cerebral toxoplasmosis, hydatid)
- Cognitive decline (chronic toxoplasmosis associations)
- Behavioral changes in children (PANDAS/PANS overlap with parasite reactivity)

### 2.4 Immune and Allergic
- Unexplained peripheral eosinophilia (>500/µL absolute)
- Elevated total IgE without atopy
- New or worsening allergies, MCAS-like flares
- Asthma flares (Loeffler syndrome with Ascaris/hookworm/Strongyloides migration)
- Recurrent infections, low secretory IgA
- Autoimmune flares (Hashimoto, RA, lupus, MS) — molecular mimicry hypothesis

### 2.5 Hematologic
- Iron deficiency anemia (hookworm, schistosomiasis, heavy Trichuris)
- B12 deficiency (Diphyllobothrium)
- Hemolytic anemia, low haptoglobin (Babesia, malaria)
- Thrombocytopenia (Babesia, malaria, hyperinfection)
- Eosinophilia patterns:
  - Tissue-migrating helminths: high
  - Protozoa (Giardia, Crypto): typically normal
  - Encysted helminths (cysticercosis): normal between flares

### 2.6 Constitutional and Metabolic
- Fatigue, post-exertional malaise
- Night sweats (Babesia, lymphatic filariasis)
- Low-grade fevers
- Air hunger, breathlessness without cardiopulmonary cause (Babesia hallmark)
- Weight loss or failure to thrive
- Reactive hypoglycemia
- Cravings for sugar, refined carbs ("worms feed sugar craving" — folk wisdom with partial support; helminths ferment carbohydrates and protozoa upregulate host glucose uptake)
- Cravings for raw meat, dirt (pica)
- Reduced libido, hormonal disruption

### 2.7 Genitourinary
- Recurrent UTI without bacterial growth (Trichomonas, Schistosoma haematobium)
- Hematuria (Schistosoma haematobium)
- Vaginitis, vulvovaginal itching (Trichomonas, Enterobius migration)
- Prostatitis-like (Trichomonas)

### 2.8 Musculoskeletal
- Myalgia, especially with eosinophilia (Trichinella, toxocariasis)
- Arthralgia, migratory joint pain
- Periorbital and facial myositis (Trichinella)

### 2.9 Cardiopulmonary
- Cough, wheeze during migration (Loeffler with Ascaris, hookworm, Strongyloides)
- Cardiomyopathy (chronic Chagas, late-stage trypanosomiasis)
- Pulmonary nodules (Paragonimus, Echinococcus, dirofilaria)

## 3. Pattern Clusters That Should Raise Suspicion

### Cluster A — "Chronic GI plus skin"
Bloating + chronic urticaria/eczema + brain fog → think Blastocystis, Giardia, Dientamoeba, Strongyloides.

### Cluster B — "Tick exposure plus air hunger"
Lyme history + drenching night sweats + air hunger + headache → Babesia.

### Cluster C — "Travel/immigrant plus eosinophilia"
Tropical exposure + eosinophilia ± migrating urticaria → Strongyloides, schistosomiasis, filariasis.

### Cluster D — "Pediatric anal itch + insomnia"
Nocturnal perianal itching + irritability + tooth grinding → pinworm.

### Cluster E — "Cat owner with neuropsych symptoms"
Cat household + anxiety, panic, brain fog ± toxoplasmosis IgG positive → consider chronic toxoplasmosis reactivation, especially with iron dysregulation.

### Cluster F — "Refractory IBS"
Years of IBS-D/IBS-M, normal colonoscopy, failed FODMAP trials → multi-target stool PCR for Blastocystis, Dientamoeba, Giardia, Cryptosporidium.

### Cluster G — "Pre-immunosuppression patient"
Anyone about to start chemo, biologics (especially anti-TNF, JAK inhibitors), high-dose steroids, or transplant → screen for Strongyloides serology mandatorily, regardless of symptoms.

## 4. Red Flags Requiring Conventional Workup First

- Bloody diarrhea → rule out invasive amebiasis, EHEC, IBD
- Acute hemolysis → malaria/babesia smear, urgent referral
- Seizures, focal neuro deficits → CT/MRI, infectious disease referral (cysticercosis, toxoplasmosis, hydatid)
- Hepatic mass on imaging → echinococcosis serology before any biopsy (cyst rupture causes anaphylaxis)
- Ocular floaters with retinitis → toxoplasmosis, toxocariasis ocular exam
- Hyperinfection signs in immunosuppressed (gram-negative sepsis, ARDS, GI bleeding) → suspect Strongyloides hyperinfection, ID consult
- Pregnancy with new infection → toxoplasmosis, malaria, Chagas need specialty referral

## 5. Differential Diagnosis to Always Run in Parallel

Before assuming parasites, ensure these are considered:

1. **SIBO/IMO** — overlapping bloating, distention. Hydrogen/methane breath test.
2. **Candida overgrowth** — overlapping skin, brain fog, sugar cravings. OAT for arabinose, tartaric acid.
3. **H. pylori** — overlapping GI, rosacea, B12, iron deficiency. Stool antigen or breath test.
4. **Mold/mycotoxin illness** — major overlap (MCAS, fatigue, neuro). Urine mycotoxin panel.
5. **Lyme and co-infections** — Babesia, Bartonella, Borrelia overlap heavily with parasite presentations. Clinical, ELISA + Western blot, FISH if available.
6. **Mast cell activation syndrome** — chronic urticaria, GI, neuro. Tryptase, n-methylhistamine, prostaglandin D2.
7. **Celiac and non-celiac gluten reactivity** — GI plus skin plus neuro overlap.
8. **IBD** — bloody stool, weight loss, iron deficiency. Calprotectin, scope.
9. **Thyroid disease** — fatigue, weight, mood. Full panel with antibodies.
10. **Heavy metal toxicity** — neuro, fatigue, pediatric behavioral. Provoked or hair testing with caveats.

The functional medicine answer is rarely "either/or." It is usually a primary insult layered with secondary infections, including parasites.

## 6. Severity Stratification for Treatment Planning

| Tier | Profile | Approach |
|------|---------|----------|
| **Tier 1: Empiric / preventive** | Asymptomatic, low-risk household member of treated patient, or prophylactic family deworming | Short herbal protocol, dietary support, no testing |
| **Tier 2: Symptomatic, immunocompetent, low-acuity** | Chronic GI, skin, fatigue. Stable. | Test → 4-phase functional protocol. Herbs primary. |
| **Tier 3: Symptomatic, multi-system, moderate acuity** | MCAS overlap, MTHFR, mycotoxin co-burden, eosinophilia. | Test broadly. Extended prep phase. Herbs ± Rx. Drainage critical. |
| **Tier 4: High-risk / high-acuity** | Travel-acquired tropical infection, immunocompromised, pregnant, pediatric severe, neuro/ocular involvement, hemolysis, eosinophilia >2000. | Conventional ID referral. Pharmaceutical primary. Functional support adjunctive. |

Atlas should always identify tier before generating a protocol, and refuse to generate Tier 4 protocols without a licensed practitioner attesting clinician oversight.
