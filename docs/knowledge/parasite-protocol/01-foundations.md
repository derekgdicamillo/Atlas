# 01 — Foundations: Parasite Biology, Taxonomy, Life Cycles, Prevalence

## 1. Why Parasites Matter in Functional Medicine

Conventional medicine in the U.S. tends to consider parasitic infection rare outside of travel medicine, immigration, immunocompromise, or daycare exposures. Functional medicine takes a wider view because:

1. **Sub-clinical chronic infection is common.** Blastocystis hominis is found in 5–25% of stool samples in industrialized countries; in symptomatic GI cohorts, 30–50%. Dientamoeba fragilis ranges from 0.4% to 42% depending on population and assay (PCR raises detection 10x over microscopy).
2. **Standard ova-and-parasite microscopy misses 50–80% of infections** when only 1–2 specimens are submitted. Multi-target PCR and antigen panels detect organisms missed by O&P.
3. **Parasites disrupt the gut ecosystem** (mucosal IgA, microbiome diversity, brush-border enzyme expression) and drive systemic inflammation, MCAS-like presentations, autoimmune flares, and HPA axis dysregulation.
4. **Parasite-microbiome-mycotoxin overlap.** Klinghardt model: parasites carry bacterial and viral co-infections (Bartonella, Borrelia) and concentrate heavy metals and mycotoxins. Killing them without drainage and binders can worsen symptoms.

## 2. Three Major Categories

### 2.1 Protozoa (single-celled eukaryotes)

| Organism | Habitat | Key facts |
|----------|---------|-----------|
| **Giardia lamblia (intestinalis, duodenalis)** | Small intestine | Most common waterborne parasite in the U.S. Cysts survive chlorination. Lactase deficiency and IBS-D persist post-clearance. |
| **Blastocystis hominis (subtypes 1–9)** | Colon | Most prevalent enteric parasite globally. Subtypes 1, 3, 7 most associated with IBS, urticaria, fatigue. |
| **Entamoeba histolytica** vs **E. dispar / E. moshkovskii** | Colon | Only histolytica is invasive; dispar/moshkovskii are commensal but indistinguishable on microscopy. PCR or antigen needed. |
| **Cryptosporidium parvum / hominis** | Small bowel | Self-limited in immunocompetent, severe in HIV/transplant. Chlorine-resistant; responsible for major waterborne outbreaks. |
| **Dientamoeba fragilis** | Colon | Long thought commensal; now accepted pathogen. Strongly linked to chronic abdominal pain in children. |
| **Cyclospora cayetanensis** | Small bowel | Outbreaks via contaminated produce (raspberries, basil, cilantro). Prolonged watery diarrhea, weight loss. |
| **Trichomonas vaginalis** | GU tract | Most common non-viral STI worldwide. Increases HIV transmission risk. |
| **Toxoplasma gondii** | Multi-organ, CNS, muscle | Cat-feces or undercooked meat. Latent cysts in 11–30% of U.S. adults. Reactivation in immunocompromise; behavioral and neuropsychiatric associations. |
| **Babesia microti / divergens / duncani** | RBCs | Tick-borne; Lyme co-infection. Air hunger, night sweats, drenching sweats, hemolysis. |
| **Plasmodium falciparum/vivax/ovale/malariae/knowlesi** | RBCs, liver | Travel-associated. Falciparum is medical emergency. |
| **Leishmania spp.** | Skin, mucosa, viscera | Sandfly vector. Cutaneous, mucocutaneous, visceral (kala-azar) forms. |
| **Trypanosoma cruzi (Chagas)** | Cardiac, GI smooth muscle | Endemic in Latin America; underdiagnosed in U.S. immigrants. Causes cardiomyopathy and megacolon decades later. |
| **Trypanosoma brucei** | CNS, lymph | African sleeping sickness. |
| **Naegleria fowleri / Acanthamoeba** | CNS, cornea | Rare but devastating; warm freshwater exposure, contact lens contamination. |

### 2.2 Helminths (multi-cellular worms)

#### Nematodes (roundworms)

| Organism | Notes |
|----------|-------|
| **Ascaris lumbricoides** | Most common helminth globally (~800M). Pulmonary migration phase (Loeffler syndrome). Adults reach 30 cm. |
| **Enterobius vermicularis (pinworm)** | Most common helminth in U.S. children. Perianal itching, "scotch tape test." |
| **Trichuris trichiura (whipworm)** | Co-infects with Ascaris. Heavy load → rectal prolapse in children. |
| **Hookworm (Necator americanus, Ancylostoma duodenale)** | Skin penetration → pulmonary migration → small bowel. Iron deficiency anemia hallmark. |
| **Strongyloides stercoralis** | Auto-infection cycle → lifelong infection. Hyperinfection syndrome with steroids. Ground itch, larva currens (serpiginous urticaria). Always test before immunosuppression. |
| **Trichinella spiralis** | Undercooked pork/wild game. Periorbital edema, myositis, eosinophilia. |
| **Toxocara canis/cati (visceral & ocular larva migrans)** | Dog/cat roundworm; children eating soil. Can mimic asthma, retinoblastoma. |
| **Filariae (Wuchereria, Brugia, Loa loa, Onchocerca)** | Lymphatic filariasis (elephantiasis), eye worm, river blindness. |
| **Anisakis** | Raw fish/sushi. Acute gastric pain, allergic anisakiasis. |
| **Dracunculus medinensis (guinea worm)** | Near eradication. |

#### Cestodes (tapeworms)

| Organism | Notes |
|----------|-------|
| **Taenia saginata (beef)** | Up to 10 m. Often asymptomatic. Proglottids in stool. |
| **Taenia solium (pork) → cysticercosis** | Larval form invades CNS (neurocysticercosis), most common cause of acquired epilepsy in endemic areas. |
| **Diphyllobothrium latum** | Raw freshwater fish. B12 deficiency. |
| **Hymenolepis nana (dwarf tapeworm)** | Most common tapeworm in U.S. Auto-infection cycle. |
| **Echinococcus granulosus / multilocularis** | Hydatid cysts in liver, lung; alveolar form is malignant-like. Hand washing after dog contact. |

#### Trematodes (flukes)

| Organism | Notes |
|----------|-------|
| **Schistosoma haematobium / mansoni / japonicum** | Freshwater snail vector. Cercarial dermatitis ("swimmer's itch"), bladder cancer (haematobium), hepatic fibrosis (mansoni/japonicum). |
| **Clonorchis sinensis / Opisthorchis** | Raw fish. Cholangiocarcinoma risk. |
| **Fasciola hepatica / gigantica** | Watercress, raw greens. Liver, biliary tree. |
| **Paragonimus westermani** | Raw crab. Lung; mimics TB. |

### 2.3 Ectoparasites (referenced for completeness)

- **Sarcoptes scabiei** (scabies)
- **Pediculus humanus capitis / corporis / Pthirus pubis** (lice)
- **Demodex folliculorum / brevis** (rosacea, blepharitis)

## 3. Life-Cycle Patterns Relevant to Treatment Timing

### 3.1 Egg-larva-adult cycles (most helminths)
- Antiparasitic herbs and drugs are most active against adult and larval forms; eggs are often resistant.
- Repeat dosing across 2–3 reproductive cycles is standard. Lunar phase dosing (3 days before, during, after full moon) is a functional medicine convention based on observation that adult worms become more active in the gut lumen during full-moon phase.

### 3.2 Auto-infection (Strongyloides, Hymenolepis, Enterobius)
- Re-establishes infection internally without re-exposure → requires extended protocols and often pharmaceutical adjuncts (ivermectin for Strongyloides).

### 3.3 Tissue migration (Ascaris, hookworm, Strongyloides, Toxocara, Trichinella)
- Eosinophilia spikes during migration. Steroids alone can mask while worsening (Strongyloides hyperinfection).

### 3.4 Cyst dormancy (Toxoplasma, Echinococcus, Taenia solium cysticerci, Giardia cysts)
- Encysted forms resist most agents. Treatment may control rather than eradicate (toxoplasmosis cysts).

### 3.5 RBC cycles (Babesia, Plasmodium)
- Synchronized parasitemia → fevers cycle. Hemolysis monitoring (haptoglobin, LDH, retic) is required.

## 4. Prevalence in U.S. and Industrialized Cohorts

- **CDC** classifies five "Neglected Parasitic Infections" prioritized for U.S. action: Chagas, neurocysticercosis, toxocariasis, toxoplasmosis, trichomoniasis.
- **Pinworm**: ~40 million U.S. cases at any time, mostly children.
- **Toxoplasma seroprevalence**: 11% U.S. adults (NHANES); >30% in some immigrant cohorts.
- **Blastocystis**: 23% in U.S. stool surveys with PCR; >50% in some rural/agricultural cohorts.
- **Giardia**: ~1.2 million U.S. cases/yr estimated.
- **Strongyloides**: prevalent in Appalachia, Gulf states, and immigrants from endemic regions; underdiagnosed.
- **Chagas**: ~300,000 U.S. residents infected; <1% diagnosed.
- **Functional medicine surveys** (CellCore, Microbe Formulas practitioner data) consistently find 60–80% of chronic-illness patients positive on multi-target stool PCR for at least one parasite.

## 5. Transmission Routes

| Route | Examples |
|-------|----------|
| Fecal-oral (water) | Giardia, Cryptosporidium, Cyclospora, Entamoeba |
| Fecal-oral (food) | Ascaris, Trichuris, Hymenolepis, Toxoplasma (oocysts) |
| Undercooked meat/fish | Trichinella, Taenia, Diphyllobothrium, Anisakis, Toxoplasma (tissue cysts), Clonorchis |
| Soil-transmitted (skin) | Hookworm, Strongyloides |
| Vector-borne | Plasmodium, Babesia (tick), Trypanosoma (kissing bug, tsetse), Leishmania (sandfly), Filariae (mosquito, blackfly) |
| Sexual | Trichomonas, Entamoeba (oral-anal), Giardia |
| Animal contact | Toxocara, Echinococcus, Toxoplasma, Cryptosporidium, scabies |
| Auto-infection | Strongyloides, Enterobius, Hymenolepis, Cysticercosis |
| Transplacental | Toxoplasma, Trypanosoma cruzi, Plasmodium |

## 6. Risk Factors That Should Trigger Workup

- Travel to endemic areas (within 5 years for tropical agents; lifetime for Strongyloides, Chagas)
- Immigrant from endemic region (lifetime screening once)
- Untreated well water, recreational freshwater exposure
- Pet ownership, especially cats (Toxoplasma) and dogs (Toxocara, Echinococcus)
- Daycare, institutional living
- Raw/rare meat, sushi, ceviche, raw fish, raw watercress, undercooked pork
- Walking barefoot on contaminated soil
- Chronic GI symptoms refractory to standard care
- Eosinophilia >500 unexplained
- Immunosuppression about to start (always rule out Strongyloides first)
- Tick exposure with Lyme symptoms (consider Babesia)

## 7. Functional Medicine Lens: Why Parasites Persist in "Healthy" Hosts

1. **Compromised stomach acid** (PPI use, atrophic gastritis, H. pylori) → cyst/ova survive gastric passage.
2. **Bile insufficiency** → poor mucosal antimicrobial defense.
3. **Low secretory IgA** (chronic stress, gluten reactivity, mold exposure).
4. **Microbiome dysbiosis** → loss of competitive exclusion; parasites thrive when commensals are depleted.
5. **Heavy metal and mycotoxin burden** → biofilms shelter parasites; metals inactivate immune cells.
6. **Genetic detox SNPs** (GSTM1 null, MTHFR, PEMT) → poor toxin clearance during die-off.
7. **Vagal tone and motility issues** (SIBO, gastroparesis) → stagnation favors colonization.

These are the leverage points addressed in Phase 1 (preparation) before eradication begins.
