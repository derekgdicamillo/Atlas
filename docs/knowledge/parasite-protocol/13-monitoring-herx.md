# 13 — Monitoring, Herxheimer, and Retreatment

## 1. The Herxheimer Reaction (Jarisch-Herxheimer)

Originally described in syphilis treatment, "Herx" refers to a transient worsening of symptoms triggered by rapid pathogen die-off and release of endotoxin (LPS), pro-inflammatory cytokines, and metabolic byproducts. In parasite protocols, expect:

- Fatigue, malaise
- Headache, brain fog
- Joint and muscle aches, flu-like
- GI upset, loose stools, bloating
- Skin flares, itching, urticaria
- Mood — anxiety, irritability, depression
- Sleep disruption
- Sweats, low-grade fever
- Old symptoms briefly returning ("retracing")

### Severity scale (use daily during Phase 2)

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0 | No reaction |
| 1 | Mild — slight fatigue, headache, but functional |
| 2 | Moderate — interferes with normal day, manageable with self-care |
| 3 | Severe — bedbound or near, GI/skin marked, mental load high |
| 4 | Dangerous — fever >101.5, vomiting, neurologic symptoms, dehydration |

Target operating zone: 1–2 daily. If 3 sustained for 48 hr, halve dose. If 4, stop and reassess.

## 2. Herx Management Toolkit

### First-line (always available)
- Increase water intake to 35 mL/kg minimum
- Electrolytes 1–3 servings
- Binders — increase frequency to 3–4x/day during heavy Herx
- Magnesium 400–800 mg at bedtime
- Vitamin C 2–4 g divided
- Liposomal glutathione 500–1000 mg
- Epsom salt bath (2 cups) 30 min nightly
- Sauna 20 min if tolerated; otherwise rest
- Coffee enema 1x (if patient familiar)
- Sleep, sleep, sleep

### Second-line
- Activated charcoal 1–2 g extra dose
- Bentonite slurry 1 tbsp in water
- TUDCA 500 mg
- NAC 1200 mg
- Anti-inflammatory diet strict
- Reduce kill dose 50% temporarily

### Severe Herx escalation
- Stop kills 24–48 hr
- High-dose binders
- IV vitamin C if available (10–25 g)
- Methylene blue 0.5 mg/kg if specialist available
- Steroid burst only if anaphylactoid presentation under physician
- Consider whether actual adverse drug reaction vs Herx (rule out SJS, DRESS, angioedema)

## 3. Differentiating Herx from Adverse Reaction

| Feature | Herx | Adverse drug/herb reaction |
|---------|------|---------------------------|
| Onset | Within hours after dose, often after first 1–3 doses | Anytime, often after multiple doses (DRESS 2–8 wks) |
| Symptom pattern | Familiar to baseline disease + flu-like | New, often skin/mucosa, eosinophilia, LFT spike |
| Improves with binders/hydration | Yes | No or worse |
| Resolves with brief drug pause | Yes, within 48–72 hr | Variable, may persist or progress |
| Skin | Itching, hives, eczema flare | New rash, mucosal involvement, sloughing |
| Labs | Mild WBC shift, mild LFT possible | Marked LFT, eosinophilia >2000, cytopenias |

When in doubt: stop drug, contact prescriber.

## 4. Lab Monitoring Schedule

| Timepoint | Test | Why |
|-----------|------|-----|
| **Baseline** | CBC, CMP, ferritin, IgE, eosinophils, vitamin D, stool PCR/O&P, targeted serologies | Establish floor |
| **Week 2** (Phase 1) | Symptoms only | Drainage tolerance check |
| **Week 4** (start Phase 2) | Symptoms; LFT if pharmaceuticals | Baseline kills |
| **Week 6** | CBC, LFT, CMP if Rx or extended wormwood/neem/berberine | Early hepatic check |
| **Week 8–10** | Symptom diary; LFT/CBC if extended | Mid-protocol |
| **Week 12** | CBC, CMP, LFT, ferritin, IgE, eosinophils, repeat stool PCR/O&P | End of eradication assessment |
| **Week 16** | Stool re-test if Week 12 still positive | Confirm clearance |
| **3 months post** | Stool PCR; symptom reassessment; SIBO breath if GI persists | Durability |
| **6 months post** | Optional retest if relapse-prone or symptomatic | Long-term |
| **12 months post** | Strongyloides IgG (if positive at baseline) — should decline | Confirm cure |

### Weekly review checkpoints
- Did patient maintain bowel movements daily?
- Did Herx score stay in 1–2 zone?
- Are symptoms trending down or up?
- Any new concerning symptoms?
- Are binders timed correctly?
- Any missed doses or compliance issues?

## 6. Retreatment Criteria

### Indications for second course
- Symptoms persisting past Week 12 with confirmed positive stool
- Recurrence within 3 months
- Family member newly positive
- Re-exposure (travel, water, food)
- Pet positive
- Strongyloides serology not declining at 6 months

### Second-course adjustments
- Switch herb stack or add pharmaceutical pulse
- Address co-infection that was missed (mold, H. pylori, SIBO)
- Address microbiome — was Phase 4 adequate?
- Address terrain — was prep deep enough?
- Address adherence honestly — was the patient compliant?
- Consider less common organisms (Dientamoeba, microsporidia, atypical Babesia)

## 7. Markers of Successful Treatment

### Symptomatic
- Resolution or major reduction of presenting symptoms
- Bowel function normalized
- Sleep restored
- Energy restored
- Skin clear
- Mood stable

### Laboratory
- Eosinophils normalized (<500)
- IgE trending toward normal (atopic baseline considered)
- Ferritin replete (>50 ng/mL ideal for women, >75 men)
- LFTs normal
- Stool PCR negative twice
- Strongyloides IgG declining (50%+ at 6 months, undetectable at 12)

### Functional
- HRV improved
- Exercise capacity recovered
- No food reactivity flare
- Stable weight
- Hormonal cycles regular (women)
- Cognitive testing improved (if baseline obtained)

## 8. When to Refer Out

- Tier 4 patients (immunocompromised, neuro/ocular involvement, hemolysis, severe eosinophilia)
- Confirmed neurocysticercosis, hydatid, schistosomiasis with tissue damage
- Pregnancy with confirmed parasitic infection
- Pediatric severe cases
- Failure of two functional courses with stool persistence
- LFT >3x ULN sustained
- Bone marrow suppression
- Suspected drug-induced reaction (SJS, DRESS, angioedema)
- Cardiac symptoms during Babesia/Chagas treatment
- Suicidal ideation during methylene blue or other neuro-active treatment
