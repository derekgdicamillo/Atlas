# Lab Optimization — Interpretation Workflow

How Jenni Gallagher, APRN (Joyful Heart Institute) orders, preps, sequences, and interprets a functional lab panel, as taught in the Spring 2026 mentorship Week 1 session. Pair with [optimal-vs-normal-ranges.md](optimal-vs-normal-ranges.md) for the specific target numbers referenced below.

## 1. The Standard Panel Structure ("Lab Worksheet")

The course uses a standardized tracking worksheet ("lab worksheet.pdf" / "LAB WORKSHEET.pdf") for every patient, recorded at each visit with a date. The worksheet defines the panel Derek should default to when a patient's labs are being tracked longitudinally in a functional-medicine context. Fields, in the order they appear on the worksheet:

- **Thyroid:** TSH, thyroid antibodies (AB), Free T4 (FT4), Free T3 (FT3), Reverse T3 (RT3)
- **Inflammation:** Homocysteine, CRP, Uric Acid, WBC
- **Iron status:** Ferritin, Iron / iron saturation (%)
- **B vitamins:** B12
- **Electrolytes:** Na/K
- **Protein:** P (total protein), A (albumin), G (globulin)
- **Liver:** Alk Phos, AST/ALT
- **Vitamin D**
- **Glucose-insulin:** Insulin, A1c/glucose
- **Lipids:** Triglycerides, Trig/HDL ratio, HDL
- **Sex hormones:** SHBG, Estrogen (Estr), DHEA-S, Testosterone (Testost), Progesterone (Prog), FSH
- **Notes** field for clinical impression

This worksheet ordering (thyroid → inflammation → iron → electrolytes/protein/liver → glucose/insulin → lipids → hormones) reflects the sequence in which the course teaches practitioners to review a panel, moving from the systems most likely to explain global fatigue/energy complaints (thyroid, iron) toward inflammation, metabolic, and hormonal contributors.

## 2. Pre-Draw Preparation — Rules That Change Results

The course is explicit that lab values cannot be interpreted against optimal targets unless draw conditions are standardized. Patient instructions taught in this course:

- **Biotin / B12:** hold 5–7 days before the blood draw (biotin interferes with ~150 different lab assays via streptavidin-biotin immunoassay methods).
- **Fasting:** 10–12 hours is the target fasting window; 8 hours is the bare minimum.
- **Morning of the draw:** no coffee or juice; drink a modest amount of water (less than 16 oz) on the way to the lab. Adequate hydration prevents sodium/potassium from appearing falsely elevated ("too dry") on the electrolyte panel.
- **Thyroid medication:** stop all thyroid doses approximately 18–20 hours before the blood draw for the most accurate TSH/FT3/FT4/RT3 values.
- **Other hormones (estrogen/progesterone/testosterone):** stop 2–3 days before the draw if the patient can tolerate doing so.
- **Iron supplementation:** stop 4 days prior to the draw.
- **Other vitamins/supplements generally:** stop approximately 2–3 days before the draw.
- **Vitamin D:** stop approximately 10–14 days before the draw — vitamin D has a long half-life, so a recent dose skews the result upward. The course specifies this hold is only necessary when the goal is *confirming a true high level*; it is not required for a routine/baseline draw.

Patients are instructed to print and carry a copy of their lab orders to the draw, and to verify the in-system orders match the paper order, since electronic orders can be lost. If using LabCorp, insurance information must be confirmed directly with the lab (LabCorp techs will not proactively ask), or the draw defaults to cash-pay billing.

## 3. Order of Interpretation

The course's teaching sequence for reading a completed panel:

1. **Thyroid first pass.** Do not stop at TSH and/or Free T4 alone — neither molecule is capable of entering the cell for mitochondrial ATP production. Free T3 ("gas pedal") and Reverse T3 ("brake pedal") are the values that actually explain a hypothyroid symptom picture; a patient can have a "normal" TSH/FT4 and still be functionally hypothyroid if FT3 is low or RT3 is elevated.
2. **Iron/ferritin.** Ferritin below the ~65 ng/mL symptom threshold is treated as a likely contributor to fatigue regardless of where TSH/FT4 fall, because ferritin governs how effectively hemoglobin can transport oxygen. Because standard reference ranges permit ferritin as low as 10–12 ng/mL to read WNL, this is one of the most common places a symptomatic patient is told their labs are "normal."
3. **Inflammation markers.** hs-CRP, WBC, homocysteine, uric acid, and ferritin (as an acute-phase reactant) are reviewed together. A ferritin value running 1.5–2x higher than the iron number is read as an inflammation signal rather than a true iron-storage picture, and should prompt looking at the inflammation panel before treating ferritin as "high iron stores."
4. **Glucose-insulin axis, treated as a probable root cause.** Insulin is evaluated first among the metabolic panel because elevated insulin (above the ~5 mcU/mL optimal ceiling) is taught as a common upstream driver of both inflammation (elevated CRP) and hormone imbalance. HbA1c and fasting glucose are interpreted against the tighter optimal bands (A1c <5.3%, FBS ~80s) rather than the mainstream diabetes-diagnostic thresholds, since the course teaches that diabetic-complication risk begins well below the diagnostic cutoff for diabetes itself. Insulin should never be read in isolation — cross-check against A1c, FBS, triglycerides, thyroid panel, DHEA-S, estradiol, and testosterone before concluding a patient is or is not insulin resistant.
5. **Liver/protein panel** as a nutritional-status check. Low AST/ALT/Alk Phos points toward malnutrition (cross-check protein, vitamin D, iron, iodine); high values point toward liver stress (alcohol, medications, NAFLD). Albumin and globulin are interpreted together (both derive from total protein) — low or high readings in either direction are non-ideal and both point back to malnutrition or malabsorption rather than a "the higher/lower the better" read.
6. **Electrolytes and RBC minerals.** Sodium and potassium are read against the upper end of the standard range (Na >140 mEq/L, K >4.0 mEq/L) rather than mid-range. Magnesium, chromium, and selenium should be ordered as RBC (intracellular) levels rather than plasma/serum, because RBC levels are more stable and better reflect tissue status for targeted supplementation decisions.
7. **Vitamin D**, checked against the 60 ng/mL functional floor (versus a 30 ng/mL mainstream floor), always alongside its cofactors (magnesium, K2, boron, dietary fat) — a low functional vitamin D on adequate supplementation should prompt a cofactor/absorption review, not just a dose increase.
8. **Sex hormones last**, in the context of everything above — the course explicitly frames most hormone imbalances as downstream of insulin dysregulation, so hormone panel abnormalities are interpreted in light of the glucose-insulin findings rather than treated as a first-line, independent problem.

## 4. Patterns and Red Flags to Actively Flag

- **"Normal" that isn't optimal:** any value inside the standard reference range but outside the optimal/functional band (see [optimal-vs-normal-ranges.md](optimal-vs-normal-ranges.md)) should be flagged to the clinician even though the lab report itself will not flag it.
- **Ferritin:iron ratio >1.5–2x** — read as inflammation/acute-phase-reactant pattern, not as a true iron-overload picture.
- **Insulin >5 mcU/mL**, even without frank fasting hyperglycemia or an elevated A1c — treated as an early/root-cause finding, not a value to defer until glucose or A1c also become abnormal. Per the Kraft insulin-response-pattern framework cited in this course, the majority of patients with "normal" glucose tolerance testing can still show a hyperinsulinemic response pattern consistent with occult diabetes.
- **TSH/FT4 normal but symptomatic** — do not close the thyroid workup without FT3 and RT3; a high RT3 ("brake") can produce a hypothyroid symptom picture despite normal TSH/FT4/FT3 lab-flag status.
- **Discordant post-prandial glucose** — a post-meal glucose that rises more than ~10–12 points above pre-meal glucose is flagged even if the absolute post-prandial number is within the standard range.
- **Low AST/ALT/Alk Phos** — do not dismiss as benign; treat as a malnutrition signal and cross-check protein, vitamin D, iron, and iodine status.
- **Vitamin D confirmatory retesting** — if a result reads unexpectedly high, confirm with a 10–14 day supplement hold before retesting, since recent dosing (given vitamin D's long half-life) can produce a falsely elevated result.

## 5. Tools Referenced for Ongoing Monitoring

- **Continuous glucose monitor (CGM):** recommended broadly ("a great tool for anyone"), not reserved for diagnosed diabetics/pre-diabetics. OTC options named: Stelo and "Theia" [spelling as given in source].
- **Serial worksheet tracking:** the lab worksheet is designed for repeat use over time (a "Dates" field heads each patient block), supporting trend-based interpretation rather than single-draw snapshots.

## Clinical Disclaimer

This workflow reflects one functional-medicine practitioner's taught convention for a single mentorship session (Week 1, Spring 2026) and is not a substitute for standard-of-care diagnostic criteria (e.g., ADA diabetes diagnostic thresholds, AACE/ATA thyroid guidelines). Numeric "optimal" targets in this course are largely presented as clinical convention rather than RCT-validated treatment targets — see the Evidence-Quality Summary in [optimal-vs-normal-ranges.md](optimal-vs-normal-ranges.md) before treating any single number as a guideline-level target. Always correlate lab findings with clinical presentation and standard diagnostic criteria before treatment decisions.
