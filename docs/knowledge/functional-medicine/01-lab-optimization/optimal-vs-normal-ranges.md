# Lab Optimization — Optimal vs. Normal Reference Ranges

Full lab-by-lab table of standard/mainstream reference ranges versus the "optimal"/functional targets taught in the Joyful Heart Institute Spring 2026 mentorship, Week 1 (Jenni Gallagher, APRN). Numbers are transcribed verbatim from three source artifacts: the "OPTIMAL LAB RESULTS.jpg" quick-reference card, the "Functional vs Normal Lab Results.pdf" slide deck, and the Week 1 course lesson notes. Where sources disagree on a specific number, both are given and the discrepancy is flagged rather than resolved by guess.

## How to Read This Table

- **Standard/Mainstream Range** — the conventional lab-report reference range or conventional clinical target, as stated in the source.
- **Optimal/Functional Target** — the narrower functional-medicine target taught in this course.
- **Source** — which artifact the number comes from (jpg = OPTIMAL LAB RESULTS.jpg; PPT = Functional vs Normal Lab Results.pdf; Notes = Week 1 course lesson notes).
- **Evidence label** — RCT/cohort citation where the course explicitly cited one; otherwise "functional-medicine consensus" (practitioner convention taught in this course, not independently verified against primary literature here).

## Iron / Ferritin

| Lab | Standard/Mainstream Range | Optimal/Functional Target | Source | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ferritin | Mainstream reference range allows values as low as 10–12 ng/mL to read as WNL | jpg: 80–100 ng/mL. PPT slide: iron and ferritin should both be 80–120 and close together in value. Symptom threshold: ferritin <65-ish begins symptoms for most patients | jpg, PPT, Notes | Functional-medicine consensus |
| Iron saturation (%) | — | Around 40% | jpg | Functional-medicine consensus |

Notes on iron/ferritin ordering: the course teaches that mainstream practice typically orders only a CBC, relying on hemoglobin/hematocrit as proxies; hemoglobin uses ferritin-derived iron to transport O2, and hematocrit reflects the percentage of RBCs delivering oxygen. Ordering iron and ferritin directly gives a clearer picture of iron utilization vs. storage than CBC indices alone.

## Liver Panel / Protein

| Lab | Standard/Mainstream Range | Optimal/Functional Target | Source | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Alkaline phosphatase (Alk Phos) | — | Around 60 IU/L | jpg, PPT | Functional-medicine consensus |
| AST | — | Around 20 IU/L | jpg, PPT | Functional-medicine consensus |
| ALT | — | Around 20 IU/L | jpg, PPT | Functional-medicine consensus |
| Total protein | — | 7–7.4 g/dL | jpg | Functional-medicine consensus |
| Albumin | — | 4.1–4.4 g/dL (mid-range) | jpg, PPT | Functional-medicine consensus. Albumin manages water balance inside/outside the cell membrane; low or high alike are non-ideal and suggest malnutrition or poor absorption |
| Globulin | — | PPT slide: ~3.0 g/dL. Week 1 notes: 2.9–3.2 g/dL. (Globulin = Total Protein − Albumin) | PPT, Notes | Functional-medicine consensus. Globulin is the "building block" protein — becomes immunoglobulins, muscle cells, bone cells, brain cells. Low globulin suggests poor protein intake/absorption; high globulin suggests muscle breakdown to source amino acids |

Interpretation convention for AST/ALT/Alk Phos: low values suggest malnutrition (evaluate protein, vitamin D, iron, iodine status); high values suggest liver stress (alcohol, medications, NAFLD).

## Vitamin D

| Lab | Standard/Mainstream Range | Optimal/Functional Target | Source | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 25-OH Vitamin D | 30–100 ng/mL (mainstream reference range) | jpg: >60 ng/mL. PPT slide: 60–100 ng/mL. Week 1 course notes: 60–80 ng/mL — levels <60 ng/mL are linked to higher morbidity and mortality. [Note: source materials give three slightly different upper bounds for the optimal range (100, 100, and 80 ng/mL, respectively) — treat 60 ng/mL as the firm functional floor and 80–100 ng/mL as the taught ceiling band.] Toxicity threshold: 200 ng/mL | jpg, PPT, Notes | PPT cites Garland et al. (2014), *Am J Public Health* 104(8):e43–e50 — meta-analysis of all-cause mortality by serum 25-OH-D (a disease-incidence-prevention-by-serum-level chart from this literature is reproduced in the deck, attributed to Garland CF & Baggerly CA, suggesting a 40–60 ng/mL range to prevent the majority of diseases across cancers, fractures, MS, heart attack, and other outcomes) |

Vitamin D cofactors required for utilization (cannot work in isolation): magnesium, vitamin K2, boron, and dietary fat/fatty food at time of dosing.

## Evidence-Quality Summary

Primary literature explicitly cited in the source PPT (cohort/RCT-derived, not course-only consensus):

- Ridker PM, MacFadyen J, Libby P, Glynn RJ (2010). Relation of baseline hs-CRP to cardiovascular outcomes with rosuvastatin (JUPITER). *Am J Cardiol* 106(2):204–209.
- Butalia S, et al. (2024). Association between HbA1c and development of cardiovascular disease in adults without diabetes at baseline. *J Am Heart Assoc* 13:e031095.
- Garland CF, et al. (2014). Meta-analysis of all-cause mortality according to serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D. *Am J Public Health* 104(8):e43–e50.
- Leiba A, Vinker S, Dinour D, Holtzman EJ, Shani M (2015). Uric acid levels within the normal range predict increased risk of hypertension: a cohort study. *J Am Soc Hypertens* 9(8):600–609.

All other numeric targets in this file (fasting insulin ≤5, ferritin 80–100/80–120, Free T3 3.5–4, Reverse T3 10–12, electrolyte targets, liver enzyme targets, hormone mid-range targets, etc.) are taught as functional-medicine practitioner consensus/convention in this course and are not attributed to a specific cited study in the source materials — apply as clinical convention, not as guideline-level evidence.

Popular-press books cited in the PPT reference list (context/patient-education sources, not primary literature): DiNicolantonio (*The Salt Fix*), Davis (*Wheat Belly*), Cummins & Gerber (*Eat Rich, Live Long*), Perlmutter (*Drop Acid*), Wentz (*Hashimoto's Thyroiditis*), Berry (*Lies My Doctor Told Me*), Robinson (*Recovering with T3*), Volek & Phinney (*The Art and Science of Low Carbohydrate Living*), Moore & Westman (*Cholesterol Clarity*), Hussey (*Understanding the Heart*), Teicholz (*The Big Fat Surprise*), Amen (*Change Your Diet, Change Your Mind*), Kendrick (*The Great Cholesterol Con*).
