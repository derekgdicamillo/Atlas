## Key Takeaways

- **Chronic constipation is the root of most GI complaints** — reflux, bloating, diarrhea, hemorrhoids, and even "IBS" frequently trace back to sluggish gut motility and retained stool. 80–90% of reflux patients also have constipation on screening [source: course lesson notes, Week 7].
- **The gut is the foundation of the whole treatment plan.** Per the protocol author: "Until you empty the intestines completely and start HEALING the lining & muscles of your gut, you will not start to heal from the inside out. All nutrient absorption is based on having a HEALTHY digestive system."
- **Diarrhea does not rule out constipation** — it is one of its most common presentations when hard, impacted stool allows only liquid stool to pass around it.
- **The protocol is modular.** Average patients without major GI disease (no Crohn's, no severe IBS) typically only need Steps 1–3 (empty the gut, GI Synergy, probiotic). The toxin binder and leaky-gut repair steps (BioFilm, L-glutamine) are reserved for patients with suspected toxin burden (mold, heavy metals, chemical exposure) or documented/suspected intestinal permeability.
- **Grains are treated as the primary dietary driver of gut damage** in this framework — fermentation, bloating, diverticulosis, and blocked absorption of iron, T3, zinc, and other nutrients are attributed to grain intake, and grain elimination is the first dietary instruction given at protocol start.
- **Salt, butter, and broth are treated as therapeutic, not just dietary** — minimum 2 tsp salt/day and ~½ stick (≈50 g fat) of real butter/day are stated targets, alongside daily bone broth/collagen for membrane repair.
- **Full protocol duration runs ~5–6 months for the average patient**, up to 6–12 months (course notes: "9–12 month plan") for patients with a long history of severe digestive disease (e.g., IBS, Crohn's, severe leaky gut, SIBO).

## When to Use

Use this topic when:
- Building or reviewing a patient's foundational gut-healing plan before starting other functional protocols (weight loss, hormone, thyroid, detox) — this protocol is explicitly framed by the author as the *prerequisite* foundation for other treatment plans.
- A patient reports any combination of: chronic constipation (even subtle/unrecognized), alternating hard/watery stool, reflux/heartburn/indigestion, bloating, diverticulosis, unexplained fatigue, or a history of long-term PPI/antibiotic use.
- Evaluating for SIBO, Candida overgrowth, H. pylori, or general dysbiosis and needing dosing/treatment-option reference.
- A patient has had a recent "gut insult" (antibiotics, anesthesia, GI illness, COVID/flu) and needs a restoration plan.
- Cross-reference [docs/knowledge/parasite-protocol/](../../parasite-protocol/README.md) when parasites are suspected as a contributor to dysbiosis — that system covers parasite-specific workup and eradication in depth; this topic covers the general gut-emptying/repopulation/repair framework that follows or runs alongside it.

## Clinical Disclaimers

1. This protocol is reproduced from a specific practitioner's (Jenni Gallagher, APRN) patient-facing educational handout and mentorship teaching. It is **expert-opinion / clinical-experience-based**, not derived from RCTs — see evidence-quality notes in [healing-protocol.md](healing-protocol.md). Use clinical judgment before applying verbatim.
2. Laxative use (Step 1) is contraindicated or requires caution in patients with kidney injury/damage — source material explicitly flags that laxatives can worsen kidney damage and recommends senna, coconut oil, and/or salt as alternatives in that population.
3. Binder use (Universal Toxin Binder) must be separated from food, medications, and other supplements by 1–2 hours to avoid impairing absorption.
4. This is not a substitute for standard workup of alarm features (GI bleeding, unintended weight loss, family history of colon cancer, anemia) — the source material itself notes that persistent or concerning symptoms warrant further testing (OAT, mycotoxin panel) or referral (e.g., celiac disease → gastroenterology referral).
5. Consult the patient's full medication list before starting — the source disclaimer notes "some medications may interact with some of the ingredients listed here."
