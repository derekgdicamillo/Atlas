## Vitamin C — new mechanistic/safety detail not in KB

- **Liposomal vitamin C is NOT interchangeable with standard ascorbic acid for bowel-tolerance dosing.** Liposomal C bypasses stomach breakdown and is absorbed in the small intestine, so it does **not** produce the loose-stool/laxative response used clinically to titrate a patient to bowel tolerance. Standard (non-liposomal) high-dose ascorbic acid is what's used for the gut-clearing/constipation-relief protocol. Liposomal C is reserved for patients who get diarrhea from *any* amount of vitamin C due to stomach hyperstimulation — it avoids stomach-level triggering of loose stool. [17:37–18:30]
- Mechanism metaphor given for why to dose vitamin C **throughout the day** rather than once: "vitamin C works kind of like the trash guy — it picks up reactive oxidative molecules floating around and takes them out through elimination, usually through urine." Water-soluble, used up rapidly; patients with high inflammatory markers benefit from **several smaller doses spread through the day** rather than one bolus. [19:19–19:47]
- **Vitamin C as a fast laxative option**: 10,000–15,000 mg produces a bowel response "in just a little while" — contrast with magnesium (milk of magnesia/mag citrate), which the speaker states works in **about 4–6 hours**, i.e., slower onset but not slow-acting. [77:01–77:24] ⚠️ (spoken timing figures, verify against slide.) (not found in slides — remains unverified)

## Digestive enzymes — entire topic missing from current KB

The KB mentions digestive enzymes only in passing (Candida/H. pylori/celiac/parasite contexts). This is a dedicated segment not otherwise captured:
- Speaker is **"not a huge fan [of digestive enzymes] except in a couple of cases."** Many patients wrongly believe once started, enzymes must be taken forever. [62:41–63:02]
- **The only patients who typically need them long-term: patients without a gallbladder.** Mechanism explained: the gallbladder does **not produce** digestive enzymes — it only **stores/holds them "at the ready"** so they release in a burst when chyme moves into the small intestine. The **liver** produces all digestive enzymes (amylase, lipase, protease); amylase release starts mostly in the mouth. After cholecystectomy, the liver still produces the enzymes, they're just no longer stored/released on that same trigger — some patients can retrain this response over time, others (gallbladder-dumping-syndrome patients) cannot. [63:04–64:17]
- **Dosing timing: take digestive enzymes ~15 minutes before eating** — acknowledged as a practical challenge since patients often don't know exactly when they'll eat. [64:27–64:47]
- Gallbladder-removed patients also **can't tolerate large fat intake right away** — increase fat intake **slowly** for these patients rather than all at once. [69:52–70:03] (Not currently addressed in the KB's butter/fat-intake guidance, which is written as if universal.)
