# Iron & Ferritin — Treatment Protocol

Supplementation, diet, cofactors, IV infusion protocol, and follow-up timing for correcting low iron/ferritin, as taught in the Spring 2026 mentorship Week 2 master class (Jenni Gallagher APRN, Joyful Heart Health Care). See [pathophysiology.md](pathophysiology.md) for the symptom staging and lab interpretation that drive these decisions.

## 1. First-Line Oral Supplementation

**Ferosolv (ferrous bisglycinate)** — taught as the preferred form because it is "MUCH easier for most" patients to tolerate than traditional iron salts, which are poorly absorbed and cause GI issues. (Note: the deck also refers to this product family generically as "Ferrochel"/"Ferrosolve"/"Ferrosolb" across slides and course notes — these appear to be the same ferrous bisglycinate product referenced under slightly different spellings in the source material; treat as one product.)

- **Dosing: 45 mg BID** (course notes' primary stated regimen)
- Deck alternative framing: **36 mg TID or 45 mg BID**
- Ferrosolve capsules are dosed at **45 mg per cap**
- **Caution**: many commercial ferrous bisglycinate products are dosed as low as **9 mg** per capsule — "BE AWARE" when substituting products, since a 9 mg product is not dose-equivalent
- **Hard ceiling: do NOT exceed 45 mg per dose** — this is a specific nephrotoxicity ceiling, not a general suggestion
- Iron is characterized in the course as a **heavy metal**; toxicity can occur. Patients should be counseled to **never take iron long-term without lab work** to confirm ongoing need

### De-escalation and maintenance dosing

- Once labs are improving and ferritin is around **65–70**, iron can be discontinued **if** the patient is consuming adequate red meat
- Alternative maintenance strategy: some patients take iron only on days they do *not* eat red meat, rather than daily dosing
- Long-term goal is to maintain optimal levels on **food alone** (see §5, Follow-Up)

## 2. Vitamin C — Absorption Cofactor and Constipation Counter-Measure

- **Dose: 2,000–4,000 mg (2–4 g) TID to QID**, "as tolerated by gut"
- **Titrate slowly** — start low and increase until reaching bowel tolerance, then back off; vitamin C has a laxative effect at high doses, and this effect is used deliberately to counteract the constipating effect of iron supplementation
- Rationale given in course notes: high-dose vitamin C counteracts iron-induced constipation *and* reduces systemic inflammation
- **Liposomal vitamin C is roughly double the potency of regular vitamin C** by label dose — e.g., a 1,600 mg liposomal C product is approximately equivalent to 4,000 mg of regular vitamin C. Account for this when converting between product types.
- In a severe-constipation case (ferritin 18, history of diverticulitis/sigmoid resection), vitamin C was titrated as high as **~10–12 g/day** specifically to treat constipation and aid iron absorption — an example of titrating well above the 2–4 g TID/QID starting range when clinically needed. [See case-studies.md, Case 4.]

## 3. Vitamin D and Its Cofactors

- Vitamin D is iron's cofactor because **it lowers hepcidin**, the hormone that blocks iron absorption (see [pathophysiology.md](pathophysiology.md) §2) — this is presented as the single most important reason to correct vitamin D status alongside iron.
- Prescribe vitamin D **with its own cofactors**: magnesium, vitamin K2 (explicitly **not** potassium — a stated point of potential confusion), boron, and dietary fat.
- **Never take vitamin D on an empty stomach** — poor absorption results.
- Preferred product referenced in the deck: **Osteo PX** (contains vitamin A, D, K2, magnesium, and boron in one product).
- Take vitamin D and its cofactors **with a fatty meal** — D and K are fat-soluble vitamins and require dietary fat for absorption.
- Course reference elsewhere in the broader curriculum gives a general functional-optimal vitamin D range of **60–80 ng/mL** (Week 1 lab-optimization material) — useful context, though not iron-specific dosing.

## 4. Diet, Trace Minerals, and Foods to Avoid

- **Red meat**: encourage **5+ servings/week**. Taught as the best dietary source of iron, B12, and trace minerals.
- **Grain elimination**: grains break down into **phytates**, which block absorption of iron, zinc, and T3. Grain elimination is a standing recommendation whenever iron (or thyroid) repletion is the goal.
- **Trace minerals** supporting iron absorption: copper, zinc, selenium, manganese, molybdenum — best obtained from red meat; supplement only if diet is inadequate and under lab guidance (not a default add-on).
- **Butter with meat protein**: encourage real butter intake alongside meat proteins, because vitamin A is a cofactor of proteins.
- Patient education resources referenced in the broader course for reinforcing the grain-avoidance message: *Grain Brain* and *Wheat Belly* (used to explain phytate-iron blocking to patients).

## 5. Follow-Up Timing (Oral Therapy)

- **Recheck iron, ferritin, and saturation at 4 months** while on supplementation — explicitly **not 3 months**. This is anchored to the ~100-day RBC lifecycle so the repeat draw reflects a full red-cell turnover and gives an accurate picture of whether therapy is working. Rechecking earlier is described as "pointless" because iron incorporated into new RBCs hasn't had time to show up in labs.
- Continue rechecking **Q4 months while on supplementation**.
- After discontinuation, continue rechecking **Q6–12 months for about a year** to confirm levels hold on diet alone.
- **Goal**: maintain optimal levels on food alone (red meat + grain avoidance) without ongoing supplementation.
- If a level falls after previously stabilizing, help the patient identify the cause — common culprits called out in the course: **diet changes** (e.g., increased grain intake) and **menstrual changes in women**.
- If hypothyroidism is a concurrent concern, repeat thyroid labs alongside the iron/ferritin recheck (see [pathophysiology.md](pathophysiology.md) §10).
- General lab-draw tip (not iron-specific but noted alongside this protocol): have patients hydrate before lab draws to avoid falsely elevated sodium/potassium readings.

## 6. IV Iron Infusion Protocol

### Indication

- Very low ferritin (course example threshold: **<20**), or
- Failure of oral therapy after an adequate trial

### Prior-authorization justification

- After **2–4 months** of *compliant* oral therapy, if ferritin has not risen, document that PO therapy was ineffective — this is the standard PA justification pathway for insurance approval of infusion.
- Oral iron is explicitly ineffective for correcting deficiency in this population when documented as such: **"PO is ineffective"** — insurance may require prior authorization for infusion coverage.

### Products and dosing (call local infusion center for facility-specific protocols before ordering)

| Product | Dose/concentration | Course of therapy |
|---|---|---|
| **Feraheme** (ferumoxytol) | 510 mg / 17 mL IV | x1, 2, or 3 infusions |
| **Venofer** (iron sucrose) | 100 mg / 5 mL IV | x1, 2, or 3 infusions |

- Standard spacing: **1 or 2 infusions, 7–14 days apart** (deck); course notes give a slightly narrower window of **7–10 days apart** — use clinical/facility judgment within this 7–14 day range.
- A specific documented order pattern: **Ferraheme 510 mg x 2 doses, 1 week apart.**
- Pre-medication is rarely needed; order PRN cetirizine or diphenhydramine if a reaction occurs.
- **Order appropriate iron per facility protocol** — always confirm with the local infusion center, as protocols vary by site.
- Continue **all other dietary recommendations** (red meat, grain avoidance) through the infusion course.
- **PO Ferrochel/Ferosolv may or may not be needed** in the interim, pending lab follow-up — clinical judgment based on severity.
- **Continue vitamin C** through the day of infusion.

### Post-infusion follow-up

- Recheck iron/ferritin labs at **~6–8 weeks post-infusion** (deck states "about 6 weeks"; course notes state "6–8 weeks") — this recheck is **for safety, not for effectiveness** (levels have not had time to fully reflect the infused iron's incorporation into new RBCs at this point; see §5 rationale). It also guards against dangerously high levels post-infusion and against increased clot risk from iron overload.
- If levels remain low at the 6–8 week safety check, continue PO supplements with vitamin C.
- If levels are good at 6–8 weeks (**at or above 70**), continue diet and vitamin C without restarting oral iron.
- **Recheck levels again at 4 months post-infusion**, then **Q4 months x 2 or 3** — this is the definitive effectiveness timepoint, consistent with the RBC-lifecycle rationale in §5.

## 7. Special Situations

### High ferritin >600 — suspect hereditary hemochromatosis
- Evaluate with **HFE gene test**.
- Management shifts to reducing iron burden — **blood donation** is the stated action.

### Ferritin disproportionately high relative to iron (acute-phase pattern)
- If ferritin is roughly **>1.5x iron** (course examples: iron 100/ferritin 200; iron 67/ferritin 100), treat as an **acute-phase reactant / inflammation signal**, not true iron overload.
- Action: address the underlying inflammation (anti-inflammatory diet, vitamin D, vitamin C) rather than reducing iron intake.

### Hypothyroidism overlapping with low ferritin
- **Correct low ferritin before starting T3** (liothyronine or any T3-containing product). See [pathophysiology.md](pathophysiology.md) §10 for the full ferritin/iron <45 safety-gate rule.
- If a patient needs thyroid support urgently while ferritin is still being corrected, T4-only therapy (e.g., low-dose levothyroxine) may be used as a bridge — see Case 3 and Case 2 in [case-studies.md](case-studies.md) for worked examples of this exact sequencing decision.

### Severe constipation complicating iron repletion
- Iron supplementation is constipating; in patients with a prior history of severe constipation/diverticular disease, **resolve constipation first** (e.g., high-dose vitamin C titrated up to bowel tolerance, senna, salt-water flush) before or concurrently with starting iron. See Case 4 and Case 5 in [case-studies.md](case-studies.md).

## 8. Evidence Quality Note

This protocol is drawn from a single practitioner mentorship (functional-medicine/APRN-level clinical teaching, not a peer-reviewed guideline), cross-referenced against the practitioner's own literature citations (NIH/ODS iron fact sheet, PubMed citations on iron/ferritin physiology, and a standard pathophysiology textbook — McCance & Huether, *Pathophysiology*, 4th ed., Ch. 24). Specific numeric protocols (45 mg BID ceiling, 2–4 g vitamin C dosing, 4-month recheck interval, infusion products/dosing) reflect this practitioner's clinical convention and should be treated as **practitioner-level functional-medicine protocol**, not FDA-labeled dosing or an RCT-validated regimen. Cross-check against current product labeling and institutional infusion protocols before ordering.
