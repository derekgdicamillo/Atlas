# Iron & Ferritin — Case Studies

Worked reasoning examples from the Spring 2026 mentorship course, showing how iron/ferritin findings integrate with thyroid, inflammation, hormone, and gut-health decision-making in real patients. See [pathophysiology.md](pathophysiology.md) and [treatment-protocol.md](treatment-protocol.md) for the underlying rules each case applies.

## Case 1 — 41 y/o Perimenopausal, Post-Hysterectomy (Primary Week 2 Case Study)

**Patient profile**: 41-year-old female, post-hysterectomy (no ovaries), presenting with high inflammation and insulin resistance.

**Key findings**:

| Category | Labs |
|---|---|
| Inflammation | CRP 6.0 (high), Homocysteine 12.1 (high), WBC 10.6 (high) |
| Insulin resistance | Fasting glucose 110, Triglycerides 176 (high), HDL 28 (low) |
| Hormones | Estradiol 51, Testosterone 11 (low), DHEA-S 78 (very low) |
| Thyroid | TSH 2.0, Free T4 1.0, Free T3 3.6 |
| **Iron/ferritin** | **Iron 87, Ferritin 67** |

**Analysis**:
- Root cause identified as inflammation and insulin resistance driving the broader metabolic dysfunction.
- Low HDL and low DHEA-S indicate a lack of raw materials (cholesterol) for downstream hormone synthesis.
- TSH of 2.0 is judged suboptimal in a functional framework (target <2.0, ideally <1.5) — iodine deficiency suspected as a contributor.
- **Iron/ferritin reasoning**: Iron 87 and Ferritin 67 sit *below* the 80–100 optimal target but *above* the 65 symptom-onset floor and well above the <45 thyroid-dosing safety gate — meaning this patient's iron status, while not yet optimal, is not the acute limiting factor and does not block starting other therapies. This is a useful contrast case to the more severe cases below (2–6), where ferritin is low enough to gate treatment sequencing.

**Initial plan**:
- **Diet**: anti-inflammatory, grain-free, high-protein (red meat) — serves both the inflammation/insulin-resistance picture and ongoing iron support.
- **Supplements**: Osteopiex (D3/K2/Mg/Boron), high-dose vitamin C, low-dose iodine (<300 mcg/day).
- **Insulin support**: berberine 1,000 mg BID, or dihydroberberine 200 mg/day.
- **Hormone support**: progesterone for sleep/irritability; DHEA 5–10 mg/day, titrated to 20–30 mg.
- **Follow-up**: retest labs in **4–5 months** to allow for physiological changes — consistent with the RBC-lifecycle-anchored 4-month minimum recheck interval taught for iron/ferritin.

## Case 2 — Severe Hashimoto's, Non-Compliant with Grain-Free Diet

**Patient profile**: Severe Hashimoto's thyroiditis, non-compliant with dietary changes.

**Labs**:
- Ferritin: **6 → 11 ng/mL** (after 5 months of oral iron therapy)
- TSH: 57.3 mIU/L
- Free T4: 0.4 ng/dL
- Free T3: 3.9 pg/mL (at goal)
- Reverse T3: 8 ng/dL
- TPO Ab: 386 → >1,100 IU/mL (worsening)

**Analysis**:
- **The minimal ferritin increase (6→11 over 5 months) is interpreted as a compliance marker**, not a treatment-failure marker — it confirms the patient was not following the grain-free diet, since phytates from grain intake were blocking the iron absorption that oral supplementation depended on. This is a key clinical reasoning pattern: a flat ferritin response to adequate-duration oral therapy points first to a diet-adherence problem before escalating to infusion for treatment-failure reasons.
- Thyroid picture: high TSH + very low Free T4 indicates severe hypothyroidism. Free T3 is already at goal, so **T3 (LIO) is not needed** — the deficit is entirely T4-side.
- Because ferritin remains critically low (11), this patient still falls under the **<45 T3-dosing safety gate** — reinforcing why T3 was correctly not added regardless of the Free T3 number.

**Plan**:
- **Iron**: order a Ferraheme infusion, **510 mg x 2 doses, 1 week apart**. Pre-medication rarely needed; PRN cetirizine/diphenhydramine if needed. Retest iron/ferritin in 6–8 weeks.
- **Thyroid**: start **levothyroxine 25 mcg daily** (T4-only, consistent with the "Free T3 already at goal" finding and the ferritin safety gate against T3). Retest thyroid labs alongside the post-infusion iron panel.
- **Patient education**: reiterate the necessity of a grain-free diet; recommended patient resource — Dr. Isabella Wentz, *The Hashimoto's Protocol*.

## Case 3 — 50s Patient, Hashimoto's, New-Onset AFib After Ferritin Infusion

**Patient profile**: Patient in their 50s with Hashimoto's, developed new-onset atrial fibrillation following a ferritin infusion.

**Labs**: TSH 57, Ferritin 11.

**Symptoms**: New-onset AFib (HR 120–150), referred abdominal pain.

**Analysis**: The AFib is attributed to the **severe, chronic low ferritin combined with high TSH** — not to the infusion itself as a direct adverse reaction. Reasoning given: the infusion may have overwhelmed the heart by rapidly increasing oxygen delivery capacity in a heart that had adapted (poorly) to chronically low oxygen-carrying capacity. This case is the clinical illustration of *why* the deck flags ferritin <20 as "significant cardiac risk" and why infusion sequencing/pacing matters even when the underlying indication (severe deficiency) is correct.

**Action plan**:
- **Rate control**: metoprolol 25 mg BID; monitor for fatigue, switch to carvedilol if needed.
- **Infusion management**: delay the second half of the infusion series for a few days to let the beta-blocker take effect; consider switching to Venofer (lower side-effect profile than Feraheme in this practitioner's experience).
- **Thyroid**: start levothyroxine 25 mcg — addressing severe symptoms even though labs don't *strictly* mandate T4 yet, because the body needs support while ferritin is being optimized (again, T3 is withheld given ferritin well below the <45 gate).
- **Follow-up**: recheck EKG and symptoms in 48 hours.

## Case 4 — Severe Constipation & Diverticulitis History, Ferritin 18

**Patient profile**: History of diverticulitis and sigmoid resection, presenting with severe constipation.

**Finding**: Ferritin 18 — below the <20 "significant cardiac risk" threshold.

**Action**: High-dose vitamin C, titrated to bowel tolerance up to approximately **10–12 g/day**, to simultaneously (a) treat the constipation and (b) aid iron absorption. This is the clearest example in the source material of titrating vitamin C well beyond the standard 2–4 g TID/QID starting protocol when constipation is the rate-limiting barrier to iron therapy, and of using vitamin C's laxative effect therapeutically rather than treating it as a side effect to avoid.

## Case 5 — 70 y/o Female, Ferritin 18, Severe Constipation

**Patient profile**: 70-year-old female with ferritin of 18 and severe constipation.

**Analysis**: Ferritin 18 is critically low and poses cardiac risk (consistent with the <20 threshold), making iron supplementation urgent — but starting iron immediately would worsen the pre-existing constipation, creating a sequencing conflict.

**Action**: **Resolve constipation first**, immediately, using a strong natural laxative approach — high-dose vitamin C, senna, or a salt-water flush — before layering in iron therapy. This case, paired with Case 4, establishes the general principle: in a patient with baseline severe constipation, bowel-function correction is a prerequisite step before (or tightly paired with) iron initiation, not an afterthought managed reactively once iron causes symptoms.

## Case 6 — 46 y/o Perimenopausal, Ferritin 18, Extreme Estrogen Dominance

**Patient profile**: 46-year-old perimenopausal patient.

**Key findings**:
- **Severe iron deficiency**: Ferritin 18 (goal >80)
- High inflammation: WBC 12 (goal <7)
- Extreme estrogen dominance: Estradiol 6,000
- Low protein status: Globulin 2.5

**Action plan** (sequenced):
1. **Address iron first**: order an iron infusion; retest panel in 6–8 weeks to screen for iron overload, since overload increases clot risk — an explicit safety rationale for the post-infusion recheck beyond simple efficacy tracking.
2. **Investigate the extreme estrogen elevation**: order mammogram and uterine ultrasound to rule out pathology (fibroids, malignancy) — the magnitude of estradiol 6,000 is treated as a red flag requiring structural workup, not just a hormone-balancing intervention.
3. **Correct protein deficiency**: increase dietary protein and support absorption (globulin 2.5 is low; globulins are the protein type relevant to structural/bone health per this course's broader teaching).
4. **Defer thyroid treatment**: re-evaluate thyroid function only after iron and protein deficiencies are corrected — another example of ferritin/iron correction being sequenced *ahead of* thyroid intervention rather than run concurrently.

## Cross-Case Pattern Summary

Reading these six cases together, the course's consistent clinical sequencing logic is:

1. **Stage the ferritin severity first** (using the thresholds in [pathophysiology.md](pathophysiology.md) §6) — this determines urgency (oral vs. infusion) and which other therapies must wait.
2. **Check the <45 safety gate before touching T3.** Every case with ferritin at or below 18 either withheld T3 entirely or used T4-only bridging (Cases 2, 3, 6). Case 1, with ferritin 67, was free to proceed with other therapies without an iron-driven thyroid restriction.
3. **Fix bowel function before/alongside starting iron** if there's a constipation history — otherwise iron will not be tolerated long enough to work (Cases 4, 5).
4. **A flat ferritin response to adequate-duration oral therapy is a compliance question first** (grain intake, § phytates), not an automatic indication to escalate — but if compliance is confirmed and ferritin remains critically low, escalate to infusion (Case 2).
5. **Post-infusion rechecks serve two different purposes on two different timelines**: the 6–8 week check is for *safety* (avoid overload/clot risk, catch cardiac decompensation as in Case 3), and the 4-month check is for *efficacy* (RBC-lifecycle-aligned).
