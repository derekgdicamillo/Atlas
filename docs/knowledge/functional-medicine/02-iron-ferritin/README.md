# Iron & Ferritin — Overview

Navigation and scope for the Iron & Ferritin Metabolism topic, built from Derek's Spring 2026 functional-medicine mentorship (Week 2, Jenni Gallagher APRN / Joyful Heart) plus related iron/ferritin content scattered through the broader course.

## Scope

This topic covers the functional-medicine approach to iron and ferritin status: the physiologic distinction between circulating iron and stored ferritin, hepcidin-mediated absorption control, symptom staging by ferritin threshold, cardiac risk from untreated deficiency, oral and IV repletion protocols, dietary and cofactor strategy, and follow-up timing anchored to the ~100-day RBC lifecycle. It also covers the differential between low-ferritin symptoms and hypothyroidism, since the two overlap almost completely and the course treats this as a recurring diagnostic trap.

Primary source: Jenni Gallagher APRN, *Iron & Ferritin Metabolism Master Class* (Joyful Heart Health Care, Plattsmouth NE), Spring 2026 Mentorship Week 2, plus iron/ferritin references embedded in later weeks (thyroid, inflammation, bone/liver, gut health) of the same course.

## Key Takeaways

- **Iron vs. ferritin**: iron drives hormone synthesis, immune function, and general cellular processes; ferritin is the *stored* form of iron that becomes hemoglobin (RBC oxygen delivery) and myoglobin (muscle oxygen delivery).
- **Optimal target**: ferritin and iron in the **80–100** range (PPT states "optimal above 80 ng/mL, no symptoms"; course notes state optimal ferritin/iron target 80–100 and optimal ferritin >65 ng/mL as the minimum symptom-free floor — treat 65 as the lower symptomatic threshold and 80–100 as the true optimal target).
- **Ferritin <65** → fatigue, brain fog, lack of focus, decreased stamina, headache, heart palpitations, muscle/joint aches, feeling winded with minimal activity.
- **Ferritin <20** → significant cardiac risk (HTN, dysrhythmias, CHF can develop long-term); ferritin <10 impacts the CBC directly and becomes life-threatening.
- **First-line oral therapy**: Ferosolv (ferrous bisglycinate) 45 mg BID, **never exceeding 45 mg per dose** (nephrotoxicity ceiling), paired with high-dose vitamin C 2–4 g TID–QID titrated to bowel tolerance.
- **Vitamin D lowers hepcidin**, the hormone that blocks/impairs iron absorption — D repletion is a required cofactor, not optional.
- **Diet**: 5+ servings/week red meat (best source of iron, B12, trace minerals); eliminate grains (phytates block iron, zinc, and T3 absorption).
- **Ferritin is a safety gate for thyroid dosing**: do not start T3 medication (liothyronine/LIO, NP thyroid, Armour) if ferritin OR iron is <45 — T3 is a cardiac stimulant and low ferritin already produces cardiac symptoms; combining them is unsafe.
- **Recheck labs at 4 months**, not 3 — this aligns with the ~100-day RBC lifecycle so the repeat draw reflects a complete red cell turnover.

## When to Use

Reference this topic whenever a patient presents with fatigue, hair loss, palpitations, poor exercise tolerance, or brain fog and the differential includes iron/ferritin deficiency vs. hypothyroidism vs. anemia of chronic disease — or whenever iron/ferritin labs are being ordered, interpreted, dosed, or followed up in a functional-medicine visit. Also reference before initiating or adjusting T3-containing thyroid medication, since ferritin status gates that decision.
