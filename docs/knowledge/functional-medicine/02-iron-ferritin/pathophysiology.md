# Iron & Ferritin — Pathophysiology

Core physiology needed to interpret iron/ferritin labs and stage a patient's clinical risk, drawn from the Spring 2026 mentorship Week 2 master class (Jenni Gallagher APRN) and reinforced across later weeks of the same course.

## 1. Iron vs. Ferritin — What Each Actually Does

The course draws an explicit functional split between iron and ferritin rather than treating them as interchangeable:

| Function | Attributed to |
|---|---|
| Synthesis of hemoglobin for RBC oxygen delivery to body cells | **Ferritin** (stored iron mobilized into heme synthesis) |
| Synthesis of myoglobin for oxygen delivery to muscle cells | **Ferritin** |
| Hormone synthesis | **Iron** |
| Immune function | **Iron** |
| "Many body processes" (general cellular metabolism) | **Iron** |

Ferritin is the storage form of iron; it becomes hemoglobin and thereby governs oxygen delivery via red blood cells. Iron itself is the substrate consumed more broadly — hormone synthesis, immune cell function, and general cellular processes. This is why a patient can have a "normal" serum iron but a low ferritin (or vice versa) and present very differently: ferritin depletion hits oxygen-delivery capacity first, which is why fatigue, dyspnea on exertion, and palpitations dominate the low-ferritin symptom picture.

**Illustrative diagram from the mentorship deck** ("Iron's Cofactors," PPT p.9) maps iron's downstream roles across cell types:
- **Macrophage** — scavenging/recycling iron from RBCs, detoxing free heme, supporting erythropoiesis, M1/M2 polarization
- **T cell** — development, proliferation/activation, Th1/Th2 polarization
- **Pathogen** — iron drives high metabolic growth and pathogenicity/virulence in invading organisms (mechanistic basis for iron restriction as an innate immune strategy)
- **Neutrophil** — bactericidal activity (myeloperoxidase, oxidative burst), restricting plasma iron
- **Eosinophil** — EPX to combat attached parasites
- **RBC** — mature erythrocyte development, hemoglobin and O2 transport
- **Neuron** — dopaminergic neurotransmitter synthesis, myelination of axons
- **Muscle** — myoglobin, O2 storage/release, efficient OXPHOS (limits lactate accumulation)

## 2. Hepcidin — The Absorption Gatekeeper

**Hepcidin is a hormone that blocks/impairs iron absorption.** The course's single highest-emphasis physiology point (bolded in the deck): **Vitamin D lowers hepcidin**, which is precisely why vitamin D repletion is taught as a required cofactor for iron therapy rather than an optional add-on — without adequate vitamin D, hepcidin stays elevated and oral iron is absorbed poorly regardless of dose.

Standard physiology context (not itself dosed in the source, included for clinical reasoning): hepcidin is hepatically produced and acts on ferroportin, the transporter that exports iron from enterocytes and macrophages into plasma. High hepcidin degrades/internalizes ferroportin, trapping iron in the gut lining and in macrophage stores — functionally mimicking iron deficiency even when total body iron may be adequate (this is also the mechanism behind anemia of chronic disease/inflammation, see §5 below). The course's clinical takeaway is narrower and should be treated as the authoritative teaching point: correct vitamin D status alongside iron repletion, because low D keeps hepcidin high and blunts absorption.

## 3. Cofactors Required for Ideal Iron Absorption

Per the mentorship deck, ideal iron absorption depends on:

1. **Vitamin C** — see [treatment-protocol.md](treatment-protocol.md) for dosing
2. **Vitamin D** — lowers hepcidin (§2); dose with its own cofactors (magnesium, vitamin K2 — *not* potassium, boron, and dietary fat); never take on an empty stomach (poor absorption); D and K are fat-soluble and should be taken with a fatty meal
3. **Trace minerals** — copper, zinc, selenium, manganese, molybdenum; best obtained from red meat, supplement only if diet is inadequate and under lab guidance
4. **Vitamin A** — taught as "a cofactor of proteins"; encourage real butter intake *with* meat proteins to support this

Grains are the counter-force: grains break down into **phytates**, and phytates block iron absorption (and, per later course material, also interfere with T3 and zinc absorption).

## 4. RBC Lifecycle and Why Timing Matters

The red blood cell lifecycle (~100 days) is the physiologic basis for follow-up timing: iron incorporated into new red blood cells needs a full RBC turnover cycle to be reflected in repeat labs. The course is explicit that rechecking labs at 3 months is premature — **recheck at 4 months**, not 3, to align with a complete ~100-day cycle. This same logic is repeated independently in the course's inflammation-markers material: "it takes ~4 months for iron from an infusion to be incorporated into new red blood cells, making earlier retesting pointless." See [treatment-protocol.md](treatment-protocol.md) §Follow-Up for the full monitoring cadence.

## 5. Ferritin as an Acute-Phase Reactant (High Ferritin)

Ferritin is not a pure iron-storage marker — it also behaves as an acute-phase reactant. Two distinct high-ferritin patterns are taught:

- **Inflammation pattern**: if ferritin is meaningfully higher than iron (course examples: iron 100 / ferritin 200; iron 67 / ferritin 100 — roughly ferritin >1.5x iron), this signals an acute-phase/inflammatory process rather than true iron overload. Action: address the underlying inflammation (diet, vitamin D, vitamin C) rather than reduce iron.
- **True overload pattern**: ferritin >600 warrants evaluation for **hereditary hemochromatosis** (HFE gene test); management shifts toward reducing iron burden (e.g., blood donation) rather than supplementation.

## 6. Low-Ferritin Symptom Staging (Dose-Response by Threshold)

The mentorship deck stages symptom severity by ferritin level (values are ng/mL, also reported as mcg/L — same numeric value). This is the primary "Ferritin levels" reference graphic and should be treated as the authoritative symptom ladder:

| Ferritin level | Clinical picture |
|---|---|
| **Optimal: above 80 ng/mL** | No symptoms |
| **Below 65 ng/mL** | Heart palpitations, fast heart rate (even at rest), brain fog, muscle aches, joint pains, fatigue/lack of energy, feeling "winded" with little activity; also: fatigue, brain fog, lack of focus/concentration, decreased stamina, headache |
| **Below 45 ng/mL** | Symptoms worsen — active life becomes challenging, heart dysrhythmias develop, shortness of breath more apparent, poor sleep patterns, waking fatigued, palpitations, tachycardia, dyspnea on exertion (DOE) noticeable, poor motivation |
| **Below 20 ng/mL — significant cardiac risk** | Headaches, dizziness, hypertension begins, heart symptoms worsen, fainting/lightheadedness, lack of focus/concentration; all symptoms begin to alter quality of life; body halts weight loss because of malnutrition. Long-term: HTN, dysrhythmias, and even CHF can develop. |
| **Below 10 ng/mL** | Impacts the CBC directly and becomes **life-threatening** |
| **Below the lab's reference range** | Iron infusion recommended if covered by insurance (oral therapy alone is considered inadequate at this point) |

Separately, course narrative text states optimal ferritin is **>65 ng/mL** as the floor below which "severe fatigue and brain fog" occur, and that a "normal" lab-reported ferritin of 12 is still functionally symptomatic — reinforcing the functional-medicine principle of treating to an optimal range rather than the standard lab reference range. Reconcile these two framings as: 65 = symptom-onset floor, 80–100 = true optimal/target, and the graphic's finer-grained staging (65 / 45 / 20 / 10) as the severity ladder within the "not yet optimal" zone.

## 7. Standard Lab Ferritin Reference Range (for orientation only)

The "Types of Anemia" comparison table in the deck lists a standard-lab ferritin reference range of **10–291 ng/mL** for context when reading a lab report — this is the conventional lab range, not the functional-medicine optimal range (which is 80–100, see above).

## 8. Types of Anemia — Differential by Labs

| Marker (ref. range) | IDA (Iron Deficiency Anemia) | B12 Deficiency Anemia | ACD (Anemia of Chronic Disease) |
|---|---|---|---|
| RBC size | Microcytic | Macrocytic | Normocytic |
| MCV (82–99) | Decreased ↓ | Increased ↑ | Normal |
| MCH (27–32) | Low ↓ | Normal or High ↑ | Normal |
| TIBC | High ↑ | Normal | Normal or Low |
| Ferritin (10–291) | Low ↓ | High ↑ | Normal or High ↑ |
| B12 (130–700) | Normal | Low ↓ | Normal |
| Folate (200–650) | Normal | Normal | Normal |
| RBC (4–5) | Low | Low | Low |

Clinical read: IDA is the classic microcytic/hypochromic picture with low ferritin and high TIBC (the body is "reaching" for more iron-binding capacity). B12 deficiency is macrocytic with a paradoxically *high* ferritin. ACD (chronic inflammation driving hepcidin-mediated iron sequestration, §5 above) is normocytic with normal-to-high ferritin despite functional iron restriction — this is the pattern to recognize when ferritin looks reassuring but the patient is still symptomatic and iron is disproportionately low relative to ferritin.

## 9. The Differential Trap: Low Iron/Ferritin vs. Hypothyroidism

The course frames this explicitly as "The Challenge": **it is impossible to distinguish low iron/ferritin from hypothyroidism on symptoms alone** — the symptom sets are generally identical, including hair loss. Overlapping symptoms (from the deck's "Symptoms of Anemia" / hypothyroid symptom lists):

- Fatigue, hair loss, poor stamina
- Heart racing, palpitations
- Cold hands & feet / feeling cold
- Poor sleep, mood swings, depression, lack of motivation
- Dry skin, morning headache
- Shortness of breath

Because the two conditions cannot be separated clinically, **proper lab work is mandatory** before treatment decisions. This differential is also why ferritin is a gating factor for thyroid dosing (§10 below) — a patient can appear hypothyroid on symptoms while the true driver is iron deficiency, or have both simultaneously and require iron correction first for medication safety.

## 10. Ferritin as a Thyroid-Dosing Safety Gate

Repeated independently across multiple weeks of the course as a hard rule: **do not prescribe/start liothyronine (T3) — or any T3-containing thyroid medication (LIO, NP thyroid, Armour) — if ferritin OR iron is below 45.** Rationale stated directly: this combination is unsafe because of cardiac risk (palpitations, tachycardia) — T3 is a cardiac stimulant, and low ferritin already produces cardiac symptoms on its own, so combining them compounds risk. Practically: repeat thyroid labs alongside iron/ferritin labs, use Free T3 to guide liothyronine dosing and Reverse T3 to guide levothyroxine dosing, and correct ferritin/iron before advancing T3 therapy. Grain intake is called out specifically as interfering with *both* T3 and iron absorption simultaneously (phytates), which is a common reason a patient fails to progress on both fronts at once.
