# NAFLD — Hepatic Steatosis: Drivers, Lab Interpretation & Reversal

Provider-level reference on non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) as a carbohydrate/insulin-driven condition, with liver physiology background, LFT interpretation caveats, and the functional reversal strategy taught in Derek's Spring 2026 mentorship (Jenni Gallagher, APRN, Joyful Heart Institute), supplemented by worked case studies from the accompanying lesson notes.

## 1. Liver Physiology Background (as taught)

- The liver comprises **~2% of total body weight** and is the largest organ other than skin.
- Core functions: converts consumed nutrients into usable substances, stores them, and supplies cells with them on demand.

### Liver Zonation (cited to Kalra, et al. in source)
- **Zone I (periportal):** Best perfused, closest to oxygenated blood/nutrients, first to regenerate. Dominant in oxidative metabolism — beta-oxidation, gluconeogenesis, bile formation, cholesterol formation, amino acid catabolism.
- **Zone II (pericentral):** Sits between zones I and III.
- **Zone III:** Lowest perfusion (farthest from portal triad). Dominant in detoxification, drug biotransformation, ketogenesis, glycolysis, **lipogenesis**, glycogen synthesis, glutamine formation. This is the zone most relevant to fat accumulation in NAFLD.

### Space of Disse
Houses an extracellular matrix of collagens, proteoglycans, and other proteins that scaffold hepatocytes and the lobule as a whole. (Relevant background for fibrosis progression — stellate cell activation in this space is the classic fibrogenesis site, though the source deck does not elaborate beyond defining the structure.)

### Protein Metabolism
- Amino acid breakdown produces the toxin **ammonia**.
- Liver converts ammonia to urea and sends it to the kidneys for release. *(Direct CKD/NAFLD interaction point: hepatic urea handling links liver protein metabolism to renal clearance — relevant when titrating protein intake in patients with concurrent CKD and NAFLD. See [chronic-kidney-disease.md](chronic-kidney-disease.md).)*

### Fat Metabolism
Liver breaks down lipids for use as:
- Cholesterol and its transporters (LDL, HDL)
- Cellular matrix component — phospholipid bilayer of cell membranes
- Brain/CNS structure maintenance and preservation

### Carbohydrate Metabolism
- Excess glucose is stored as glycogen in the liver (the "reserve gas tank").
- In low-glucose periods, the liver converts glycogen back to glucose and releases it.
- **Dawn phenomenon** is named explicitly: the (previously poorly understood) elevated morning glucose after an overnight fast, driven by this glycogen-release mechanism.

## 2. Driver: Carbohydrate/Glucose Overconsumption → Insulin Resistance

**As taught, the primary mechanistic driver is carbohydrate/glucose overconsumption, not fructose specifically** — the source deck and lesson notes frame this in terms of total carbohydrate load, not an isolated fructose-metabolism pathway. When the liver is overloaded with carbohydrate, three things happen simultaneously (named explicitly in source as "the liver becomes stressed"):

1. Glycogen storage (reserve capacity fills)
2. Triglyceride synthesis (excess converted to fat)
3. Insulin release from the pancreas (drives further storage signaling)

Lesson-notes framing (verbatim concept): **"Fatty liver disease is a carbohydrate-driven condition. The liver converts excess glucose into fat (triglycerides) and stores it as glycogen, which causes the liver to become fatty when [glycogen] stores overflow."**

## 3. Disease Definitions and Progression

**Fatty Liver Disease** is used as an umbrella term encompassing:
- NAFLD
- NASH
- Some cases of cirrhosis

**NAFLD** = early liver injury thought to be caused by fat stored in the liver; dysregulation of glycogen metabolism produces histological changes causing significant hepatic dysfunction.

**NASH (non-alcoholic steatohepatitis)** = fatty liver deposits **plus** inflammation (the combination distinguishes NASH from simple steatosis/NAFLD).

### Progression pathway (as taught)
1. **Cirrhosis** — progressive dysregulation results in scar tissue.
2. **Hepatic cancers** — liver tissue becomes susceptible to altered cellular replication.
3. **Liver failure** — continued progression to the most severe state.

## 4. Diagnosis — Clinical Suspicion Factors

Consider NAFLD workup when a patient has any of:
- Diabetes of any type, insulin resistance, or PCOS
- Climbing A1c, **even when still below 7%**
- Increased waist circumference / central obesity
- Hypertension
- Elevated triglycerides
- Metabolic syndrome

### Lab Markers — Indicative but NOT Diagnostic (explicit caveat in source)

| Marker | "Optimal" target (as taught) |
|---|---|
| Alkaline phosphatase | ~60 IU/L (range given: 55–69) |
| AST | ~20 IU/L (range given: 15–25) |
| ALT | ~20 IU/L (range given: 15–25) |
| Fibrosis-4 (FIB-4) Index | Levels above optimal *might* suggest early fatty liver disease — explicitly hedged ("might," not diagnostic) |

**Key teaching point:** these CMP-derived markers are possibly *indicative* but are explicitly **not diagnostic** for NAFLD.

### Gold Standard: Imaging
- Ultrasound, CT, X-ray, MRI — **any imaging modality where the liver is directly visualized.**
- **Diagnose FLD when it is SEEN on imaging.**
- Use clinical judgment to order imaging when no other imaging is available, or when liver enzymes are above lab reference ranges.

## 5. Mainstream Treatment (as taught)

Weight-loss dose-response relationship (specific, cite-worthy thresholds from source):
- **≥5% body weight loss** → improved steatosis
- **7–9% body weight loss** → improved most histopathologic changes
- **>10% body weight loss** → the threshold at which **fibrosis improvement** was observed

Additional mainstream levers named:
- Lifestyle interventions generally
- **GLP-1s** — explicitly noted as "recommended by AAFP" for fatty liver
- Exercise

## 6. Functional Treatment (as taught)

**Primary lever: low-carb, ketogenic, or carnivore eating pattern.**

Rationale ties directly back to the three carbohydrate-overload mechanisms in §2 — reducing carbohydrate intake is taught to:
- Reduce carbs so insulin is no longer driving adipose/fat storage
- Reduce carbs so glycogen stores are reduced (less overflow into fat)
- Reduce carbs so triglycerides are significantly lowered

### Supplements to Consider (doses as taught)

| Supplement | Dose |
|---|---|
| Milk thistle | 100–250 mg once or twice daily |
| NAC | 100–600 mg once daily |
| Curcumin | 100–500 mg once or twice daily |
| Vitamin C | 2000–4000 mg BID |

Also: **optimize proteins, vitamin A/D/K status, iodine, and thyroid function** — named as a supporting bundle rather than dosed individually in this deck.

*(Note the dosing discrepancy across the two source documents: the CKD deck lists curcumin at 250–500 mg BID while the NAFLD deck lists curcumin at 100–500 mg once or twice daily. Both are transcribed here exactly as given in their respective source slides — treat as two independently authored dosing ranges from the same presenter, not a single canonical number.)*

## 7. Protein Dosing Context (cross-cutting with CKD)

The mentorship lesson notes (Week 10, delivered alongside the deferred Week 8 liver session) make an explicit protein-dosing point relevant to NAFLD/CKD overlap patients:

> "For most adults, 150g/day [of protein] is an overdose that converts to glucose via gluconeogenesis. The correct amount is based on ideal body weight (e.g., 61g/day for a 5'7" person)."

A "Do the Math" article on **joyfulhearthousecalls.com** is referenced as the source calculation tool for individualized protein targets dosed to ideal body weight rather than actual weight. This directly supports the "moderate protein" instruction in the CKD functional recommendations (see [chronic-kidney-disease.md](chronic-kidney-disease.md) §5, Recommendation #1) — excess protein converting to glucose via gluconeogenesis would counteract both the CKD and NAFLD carbohydrate-reduction strategies.

## 8. Worked Case Example (from lesson notes, Week 10 session covering deferred Week 8 liver content)

**Case: 46-year-old perimenopausal patient**

Key findings:
- Severe iron deficiency: ferritin 18 (goal >80)
- High inflammation: WBC 12 (goal <7)
- Extreme estrogen dominance: estradiol 6000
- Low protein status: globulin 2.5

Action plan (in taught sequence):
1. **Address iron first** — order iron infusion; retest panel in 6–8 weeks to prevent iron overload (which increases clot risk).
2. **Investigate the high estrogen** — order mammogram and uterine ultrasound to rule out pathology (fibroids, cancer) before assuming a purely functional cause.
3. **Correct protein deficiency** — increase dietary protein and support absorption.
4. **Defer thyroid treatment** — re-evaluate thyroid function only after correcting iron and protein deficiencies, since both can confound thyroid labs and symptoms.

This case illustrates the taught principle of **sequencing** corrections (iron → structural workup → protein → thyroid) rather than treating all abnormal findings simultaneously.

## Evidence Quality Note

- **Established/consensus:** liver zonation physiology (Kalra et al., cited), general urea cycle/protein metabolism, NAFLD/NASH/cirrhosis spectrum definitions (NIDDK), weight-loss dose-response percentages for steatosis/histology/fibrosis improvement (cited to peer-reviewed sources in the deck's reference list, exact papers not individually mapped to each claim in the slides), AAFP practice guideline recommending GLP-1s for NAFLD.
- **Practitioner-level / functional convention, not independently sourced in the deck:** the "optimal" AST/ALT/Alk Phos numeric targets (these are tighter than standard lab reference ranges and reflect the presenter's functional-medicine target zone, not a diagnostic cutoff), all listed supplement doses, and the carnivore/ketogenic recommendation as first-line NAFLD reversal (contested in mainstream hepatology, which generally endorses Mediterranean-pattern calorie-restricted diets alongside weight loss — the low-carb emphasis here is the mentorship's specific clinical position, cited internally to diet doctor and BMC Nutrition sources).
- The worked case in §8 is a single practitioner case example, not a controlled outcome — useful for clinical reasoning/sequencing pattern, not as an efficacy claim.
