# Chronic Kidney Disease — Functional Assessment & Management

Provider-level reference on CKD as a metabolic disease driven primarily by insulin resistance, with lab interpretation, staging context, and the five-part functional intervention taught in Derek's Spring 2026 mentorship (Jenni Gallagher, FNP-C, Joyful Heart Institute).

## 1. Framing: CKD as a Metabolic Disease

- CKD affects **>37 million Americans**, is often silent until advanced, is a major contributor to cardiovascular disease, and is the leading cause of dialysis/transplant. *(Source: mentorship slide, no citation given for the 37M figure — [source unclear] on year/dataset.)*
- **Core functional-medicine reframe (as taught):** "CKD often results from years of metabolic dysfunction." Insulin resistance is treated as a primary driver, not merely a comorbidity alongside diabetes and hypertension.
- Take-home line from the source deck: **"Treat the metabolism to protect the kidneys."**

### Major Causes of CKD (as listed in source)
- Type 2 diabetes
- Smoking
- Insulin resistance
- Obesity
- Hypertension
- Autoimmune disease
- Chronic infections
- Kidney stones
- Medication toxicity

## 2. Why Insulin Resistance Matters — Mechanism

Insulin resistance drives kidney injury through:
- Glomerular hyperfiltration
- Sodium retention
- Elevated blood pressure
- Endothelial dysfunction
- Oxidative stress
- Chronic inflammation
- **Result: progressive kidney injury**

### The Insulin Resistance Cascade (as taught, linear model)

> Insulin Resistance → Hyperinsulinemia → Hypertension → Inflammation → Oxidative Stress → Albuminuria → Reduced Kidney Function

This is presented as a teaching cascade, not a fully citation-backed sequential pathway — treat as a functional-medicine heuristic for patient education and clinical reasoning, not a peer-reviewed causal chain.

## 3. Lab Interpretation

### Serum Values

| Marker | Target / Interpretation (as taught) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Creatinine | Age/gender specific | Used to estimate kidney function |
| BUN | 10–20 mg/dL, **mid-range is ideal** | Rises with kidney dysfunction, dehydration, high protein intake |
| eGFR | **>90 is ideal** | — |
| Cystatin C | Lower is better | More sensitive than creatinine in some patients |
| Uric acid | **<5 is ideal** | Associated with HTN, insulin resistance, CKD progression, CVD |

### Urine Values

| Marker | Target (as taught) |
|---|---|
| Microalbumin | Negative |
| Urine albumin | <30 mg/g |
| Proteinuria | Negative |
| Urinalysis | No blood, protein, casts, or glucose |

### Clinical Pearls (as taught, verbatim concepts)
- **Kidney disease often begins before creatinine rises** — creatinine is a late marker.
- **Fasting insulin may be abnormal before glucose** — screen insulin resistance earlier than glycemic markers.
- **Micro-albuminuria is an early warning sign.**
- Earlier intervention improves outcomes.

## 4. CKD Staging — Reference Context

The source deck cites **KDIGO Clinical Practice Guideline for the Evaluation and Management of CKD** and **National Kidney Foundation KDOQI Guidelines** in its reference list but does not reproduce a staging table in the slides. The deck's only explicit staging-relevant number given is **eGFR >90 = ideal**. Standard KDIGO staging by eGFR category (G1–G5) is well-established conventional nephrology reference and is included here for provider context — **this table is external/conventional reference, not transcribed from the mentorship slides**:

| Stage | eGFR (mL/min/1.73m²) | Description |
|---|---|---|
| G1 | ≥90 | Normal or high (kidney damage with normal eGFR) |
| G2 | 60–89 | Mildly decreased |
| G3a | 45–59 | Mildly to moderately decreased |
| G3b | 30–44 | Moderately to severely decreased |
| G4 | 15–29 | Severely decreased |
| G5 | <15 | Kidney failure |

*(KDIGO also cross-stages by albuminuria category A1/A2/A3 — not detailed in source; consult KDIGO guideline directly for the full heat-map risk grid before staging a specific patient.)*

## 5. The Five Functional Recommendations (as taught)

### #1 — Reverse Insulin Resistance
- Reduce or discontinue specific medications that worsen insulin resistance (deck does not name which agents beyond the general instruction to look them up — see §7).
- Reduce grains and refined carbohydrates.
- Eliminate sugary beverages and sodas.
- Drink adequate water and electrolytes.
- Emphasize **moderate** protein and healthy fat (explicitly not high-protein).
- Consider low-carb or ketogenic eating plans.
- *(Cross-reference: lesson notes point to a "Do the Math" protein-calculation article on joyfulhearthousecalls.com, dosing protein to ideal body weight rather than actual weight — e.g., ~61 g/day cited elsewhere in the curriculum for a 5'7" person. See [nafld.md](nafld.md) §7 for the fuller protein-dosing discussion.)*

### #2 — Promote Healthy Weight Loss
Goals: improve insulin sensitivity, lower blood pressure, reduce inflammation.

Agents to consider "when appropriate": GLP-1s, berberine, inositol, metformin.

**Interaction warning (as taught):** Metformin and berberine act through the same pathways (both lower insulin, not glucose, per lesson notes) — **use cautiously together or avoid concurrent use**. The mentorship lesson notes specify the underlying concern as **risk of lactic acidosis** when combining metformin and berberine.

### #3 — Reduce Chronic Inflammation
Markers to track: hs-CRP, uric acid, homocysteine, "?Ferritin" (flagged with a question mark in the source, i.e., taught as an inflammation marker of uncertain/variable reliability), omega-3 status, gut health.

Interventions named on the slide graphic:
- High-dose vitamin C
- Vitamin D with its cofactors
- Curcumin/turmeric
- Chlorella
- Resveratrol
- CoQ10
- Fish oil
- Omega-3s
- Sleep optimization (see [sleep-optimization.md](sleep-optimization.md))

### #4 — Optimize Blood Pressure
- Primary lever: lower insulin levels.
- Improve magnesium status.
- Exercise.
- Stress management.
- Weight reduction.

### #5 — Build and Preserve Muscle
- Improves insulin sensitivity, supports mitochondrial health, improves metabolic flexibility.
- **Recommendation: resistance training 2–3x/week.**
- Lesson-notes elaboration: encourage "purposeful movement" and resistance training using household items as weights where formal equipment access is limited.

## 6. Helpful Supplements (doses as taught)

| Supplement | Dose |
|---|---|
| Berberine (dihydroberberine form) | 200 mg BID |
| Berberine (regular form, alternative) | 500–1000 mg BID |
| Inositol | 750 mg–4000 mg BID |
| Alpha-lipoic acid | 200–300 mg BID |
| Omega-3 fatty acids | 1000–2000 mg BID |
| Magnesium glycinate | Up to 600 mg QD |
| Curcumin | 250–500 mg BID |
| NAC (when appropriate) | 600 mg QD |

**Caution flagged in source:** do not combine full-dose berberine with metformin without caution (see §5, Recommendation #2).

## 7. Medication Review — What to Avoid / Reassess

The source deck's only explicit instruction here is procedural, not a specific drug list:
- "Reduce or d/c specific medications" contributing to insulin resistance — **[source unclear]** on which specific agents beyond the general principle.
- **Take-home instruction (as taught):** actively look up every medication a CKD patient is on using **Epocrates, drugs.com, or rxlist.com** to check renal dosing/nephrotoxicity before prescribing or continuing therapy. This is presented as a standing clinical habit, not optional.

## 8. Take-Home Messages (verbatim structure from source)

1. CKD is often a metabolic disease.
2. Insulin resistance is a major driver.
3. Weight loss improves outcomes.
4. Muscle building improves metabolic health.
5. Look up medications (Epocrates, drugs.com, rxlist.com).
6. Treat the metabolism to protect the kidneys.
