# Sleep Optimization — Functional Protocol, Hygiene & Named Supplements

Provider-level reference on sleep as a downstream symptom of metabolic/hormonal dysfunction rather than a standalone problem, with the exact functional sleep hygiene protocol, lab workup, and named supplement doses taught in Derek's Spring 2026 mentorship (Jenni Gallagher, FNP-BC, Joyful Heart Institute).

## 1. Core Framing (as taught)

**Poor sleep is a symptom of underlying metabolic dysfunction, not a standalone issue.** The core clinical strategy is to identify and treat root causes (hormone imbalance, high cortisol, glucose swings) rather than defaulting to sedatives.

### Why Sleep Matters (systems affected, as listed)
- Insulin sensitivity
- Cortisol regulation
- Immune function
- Cognitive performance
- Hormone production
- Weight management
- Cardiovascular health

### Poor Sleep as a Mimic
**Poor sleep can mimic hypothyroidism, anxiety, depression, ADHD, chronic fatigue, and hormonal imbalance.** This is an explicit clinical warning — do not anchor on a mimicking diagnosis before ruling out a sleep-driven presentation.

## 2. Root Causes (as taught, lesson-notes framing)

- **Hormonal imbalance:** high estrogen/testosterone → low progesterone → sleep disruption.
- **Cortisol dysregulation:** chronic stress → high cortisol → glucose spikes → nighttime awakenings.
- **Glucose swings:** high-carb dinners → glucose spike → crash → hunger → waking.
- **Pain:** estrogen deficits commonly cause gluteal tendinopathy ("hip pain") that disrupts sleep — a specific, non-obvious pain-sleep link called out explicitly in the mentorship.

### "Assess Before You Supplement" Checklist (as taught, do not skip to supplements)
1. Poor sleep hygiene
2. Excess evening cortisol
3. Progesterone deficiency / estrogen dominance
4. Blood sugar dysregulation
5. Sleep apnea
6. Pain, anxiety, stress
7. Alcohol use
8. Medication side effects

Assessment tools named: questionnaires, **STOP-BANG** (apnea screening), wearables, hormone panels, and CGM (continuous glucose monitor) data.

## 4. Sleep Hygiene Fundamentals (as taught)

- Consistent bedtime/wake time
- Dark, cool room — lesson notes specify **~65°F**
- Avoid screens 1–2 hours before bed; lesson notes add: use **blue light blockers** and create a screen-free hour before bed
- Morning sunlight exposure
- Limit caffeine after noon
- Avoid alcohol as a sleep aid
- Exercise regularly, but **early in the day** (not close to bedtime)
- Nutrition: eat a **protein-rich dinner** to stabilize overnight glucose

## 5. Blood Sugar & Sleep

- Overnight glucose swings may cause **2–4 AM awakenings.**
- Associated symptoms: night sweats, palpitations, vivid dreams.
- Interventions: protein-rich dinner; reduce evening sugar/carbs; evaluate insulin resistance and CGM data.

## 6. Hormone-Directed Interventions

### Progesterone & Sleep — Mechanism
- Supports GABA activity
- Promotes relaxation
- Reduces sleep latency
- Improves sleep continuity
- May reduce nighttime anxiety
- **Symptoms of deficiency:** insomnia, anxiety, PMS, night sweats

### Clinical Use of Progesterone (as taught — women, first-line for sleep disruption)
- **Micronized progesterone is preferred.**
- **Common bedtime dosing: 100–200 mg.** Lesson notes specify this as the **first-line treatment** for sleep disruption in women.
- Titration guidance from lesson notes: **start at 100 mg.** If the patient wakes groggy, either reduce the dose or move the dosing time earlier (e.g., **5–6 PM**).
- **Route matters:** vaginal or rectal administration is **not effective for systemic sleep benefit** — oral dosing is required for the CNS/GABA effect.
- May improve sleep quality, mood, and menopausal symptoms; individualize based on symptoms and labs.

### Men — Hormone Precursors
Use hormone precursors such as **pregnenolone and DHEA** (as taught in lesson notes). **[Source unclear]** on specific doses for either agent in this context — not given in either source document.

## 7. Named Supplements & Exact Doses (as taught)

| Supplement | Dose | Mechanism / notes (as taught) |
|---|---|---|
| Magnesium glycinate or L-threonate | 200–400 mg at bedtime | Supports GABA signaling, muscle relaxation, nervous system regulation; helpful for stress, tension, restless sleep |
| Glycine | 3 grams at bedtime | May improve sleep depth/quality; supports thermoregulation and collagen synthesis |
| L-theanine | 100–400 mg before bed | Promotes alpha brain wave activity; relaxation without sedation; reduces stress response |
| Melatonin | **0.3–3 mg** | Circadian rhythm support; useful for jet lag and shift work. **Explicitly NOT positioned as a primary sleep aid.** "More is not always better." Many patients have reactions/side effects (vivid dreams called out specifically in lesson notes) and cannot tolerate it — use sparingly. |

## 8. The Functional Sleep Framework (6-step sequence, as taught)

1. Optimize sleep hygiene
2. Restore circadian rhythm
3. Stabilize blood sugar
4. Manage stress
5. Consider progesterone
6. Use targeted supplementation

This is an explicit **sequence** — hygiene and circadian/metabolic fundamentals are addressed before layering in hormone therapy or supplements, consistent with the "assess before you supplement" principle in §2.

## 9. The Unending Cycle of Poor Sleep (source infographic, Joyful Heart Institute)

Poor sleep both results from and worsens six interacting factors:
- **Stress & anxiety** — racing thoughts and elevated cortisol keep the mind wired at night
- **Blood sugar imbalance** — spikes and crashes wake the patient and disrupt deep sleep
- **Gut dysfunction** — bloating, discomfort, and inflammation interfere with sleep quality
- **Hormone imbalance** — thyroid, cortisol, estrogen, and melatonin imbalances impact sleep
- **Inflammation** — chronic inflammation causes pain, discomfort, restless sleep
- **Mood & mental health** — depression and anxiety both contribute to and result from poor sleep
- **Light & screen exposure** — blue light at night suppresses melatonin and confuses the internal clock
- **Stimulants** — caffeine, nicotine, and alcohol disrupt ability to fall and stay asleep

Framing line from the source graphic: **"Break the cycle. Heal the root cause. When we address the underlying factors, we restore restful sleep — and improve your whole health."**

## Evidence Quality Note

- **Established/consensus:** general sleep-hygiene principles (consistent schedule, dark/cool room, light exposure timing, caffeine/alcohol effects) are consistent with AASM guidelines (cited in source reference list, not individually mapped to each claim). Progesterone's GABA-agonist mechanism and role in sleep is supported by cited literature (Caufriez 2011; Abbasi 2012; Yamadera 2007; Lyon 2011 — citations given as author/year only in source, full references not provided). Melatonin's circadian role and appropriate use for jet lag/shift work is consensus (NAMS Position Statement cited).
- **Practitioner-level / functional convention, not independently sourced in the deck:** the specific "optimal" lab value table in §3 (tighter than standard lab reference ranges), the exact progesterone titration protocol (start 100mg, retime to 5–6 PM if groggy), the STOP-BANG/CGM/wearables assessment bundle, and all "additional functional options" in §7 without doses. These reflect the presenter's clinical practice pattern, not a cited trial.
- Micronized oral progesterone for sleep in perimenopausal/menopausal women has real supporting literature (the Caufriez/Abbasi citations point in this direction), but the specific 100–200 mg bedtime dose and men's pregnenolone/DHEA precursor approach should be treated as clinical convention pending direct citation, not as a guideline-level recommendation.
