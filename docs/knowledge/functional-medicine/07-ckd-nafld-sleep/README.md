# Chronic Kidney Disease, NAFLD & Sleep Optimization — Overview

Navigation and clinical scope for three distinct topics taught together in Week 8 of Derek's Spring 2026 mentorship (Jenni Gallagher, FNP-C/APRN, Joyful Heart Institute): chronic kidney disease, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease, and functional sleep optimization.

## Scope

These three conditions are grouped in the source curriculum because they share a common upstream driver — **insulin resistance / hyperinsulinemia** — but each has its own distinct organ system, lab workup, and intervention protocol. They are documented as separate files here rather than merged, because the clinical decision-making (staging, lab targets, named supplements/doses) does not overlap cleanly between them.

- **Chronic Kidney Disease** is framed as a metabolic disease of the kidney, driven by the insulin-resistance cascade (hyperinsulinemia → hypertension → inflammation → oxidative stress → albuminuria → reduced kidney function), with a five-part functional intervention framework. See [chronic-kidney-disease.md](chronic-kidney-disease.md).
- **NAFLD** is framed as a carbohydrate/glucose-overload disease of the liver (glycogen storage + triglyceride synthesis + insulin release overwhelming hepatic capacity), progressing NAFLD → NASH → cirrhosis → hepatic cancer/failure if unaddressed. See [nafld.md](nafld.md).
- **Sleep Optimization** is framed as a downstream symptom of metabolic and hormonal dysfunction (glucose swings, cortisol dysregulation, progesterone deficiency) rather than a standalone complaint — the protocol treats root cause before reaching for supplements. See [sleep-optimization.md](sleep-optimization.md).

## Key Takeaways

1. **All three conditions are treated as downstream expressions of insulin resistance/hyperinsulinemia**, not independent organ-specific diseases. The mentorship's consistent instruction is to treat the metabolism (carbohydrate reduction, weight loss, muscle building, blood sugar stabilization) as the primary lever, with organ-specific labs and supplements layered on top.
2. **CKD:** creatinine and eGFR are late markers — fasting insulin and micro-albuminuria can be abnormal earlier. eGFR >90 and uric acid <5 are the source's "ideal" targets. Metformin and berberine share a pathway (both lower insulin, not glucose) and should be combined cautiously or not at all due to lactic acidosis risk.
3. **NAFLD:** liver enzymes (AST/ALT/Alk Phos) are explicitly taught as *indicative but not diagnostic* — imaging (any modality where the liver is directly seen) is the gold standard. Weight loss has a dose-response relationship: ≥5% improves steatosis, 7–9% improves most histopathology, >10% is needed for fibrosis improvement.
4. **Sleep:** poor sleep can mimic hypothyroidism, anxiety, depression, ADHD, and chronic fatigue — rule out a sleep-driven presentation before anchoring on those diagnoses. For women, micronized oral progesterone (100–200 mg, start at 100 mg, retime to 5–6 PM if groggy) is first-line for sleep disruption; vaginal/rectal routes do not work systemically for sleep. Melatonin (0.3–3 mg) is positioned narrowly for circadian/jet-lag/shift-work use, not as a general sleep aid.
5. **Protein dosing is a cross-cutting theme.** Both the CKD and NAFLD protocols call for *moderate*, ideal-body-weight-based protein rather than high protein — excess protein converts to glucose via gluconeogenesis and undermines the carbohydrate-reduction strategy central to both conditions. See [nafld.md](nafld.md) §7.

## When to Use

- New or existing patient with any combination of: rising creatinine/BUN trending toward the high-normal range, microalbuminuria, elevated uric acid, central obesity, elevated triglycerides, climbing A1c (even under 7%), or known insulin resistance/PCOS/T2DM — use [chronic-kidney-disease.md](chronic-kidney-disease.md) and [nafld.md](nafld.md) together, since the lab-suspicion criteria overlap heavily.
- Any patient reporting poor sleep, especially with 2–4 AM awakenings, night sweats, perimenopausal/menopausal status, or symptoms that could be mistaken for thyroid/mood/ADHD pathology — start with [sleep-optimization.md](sleep-optimization.md) §2's root-cause checklist before prescribing a sleep aid.
- Building or refining a functional lab panel for a metabolic-syndrome-adjacent workup — pull exact "optimal" target values from [sleep-optimization.md](sleep-optimization.md) §3 and [chronic-kidney-disease.md](chronic-kidney-disease.md) §3; do not freehand target numbers.
- Reviewing a patient's medication list for renal or hepatic impact — [chronic-kidney-disease.md](chronic-kidney-disease.md) §7 documents the taught habit of checking every medication via Epocrates/drugs.com/rxlist.com.
- Cross-reference [06-metabolic-syndrome](../06-metabolic-syndrome/) for the underlying insulin-resistance/hyperinsulinemia model and carb-restriction dietary framework that all three topics in this folder build on.

## Evidence Quality Note

Source material mixes tiers, consistently across all three files:
- **Established/consensus:** KDIGO/KDOQI CKD guidelines, ADA Standards of Care, NIDDK NAFLD/NASH definitions, AAFP NAFLD practice guideline (GLP-1s), AASM sleep guidelines, NAMS position statement on hormone therapy, and general insulin-resistance/inflammation pathophysiology (DeFronzo, Reaven, Hall et al.).
- **Practitioner-level / functional convention, not independently sourced in the slides:** all "optimal" lab target tables (tighter than standard lab reference ranges), all named supplement doses, the progesterone titration protocol, the 5-part CKD functional framework, and the low-carb/ketogenic/carnivore-first NAFLD reversal strategy (contested in mainstream hepatology, which more commonly endorses Mediterranean-pattern calorie restriction). These reflect Jenni Gallagher's clinical practice pattern at Joyful Heart Institute — flagged inline in each file where used, and should be verified against current primary literature before being treated as guideline-level.
- Where a specific number, product, or mechanism was not stated in the source materials, it is marked **[source unclear]** rather than filled in — do not backfill missing doses from general knowledge without independent verification.
