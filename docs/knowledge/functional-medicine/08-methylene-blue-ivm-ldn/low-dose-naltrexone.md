# Low-Dose Naltrexone — Mechanism, Indications & Dosing

Opioid-receptor-antagonist repurposed at low, nightly doses for immune modulation and anti-inflammatory effect, taught by Jenni Gallagher, APRN across Week 4 and Week 9 of Derek's Spring 2026 mentorship — two distinct titration/dosing schemes are given in the source material and are preserved separately below rather than reconciled.

## Mechanism of Action

- LDN is an **opioid receptor antagonist**. At low nightly doses it produces a transient opioid receptor blockade (a few hours' duration), which the body responds to by upregulating endorphin production and opioid receptor sensitivity/density. The net effect as taught is immune modulation and reduced inflammation.
- As taught directly: *"An opioid receptor antagonist that modulates the immune system to reduce inflammation"* / *"LDN works on opioid receptors to modulate the immune system and reduce inflammation."*
- **Evidence quality**: this transient-blockade → endorphin/receptor-upregulation model is the standard mechanistic theory referenced broadly in the LDN literature (the instructor references *The LDN Book*, ed. Linda Elsegood, Volumes One and Two, as a source text). The mentorship slides state the mechanism at summary level; detailed receptor pharmacology is not elaborated in the source material.

## Prerequisite: Grain-Free Diet

- Explicitly taught as a **prerequisite** for LDN efficacy: grains cause an inflammatory spike that negates LDN's overnight anti-inflammatory effect, making the treatment less effective — and a poor value for a cash-pay compounded medication — if the patient isn't grain-free.
- Patient-facing graphic (`LDN.JPG`, Joyful Heart Health Care), transcribed in full:
  > **LDN — Low Dose Naltrexone**
  > **GREAT ANTI-INFLAMMATORY**
  > **Works best in a grain-free body**
  > *Must be increased slowly*
- Decision-to-treat framing (LDN slide deck, "How do you decide to use LDN?"): treat LDN as a partnership with the patient — assess whether the patient is willing to eat grain-free indefinitely. As taught: *"The see-saw fluctuations of inflammation and LDN will prevent adequate inflammation reduction"* if the patient is not grain-free.

## Indications / Uses as Taught

**Anti-inflammatory / autoimmune:**
- Rheumatoid arthritis, Sjogren's, Hashimoto's, systemic lupus erythematosus, and other autoimmune disease
- Inflammatory bowel disease
- Lyme disease, Long-Haul COVID, fibromyalgia
- Multiple sclerosis, cancers (listed as a benefit/use category on the source slide; evidence not further detailed in the source material)

**Mental health:**
- Antidepressant qualities
- PTSD, traumatic brain injury
- Autism spectrum
- Dissociative disorders

**Weight loss (adjunct):**
- LDN can aid weight loss by reducing inflammation; it is one component of the FDA-approved Contrave formula (naltrexone + bupropion).
- A separate compounded weight-loss formula (inositol + LDN + methylcobalamin) is taught in detail — see **Compounded Weight-Loss Formula** below.

**Evidence label**: predominantly off-label / functional-medicine-practitioner convention. LDN for autoimmune/inflammatory conditions has a growing but still limited randomized-trial evidence base; the oncology, autism, dissociative-disorder, and TBI listings here are practitioner-taught benefit categories, not established evidence-based indications for LDN — flag this distinction to patients and in documentation.

## Dosing — Two Protocols Taught (preserved separately, not reconciled)

The source material gives two different LDN titration schemes across different sessions. Both are reproduced in full below. **Do not blend the step schedules** — pick the protocol that matches the prescription/compounding-pharmacy pattern you are actually using.

### Protocol A — "Dr. Wentz's Method" (Week 4 & Week 9 session notes)

Titration:
- **Week 1–2: 0.5 mg at bedtime**
- **Week 3–4: 1.0 mg at bedtime**
- **Week 5–6: 1.5 mg at bedtime**
- (Week 4 notes phrase the general rule as: start at 0.5 mg daily for two weeks, then increase by 0.5 mg every two weeks — 0.5 mg → 1.0 mg → 1.5 mg, etc.)

Maintenance: **4.5–7 mg/day**, adjusted based on clinical response.

Rationale as taught: the slow titration prevents side effects from overwhelming opioid receptor activation too quickly — most notably **nightmares**, which are common with rapid or high initial dosing.

### Protocol B — Gallagher's Compounded Rx Pattern (`LDN for mentorship.pdf`, "How to RX LDN")

**First Rx** — Low-dose naltrexone 0.5 mg capsules:
- Take 1 cap PO HS QD x 2 weeks, then 2 cap PO HS QD x 2 weeks, then 3 cap PO HS QD x 2 weeks (i.e., 0.5 mg → 1 mg → 1.5 mg nightly over 6 weeks)
- Qty: 84; No refills

**Second Rx** — Low-dose naltrexone 1.5 mg capsules:
- Take 2 cap PO HS QD x 2 weeks, then 3 cap PO HS QD x 2 weeks (i.e., 3 mg → 4.5 mg nightly over 4 weeks)
- Qty: 70; No refills

**Long-term / maintenance:**
- LDN **4.5 mg – 5.5 mg or 6 mg** capsules
- Take 1 cap PO HS QD daily x 90 days
- May increase up to approximately **8–10 mg QD PRN** patient needs
- Inflammatory marker recheck: usually every **9–18 months** (*"Inflammation takes a LONG time to fall in most cases"*) — markers referenced: antibodies (AB), hs-CRP, uric acid, ferritin, WBC, complement 3 or 4, RA markers, etc.

### Discrepancy Flag

Protocol A reaches 1.5 mg by week 5–6 with a 4.5–7 mg/day maintenance range. Protocol B's First + Second Rx sequence also reaches 1.5 mg by week 6, then continues to 4.5 mg by week 10, with long-term maintenance dosing of 4.5–6 mg (titratable up to ~8–10 mg PRN). The two schedules are broadly consistent in shape (slow, biweekly step-ups to a similar maintenance ceiling) but differ in exact capsule strengths and weekly increments used to get there. **Use whichever protocol matches the compounding pharmacy's available capsule strengths and the prescription pattern you are titrating from.**

## Compounded Weight-Loss Formula (Inositol / LDN / Methylcobalamin)

Per the LDN slide deck ("Compound Wt Loss Med"). **Note**: the instructor states she **no longer includes topiramate** in this formula, at any step, due to side effects (retinal issues and osteomalacia reported in two patients) — the original per-capsule formula strengths below include topiramate as originally taught, with the removal explicitly flagged per step.

**Step 1 (titration)**: Inositol / LDN / topiramate / methylcobalamin — 175 mg / 1 mg / 25 mg / 0.25 mg per cap *(topiramate no longer included, per instructor's current practice)*
- Take 1 cap QAM x 7 days, then 2 caps QAM x 7 days, then 3 caps QAM x 14 days, then start Step 2
- Qty: 61 capsules

**Step 2 (maintenance)**: Inositol / LDN / topiramate / methylcobalamin — 500 mg / 4 mg / 75 mg / 0.25 mg per cap *(topiramate no longer included, per instructor's current practice)*
- Take 1 cap QAM x 7 days, then 2 caps daily for 6 weeks (maintenance)
- May refill or wean off; patients are taught **not** to start Step 3 if they want to continue Step 2 long-term
- Qty: 100 capsules

**Step 3 (optional weaning)**: Inositol / LDN / topiramate / methylcobalamin — 175 mg / 1 mg / 25 mg / 0.25 mg per cap *(topiramate no longer included, per instructor's current practice)*
- Take 3 caps QAM x 7 days, then 2 caps QAM x 7 days, then 1 cap QAM x 7 days, then stop
- Qty: 42 capsules
- Instructor's note: weaning is not physiologically necessary, but is taught to patients who psychologically prefer to decrease slowly since they increased slowly. Patients are told to put Step 3 back in the cabinet if they aren't ready to stop; once they request a refill and send payment, the instructor orders 90 days of Step 2 so they can continue.

Cost as taught for this compounded formula: approximately $225–250 for a 90-day supply.

## Practical / Perioperative Considerations

- **Lab monitoring**: recheck inflammatory markers (e.g., CRP) only after 8–9 months, to avoid patient frustration from slow-moving results.
- **Surgery**: discontinue LDN **3–5 days pre-op** if narcotics will be needed for perioperative analgesia (naltrexone blocks the opioid receptors needed for opioid pain control). The patient can restart LDN post-op at a lower dose (e.g., 1 mg) and retitrate more quickly than the initial schedule.

## Practice / Prescribing Logistics (as taught)

- LDN must be **compounded**; standard commercial naltrexone tablets (50 mg) should not be crushed and diluted by patients to approximate LDN dosing.
- Compounded LDN is not covered by insurance as taught; the instructor does not write prior authorizations. Work with a local compounding pharmacy and send a titrating prescription.
