# Methylene Blue — Mechanism, Indications, Dosing & Safety

Functional-medicine mitochondrial-support and antimicrobial agent taught in Week 9 of Derek's Spring 2026 mentorship (Jenni Gallagher, APRN, Joyful Heart Institute) — the first FDA-approved drug in history with a long safety record, but with two hard-stop screening flags that must be checked before every prescription.

## Overview / History

- First medication ever approved by the FDA, with safety data dating to 1876.
- On the WHO's List of Essential Medicines.
- Historically used as an antimalarial, antiviral, and anti-infective agent.
- Anti-inflammatory action: attenuates LPS-induced increases in IL-6 and STAT3 activation.

## Mechanism of Action

- Functions as an alternative mitochondrial electron transfer carrier — enhances bioenergetics and inhibits biosynthetic pathways (as taught: "enhancing bioenergetics and inhibiting biosynthetics").
- Shown in rodent models to provide a protective effect in Parkinson's disease, Alzheimer's disease, Huntington's disease, and Friedreich's ataxia.
- Reverses the Warburg effect and inhibits cancer cell proliferation (taught as a mechanistic claim).
- **Evidence quality**: mechanistic and preclinical (rodent/in-vitro models) for the neuroprotective and anticancer claims above — these are basic-science/preclinical rationale, not clinical outcome evidence, and should be labeled as such to patients.

## Indications / Uses as Taught

- Optimize mitochondrial ATP production
- Reduce brain fog; enhance cognitive clarity
- Improve general energy level
- Infections: malaria, Lyme disease, urinary tract infections and septic shock
- Sleep: patients reportedly sleep better since starting MB (attributed to improved oxygenation)
- Case example (session notes, not on the slides): significant improvement in function and oxygenation for a patient with severe CHF — a single anecdotal case, not systematic evidence

**Evidence label**: predominantly off-label / functional-medicine convention. Malaria treatment and antiseptic/methemoglobinemia-reduction uses have long-standing pharmacologic and historical basis; the cognitive, energy, sleep, Lyme, and CHF claims taught here are practitioner-experience level and are not supported by the source material with clinical trial citations — treat as expert-opinion/practitioner-protocol level, not RCT-established indications.

## Dosing

- Low-dose oral MB range as taught: **5–20 mg**.
- Most common dose taught: **5 mg**, with flexibility in dosing. Sig as taught: 1–2 capsules by mouth, 1–2 times per day.
- Oral bioavailability near 100%; renally eliminated.
- May be taken 1–3 times per day; oral half-life approximately **4–6 hours**.
- Time to improvement may vary from days to weeks; dose can be titrated gradually over time.
- IV methylene blue is explicitly taught as **not required** for these functional-medicine indications (as distinct from conventional IV use, e.g., for methemoglobinemia).

### Cost (as taught, reference only)

- Varies based on dose and quantity per day.
- 30 capsules: approximately $45–50.
- 90 capsules: approximately $80–90.
- Session notes give an alternate reference figure of approximately $1 per capsule.

## CRITICAL Safety — Contraindications & Cautions

### 1. Serotonin Syndrome — SSRIs / SNRIs

Methylene blue acts as a potent, reversible MAO-A inhibitor at these doses. Co-administration with serotonergic agents risks serotonin syndrome.

- As taught: **"Caution in patients taking SSRIs or SNRIs due to risk of serotonin syndrome."**
- Session notes reinforce this as a contraindication-level flag: **"avoid use with SSRIs/SNRIs due to serotonin syndrome risk"** and instruct to **"wean patients off these drugs before starting MB."**
- **Provider action**: screen every patient's full medication list for SSRIs and SNRIs before prescribing MB. The taught practice is to wean the serotonergic agent first, not co-prescribe. Do not initiate MB in a patient currently taking an SSRI/SNRI without a documented wash-out/wean plan.
- Note: the source material's explicit teaching is limited to the SSRI/SNRI class. Standard pharmacology extends serotonin-syndrome risk to other serotonergic agents (MAOIs, tramadol, triptans, linezolid, meperidine, etc.); this broader class is not explicitly enumerated in the mentorship slides, so apply independent clinical screening for those agents as well.

### 2. G6PD Deficiency / Hemolytic Anemia

- As taught: **"Avoid in patients with hemolytic anemia, especially those with G6PD deficiency."**
- Screen G6PD status before prescribing, particularly in patients from ancestry groups with higher G6PD deficiency prevalence (Mediterranean, African, Middle Eastern, Southeast Asian) — this population-risk framing is standard medical knowledge and is not itself detailed in the source slides, but follows directly from the G6PD caution as taught.

### 3. Pregnancy / Breastfeeding

- As taught: **"Avoid in patients who are pregnant and/or breastfeeding."**

### 4. Sulfa / Sulfonamide Cross-Sensitivity

- MB contains a sulfur molecule; screen for allergies to sulfa drugs such as Bactrim before prescribing (session notes).

### 5. Cosmetic / Benign Effects

- Turns urine blue/green.
- Can stain clothing.
