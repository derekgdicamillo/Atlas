# Ivermectin — Mechanism, Indications, Dosing & Safety

Broad-spectrum antiparasitic taught in Week 9 of Derek's Spring 2026 mentorship (Jenni Gallagher, APRN, Joyful Heart Institute), covering both its FDA-approved antiparasitic role and the substantially broader off-label uses (rosacea, COVID-19, investigational oncology) taught in this session — evidence quality is flagged per indication below.

## Overview / Discovery

- Discovered in the late 1970s from a soil bacterium; described as a significant advancement in medicine.
- Initially developed to treat parasitic infections; now used across both human and veterinary medicine.
- Historically developed to treat river blindness (onchocerciasis), significantly reducing disease prevalence, and is also effective for lymphatic filariasis.
- Taught as having a strong safety and efficacy profile underlying its widespread adoption; can be administered orally or topically.

## Mechanism of Action

- Primarily targets the nervous system of parasites.
- Binds to glutamate-gated chloride channels, enhancing membrane permeability and inducing cell death in parasites (as taught).
- Investigational/preclinical: may also interact with cancer cells through related mechanisms — inhibits cell proliferation, blocks cell cycle progression, promotes apoptosis, and hinders cell migration (relevant to metastasis) in oncology research models.
- In ovarian cancer preclinical models, ivermectin is described as acting through molecular networks affecting key energy-metabolism pathway molecules: PFKP (glycolysis), IDH2 and IDH3B (Krebs cycle), ND2, ND5, CYTB, and UQCRH (oxidative phosphorylation), and MCT1/MCT4 (lactate shuttle). Also described as mediating lncRNA-EIF4A3-mRNA axes in ovarian cancer.

## Indications as Taught

### FDA-Approved / Conventional Antiparasitic Use
- River blindness (onchocerciasis)
- Lymphatic filariasis
- Standard antiparasitic use (pinworms, mites, other GI parasites)

### Off-Label / Functional-Medicine Convention (evidence quality flagged)

**Rosacea** — reasonable mechanistic and trial basis:
- Demodex folliculorum mite is identified in 35–50% of rosacea cases; the mite erodes the epithelium, causing skin hypersensitivity. Other bacteria found in association: Staphylococcus epidermidis, Helicobacter pylori, and Bacillus oleronius. (Cited sources: PMC4807898, Medscape article 998076.)
- Topical formulation as taught: **Ivermectin 1% cream (Soolantra)** — apply to the area and leave for 10 minutes, then rinse off gently.
- Other rosacea treatments listed alongside ivermectin in the same teaching: doxycycline, tetracycline, metronidazole, azelaic acid, sodium sulfacetamide, erythromycin, oxymetazoline, pimecrolimus, tacrolimus, permethrin, crotamiton.

**COVID-19 / antiviral** — contested evidence base, presented as taught with citations:
- A 5-day ivermectin treatment arm showed earlier virologic clearance than placebo; this was not replicated in the ivermectin + doxycycline arm (11.5 days, p = 0.27). Proposed mechanism: IVM prevents viral proteins from entering the host cell nucleus. (Cited: PMC7709596.)
- In vitro: a single treatment produced an approximately 5,000-fold reduction in SARS-CoV-2 at 48 hours in cell culture. (Cited: ScienceDirect S0166354220302011.)
- Meta-analysis figures cited in the slides: 204 published works on ivermectin for COVID-19 (156 peer-reviewed; 96 comparing treatment vs. control groups); IVM adopted for early treatment in all or part of 22 countries (39 including non-government medical organizations); the 96 comparative studies span 1,030 scientists reporting on 135,554 patients across 27 countries.
- Reported statistically significant improvements (mortality, ventilation, ICU, hospitalization, recovery, cases, viral clearance): 85% for prophylaxis (CI 77–90%), 62% for early treatment (CI 51–70%), 43% for late treatment (CI 28–54%).
- A 56% improvement was recorded across 45 randomized controlled trials (CI 41–68%); a 51% lower mortality was evidenced across 49 studies (CI 37–61%). (Cited: Bentham Direct, `cocat/10.2174/2213337210666230809141610`.)
- **Evidence quality flag**: ivermectin for COVID-19 remains a contested area of the clinical literature. Current FDA and CDC guidance does not recommend ivermectin for COVID-19 treatment or prevention. This section reproduces the figures and framing as taught in the mentorship material; apply independent, current guideline review before acting on these figures clinically.

**Oncology (investigational, preclinical)**:
- Described as having a broad-spectrum anti-cancer effect on multiple human cancers, with the most mechanistic detail given for ovarian cancer (suppresses cell proliferation/growth, blocks cell cycle progression, promotes apoptosis; clonogenic survival assays showed inhibited colony formation).
- Breast cancer preclinical model: 40–60% of animals treated with ivermectin plus an anti-PD1 antibody combination completely eradicated their tumors; taught rationale is a dual anti-cancer and immunomodulatory mechanism that may potentiate other FDA-approved immune checkpoint inhibitors.
- **Evidence quality flag**: this is animal and cell-culture data, not human oncology treatment evidence. Do not represent as an established cancer therapy.

**Empiric use for acute GI/parasitic symptoms** (session notes, Week 9 case example):
- Patient with Hashimoto's developed acute gastritis and diarrhea for two weeks; GI endoscopy was normal. Initial pantoprazole prescription was suspected to have worsened diarrhea via gut microbiome disruption. Differential diagnosis included a new parasite outbreak, Cyclospora cayetanensis, reported in 20 US states at the time.
- Recommended plan: symptomatic relief with IV hydration and bone broth/collagen-fortified broth; a 5-day empiric course of ivermectin; and an ova-and-parasite stool test, with instructions that the patient could start ivermectin immediately after collecting the stool sample (i.e., empiric treatment did not need to wait for lab confirmation).

## Dosing

> **Dose resolved (2026-07-14, literature review).** The mentorship materials contained two conflicting figures — **0.4 mg/kg/day** (Week 9 slides) and **4.4 mg/kg/day** (Week 7 notes). A multi-source review resolved this in favor of **0.4 mg/kg/day**; the 4.4 figure has no basis and should not be used. Detail below.

- **Taught / working dose: 0.4 mg/kg PO once daily** (empty stomach, away from other food/meds/supplements; peak plasma 2–4 h post-dose). Course-taught duration ranges from a ~5-day empiric course (Week 9 case) to 7–10 days (Week 9 slides) or 1–3 doses (Week 7 parasite context), depending on indication.
  - **Basis (defensible):** 0.4 mg/kg (400 mcg/kg) is a documented, well-tolerated oral dose — the Chosidow *et al.* NEJM 2010 head-lice RCT (n=812) used 400 mcg/kg with low adverse-event discontinuation. The **multi-day integrative parasite pattern** is corroborated by published functional-medicine practice: the Integrative Medicine Center of Western Colorado doses ivermectin **0.4 mg/kg daily** across a multi-day protocol (often stacked with albendazole/mebendazole).
  - **Status:** off-label extended protocol — **not** FDA-labeled. The FDA-approved antiparasitic dose is a **single** ~0.2 mg/kg (200 mcg/kg) for strongyloidiasis / 0.15 mg/kg for onchocerciasis. So 0.4 mg/kg/day is ~2× the standard per-dose magnitude, given over multiple days as a practitioner convention.
- **~~4.4 mg/kg/day~~ — do NOT use. Almost certainly a transcription error.** No FDA label, primary pharmacology study, or established protocol contains a 4.4 mg/kg/day regimen. It exceeds the **highest dose ever formally studied in humans — 2 mg/kg (2,000 mcg/kg, 10× the approved dose)** — by >2×. The 2 mg/kg ceiling was "generally well tolerated with no CNS toxicity" in Guzzo *et al.* 2002 (PMID 12362927); 4.4 mg/kg sits above even that studied ceiling. Most plausible origin: a misread of **0.44 mg/kg**, or a mcg/mg or veterinary per-kg unit confusion. *(The original OneDrive course notes should be corrected at source.)*
- **Safety ceiling & host susceptibility.** ~2 mg/kg is the documented single/short-escalation tolerability ceiling (Guzzo 2002) — not an endorsement of routine high-dose or sustained multi-day use. Critically, the CNS-toxicity threshold is **not fixed**: patients with **ABCB1/MDR1 loss-of-function mutations** or **Loa loa co-infection** can develop severe neurotoxicity (encephalopathy, seizures, coma) **even at standard 150–200 mcg/kg doses** (2026 *J Applied Toxicology* review; Loa loa MDA reports). Any above-standard dosing warrants individualized caution and, where exposure history warrants, Loa loa screening.

**Sources:** FDA STROMECTOL label (accessdata.fda.gov, 050742s026lbl.pdf); Chosidow NEJM 2010 / PMC5133431; Guzzo 2002 PMID 12362927; PMC7449176; imcwc.com parasite protocol; *J Appl Toxicol* 2026 (10.1002/jat.70166). Full reasoning: `docs/knowledge/functional-medicine/` research note, 2026-07-14.

## Drug Interactions

- Avoid co-administration with **curcumin** or **cyclosporine** — both slow ivermectin metabolism.

## Side Effects

- Dizziness, lightheadedness
- Loss of appetite
- Nausea and/or vomiting
- Stomach pain or bloating
- Diarrhea and/or constipation
- Fatigue
- Mild GI upset is common, especially with parasite die-off (session notes)
