# Metabolic Syndrome — Diet & Carbohydrate Framework

Transcription of the low-carb/keto definitions, carb-risk messaging, fats chart, protein/crude-protein graphic, butter graphic, and berberine dosing taught alongside metabolic syndrome in Derek's Spring 2026 mentorship. Context: this is the operational diet prescription used to reverse the insulin resistance described in [metabolic-syndrome-tree.md](metabolic-syndrome-tree.md).

## 1. Low-Carb / Ketogenic Definitions

**Two source graphics give different gram thresholds for the same terms.** Both are transcribed in full below, exactly as presented, with source labeled — do not merge or "correct" one against the other; flag the discrepancy to the prescribing provider.

### 1.1 "LOW CARB DEFINITIONS.JPG" (Joyful Heart Health Care graphic — "Terms to Know")

| Term | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Low carb | < 100 g carbs/day |
| Very low carb | < 50 g carbs/day |
| Very very low carb | < 30 g carbs/day |
| Therapeutic ketogenic eating | < 20 g carbs/day |
| Minimum carbs required per day | **ZERO** |

### 1.2 Mentorship deck slide 19 ("HOW MANY CARBS?" — Joyful Heart Health Care / Society of Metabolic Health Practitioners co-branded graphic)

| Term | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Lower carbs | 50–130 g carb/day |
| Low carb | 30–50 g carb/day |
| Ketogenic | less than 30 g carb/day |
| Therapeutic keto | 20 g carb/day or less |
| Typical (standard) recommendation | above 200 g carb/day |
| Minimum carb intake required to sustain life | **0** |

**Discrepancy note:** the two sources disagree on where "low carb" and "very low carb" fall numerically (e.g., "low carb" is <100 g/day in source 1.1 vs. 30–50 g/day in source 1.2; source 1.2's "lower carbs" 50–130 g/day has no equivalent named tier in source 1.1). Both agree that ketogenic ≈ <30 g/day and therapeutic keto ≈ 20 g/day or less, and both agree the physiological minimum carbohydrate requirement is zero (consistent with the IOM 2005 quote in [metabolic-syndrome-tree.md](metabolic-syndrome-tree.md) §9.2). When prescribing, use the specific gram target agreed with the patient rather than relying on the ambiguous tier label alone.

Evidence quality: these are practitioner/clinic-branded convention definitions (Joyful Heart Health Care, Society of Metabolic Health Practitioners), not a single universally standardized nomenclature — the SMHP's own published "Clinical Guidelines for Therapeutic Carbohydrate Restriction" (v1.3.9, cited in source PDF references) is the more authoritative anchor if a single standard is needed.

## 2. Carb Risk Messaging (CARB RISK.JPG)

Patient-facing graphic, titled "CARBS:" — transcribed verbatim as bullet claims:

- Contain almost zero nutritional value
- Inflame our bodies resulting in swelling and fatigue
- Raise glucose &/or insulin, impacting ALL other internal chemistry/hormones
- That contain fructose, stress the liver even more because the body cannot break down or USE fructose (fruit sugars)
- Consumed in excess, contribute to insulin resistance, diabetes, fatty liver, & heart disease

Evidence quality: patient-education framing (Joyful Heart Health Care), directionally consistent with the insulin-resistance/fatty-liver mechanism described in [metabolic-syndrome-tree.md](metabolic-syndrome-tree.md) §6 and the fatty-liver pathophysiology in course_lesson_notes.md ("liver converts excess glucose into fat (triglycerides) and stores it as glycogen... glycogen stores overflow, causing fat deposits in the liver"), but stated as absolute patient-education claims rather than nuanced clinical statements (e.g., "almost zero nutritional value" does not apply to all carbohydrate sources, e.g., non-starchy vegetables). Use for patient messaging; qualify for provider-level differential nuance.

## 5. Butter (BUTTER.jpg — Joyful Heart Health Care)

- **2 Tbsp provides 28% of the RDA for Vitamin A.**
- Vitamin A is essential for protein absorption.
- Butter is a healthy fat useful for cellular structure in cell membranes.
- **Provides 9 kcal/g of energy, more than double that of carbohydrates** (carbohydrate = ~4 kcal/g, standard nutrition science; the "more than double" framing on the graphic is consistent with fat's known 9 kcal/g vs. carbohydrate's 4 kcal/g).
- Gets its name from **hydroxybutyrate** — described on the graphic as "molecule of healing & tissue repair." **[source unclear]** — this is a folk-etymology claim on the patient graphic; butyrate (not "hydroxybutyrate") is the short-chain fatty acid classically associated with colonocyte energy and gut healing, and the etymological claim about butter's name is not verified here. Transcribed as presented; do not repeat the etymology claim as fact without independent verification.
- Butter is described as **the major energy source for GI mucous membranes and other cells**, also thought to aid in healing "leaky gut."

Evidence quality: patient-education graphic. The kcal/g and Vitamin A content figures are standard nutrition facts. The butyrate/gut-healing and "leaky gut" claims reflect the short-chain-fatty-acid literature on butyrate (a related but distinct compound from butter's fat content) and should be treated as plausible mechanistic framing rather than a directly cited peer-reviewed claim about butter itself.

## 6. Berberine / Dihydroberberine for Insulin Resistance

Two dosing references appear in course_lesson_notes.md, from different mentorship sessions — both transcribed as given:

| Context | Dosing |
|---|---|
| Case study (41 y/o post-hysterectomy, IR + inflammation) — "Insulin Support" | **Berberine 1000 mg BID**, OR **dihydroberberine 200 mg/day** |
| Supplement protocol reference list — "Insulin Resistance" section | **Inositol 2,000–4,000 mg/day powder** (lowers testosterone in women, not men — noted as a female-specific effect); **Berberine: Zymogen Dihydro Berberine 200–400 mg, 1–2x/day** |
| Gut health session — SIBO with diarrhea | Berberine listed as an alternative to Rifaximin for SIBO with diarrhea (not IR-specific dosing given) |
| Metformin side-effect management | For dizziness/vision issues from metformin (likely rapid glucose drop): reduce metformin dose (e.g., 1000 mg BID → 500 mg BID) **or switch to berberine** |

**Practical takeaway:** dihydroberberine (the more bioavailable form) is dosed lower than plain berberine — 200–400 mg 1–2x/day for dihydroberberine vs. 1000 mg BID for standard berberine — consistent with dihydroberberine's higher absorption. Brand specifically named in the mentorship materials: **Zymogen Dihydro Berberine**.

Evidence quality: dosing is practitioner clinical convention from the mentorship (Jenni Gallagher, APRN), not sourced to a specific RCT dosing trial in the provided materials. Berberine's insulin-sensitizing mechanism (AMPK activation) is independently well-supported in the broader peer-reviewed literature, but the specific mg/day targets above should be treated as this practitioner's clinical protocol rather than a guideline-derived dose. A patient-education article is referenced but not included in source files: "https://joyfulhearthousecalls.com/berberine-worth-it-or-mythical-unicorn/".
