## 1. Case-review pearls (live Q&A, not in KB)

### 1.1 Adrenal insufficiency + hypogonadism (case "Amy Swanson")
- Sequencing hormone replacement in a severely hormone-depleted patient: start **progesterone first** (calming, lets patient feel better before adding more) → in **6–8 weeks add estradiol** → testosterone optional/added later if at all. Do not start all three at once — "overwhelming to the system"; compares it to introducing one new food at a time to an infant. [08:39–09:20]
- Foundational nutrient support before/alongside hormones for an adrenally-exhausted, malnourished patient: **sodium, protein, vitamin D + cofactors** first. [08:39–08:45]
- If ferritin is low/borderline (~45 ⚠️, not found in slides — remains unverified) and serum cortisol is also low, **liothyronine (T3) must be added very cautiously and slowly** — patient may not tolerate much T3 without adrenal support first. May need progesterone/pregnenolone first so adrenals can "adapt and adjust" before adding thyroid. [09:34–10:16]

### 1.2 Acute iron-deficiency bridging (case "Kimberly," ferritin 5, awaiting infusion)
- Erythropoiesis-based patient counseling: oral iron **cannot join red blood cells already in circulation** — ferritin/iron must be incorporated *during* erythropoiesis (new RBC production), so correction takes a genuinely long time (⚠️ "100-ish days" mentioned for the rebirth/replacement process — not found in slides — remains unverified). [17:00–17:14, 21:xx region]
- Practical bridge-to-infusion protocol: **iron bisglycinate** (better absorbed than prescription or standard OTC forms), split into ~**100 mg/day total, never more than ~50 mg per single dose**; pair with **vitamin C throughout the day**. [17:26–18:13, 18:04]
- Low vitamin D → high hepcidin → hepcidin blocks iron metabolism/absorption — explicit mechanistic chain given as the reason vitamin D + cofactors must be taken *with* iron. [18:22–18:30]
- Practical dosing-time pearl: **give vitamin D (with cofactors) at lunch or dinner, not breakfast** — patients typically don't eat enough fat at breakfast (especially if fasting), and D needs to be taken mid-fatty-meal for absorption. [14:10–14:22]
- Prescription iron forms cause **more constipation/GI upset** than bisglycinate; an already-purchased OTC product (⚠️ transcribed as "acupur" — likely a garbled iron brand name, not found in slides — remains unverified) was judged "pretty useless" but reasonable to finish before switching. [18:36–18:58]
- Genetic carrier vs. disease distinction for hemochromatosis: with iron saturation ⚠️("iron side of 10") (not found in slides — remains unverified), TIBC 29, ferritin 5, this does **not** fit typical hemochromatosis (disease) — presenter suspected a **single allele (carrier)**, not two (disease). Recommended pulling hematology records to confirm. [15:09–15:41]
- **Roux-en-Y gastric bypass (2021)** flagged as the likely driver of a ferritin trend from ⚠️"12" (March) (not found in slides — remains unverified) down to "5" (recent) in this patient — bariatric surgery as an iron-absorption risk factor not otherwise discussed in the KB. [15:59–16:17]

### 1.3 Thyroid dosing pearls (case "Amy," post-thyroidectomy)
- Reverse T3 of ⚠️18 (not found in slides — remains unverified) flagged as **not good** / a reason to consider splitting into levothyroxine + liothyronine rather than continuing desiccated thyroid alone — presented as a live judgment call, not a hard rule. [19:01–19:25]
- **Desiccated thyroid (Armour, NP Thyroid, etc.) only comes in multiples of 30 mg** (30/60/90/120…) — useful for validating what a patient reports taking. [19:54–20:00]
- **Never split a desiccated-thyroid dose into twice daily** — the T4 portion of a second daily dose will be largely unabsorbed because of food/snacking throughout the day (unless the patient fasts 4+ hours around dosing). Take desiccated thyroid **once daily**. [20:28–20:53]
- Practical conversion approach when down-titrating desiccated thyroid and adding liothyronine: drop desiccated dose (example: 120 mg/day → ~90 mg/day), make up the T3 difference immediately with liothyronine, then **titrate liothyronine up by 5 mcg every 3 weeks**. References "a chart online" / comparison-of-doses chart posted previously in the course for this conversion math. [20:57–21:11]
- **TPO antibody does not need to be redrawn more than about once a year** — it doesn't meaningfully change over months (explicitly: redrawing 5–6 months later was called unnecessary). [35:57–36:18]

### 1.4 37-year-old male, fatigue/insulin resistance case
- Insurance/diagnosis-coding pearl for getting functional labs covered (not in KB at all): use **R53.83 (other malaise and fatigue)**⚠️ (not found in slides — remains unverified), plus add **"encounter for screening for cardiovascular disorders"** and **"encounter for screening for nutritional disorder"** as secondary codes — these two screening codes were described as reliably unlocking coverage for markers like homocysteine, uric acid, ferritin, folate. General rule: **code every symptom the patient has** (dry skin, brain fog, irregular periods, syncope, chest pain, etc.) rather than just 2–3 diagnoses, because insurers look for reasons to deny. Ferritin specifically was noted as frequently denied even when iron is approved, despite being a ~$3 test. [32:03–35:13]
- Patient-communication technique for protein non-compliance: avoid saying "your labs aren't improving because you don't eat enough protein" (blaming); instead say **"I can't tell from these labs if it's consumption or absorption"** — gives the patient a face-saving "out" while still putting the onus on them to investigate. Explicitly framed as a lesson learned from early-career mistakes. [37:34–38:22]
- For this patient's insulin resistance: considered **inositol** + **omega-3s** (anti-inflammatory, supports cell-membrane fatty-acid content) as the starting supplement pair — omega-3 pairing with inositol for IR is not in the existing KB berberine/inositol section. [32:30–32:47]
- Considered **DHEA ⚠️("our VHEA plus DHEA" — garbled product/compound name, not found in slides — remains unverified) plus pregnenolone** for a stressed, travel-heavy patient to support both adrenal and hormone function — distinct combination product/compounding reference not currently in KB. [41:03–41:20]
- Diagnostic questioning pearl: for patients with "not terrible" labs but fatigue, **first ask about sleep and stress** before escalating workup — insulin resistance impairs ATP/energy production because insulin "bullies" other molecules when it can't move glucose normally. Consider adding a **cortisol level** to the next draw for stressed/traveling patients. [39:38–40:21]

## 2. Insulin-resistance / metabolic-syndrome pearls not in KB

- **Fasting insulin can be "gamed"** by a patient who has learned that intermittent fasting or low-carb changes lower insulin — a low fasting insulin (e.g., 2.4) doesn't rule out significant insulin resistance if the patient fasted strategically before the draw. This is why **SHBG, DHEA-S, triglycerides, fasting glucose, and A1c must be triangulated** rather than trusting fasting insulin alone. [56:07–57:03]
- Related nuance: even when a newly-improved insulin level looks good, the **downstream impact/damage from prior insulin resistance may not have "caught up" yet** — full marker normalization can take months to 1–2 years even after insulin itself falls. [62:45–63:16]
- **"Path of least resistance" teaching analogy** for why low-carb/keto does NOT reduce metabolic flexibility (addresses a common patient/provider myth): carbohydrates break down to glucose fastest ("path of least resistance"); in their absence, protein converts to glucose next; fat-to-glucose conversion is the slowest/most effortful path. The body will always metabolize whatever is present via the easiest path first — meaning a long-term keto/carnivore eater who reintroduces fruit will still process it to glucose "first, always and forever." Presented as a direct rebuttal to the "loss of metabolic flexibility" objection. [78:47–81:47]
- **Uric acid target**: >5 mg/dL = inflammation; goal ≤6 "even for gout" per **Dr. David Perlmutter** (named source, not previously cited in KB). [75:47–76:05]
- **WBC goal refined to 6.5–7.0 or less** (KB currently only has the upper "flag" threshold of >7.0; this session adds the explicit goal range). [75:47–76:09]
- Charting/documentation technique: write lab values as a **fraction over the reference-range limit** (e.g., globulin "1.6/3" or ferritin "398/150 (high)") directly on the worksheet, specifically for values whose normal range you don't have memorized (DHEA-S given as personal example) — saves time on future visits and avoids re-Googling ranges. [72:44–74:02]
- **DHEA-S must be read against its age/sex-specific range midpoint, not an absolute number.** Concrete worked numbers given (⚠️ not found in slides — remains unverified): childbearing-age women's range runs up to ~400–430, so **goal ≈ 200–210** (middle of range); menopausal women's range upper limit ≈ 200–250, so a level of 195 in that group is judged **not good** (too close to the top, not mid-range). [71:09–72:53]
- Vitamin D **half-life ≈ 2 weeks**: draw levels after the patient has been off their D supplement for **at least ~1 week** for a less-skewed result; conversely — do **not** stop a patient's vitamin D just because a level looks good ("that's why it's good — they're on it; if you stop it, they'll tank again"), which the speaker contrasts with mainstream practice. [30:04–30:29]
- **Home blood-pressure protocol** (patient-education technique, not in KB): don't trust in-office BP (visit-anxiety artifact); don't check BP in the **first 1–2 hours after waking** (cortisol surge + pre-waking free-T3 release both physiologically raise BP during this window); check **late afternoon after 30–40 minutes of rest**, arm at or above heart level (dependent positioning falsely lowers the reading). For engagement, have patients **graph BP on graph paper as dots** rather than tracking raw numbers — "dots are easy," numbers are hard for patients to trend. Check only ~2x/week, not daily — except when prescribing liothyronine (T3), which raises BP and needs closer home monitoring for safety. [65:18–69:23, 913–915]

## 4. Pregnancy & pediatric/Type 1 diabetes notes (not previously in KB)

Specific targets given: **~2 tsp salt/day spread across the day** (not as one bolus — large single salt-water dose used as a natural laxative); **2 tbsp butter per plate, 2 plates/day (~50 g fat total)**; **water ~2–3 quarts (~80 oz)/day**, more in hot weather. [96:44–98:34]

## 5. Hormone-therapy communication & cancer-risk nuance (not previously in KB)

- **Estrogen dominance is framed as not causally driving most cancer, but may accelerate/feed growth of already-present hormone-sensitive tumors** — rationale given: estrogen's physiologic role is to proliferate endometrial tissue ("make tissue grow"), and cancer is by definition uncontrolled tissue growth. Presented explicitly as the functional-medicine-space interpretation, not settled oncology consensus. [111:03–112:16]
- Hormone-positive cancer history does **not automatically rule out future hormone therapy** once the cancer is treated — speaker describes patients with breast-cancer history who elect to continue estrogen cream post-treatment for brain/heart/bone protection, framed as informed patient choice. [112:00–112:25]
- Bioidentical-vs-synthetic framing used specifically to counter the Women's Health Initiative (WHI) legacy fear in older patients: explain that **synthetic hormones (including birth-control-type estrogens) caused the historically-reported problems**, whereas bioidentical hormones are molecularly identical to what the ovaries/adrenals produce and function the same way the body's own hormones do. [109:49–110:19]
- Patient-communication pacing pearl: **introduce hormone education gradually across multiple visits** rather than all at once — overwhelming a skeptical patient with a strong opening stance ("I believe hormones work and this is what we're doing") risks the patient disengaging entirely; "chip away... every visit a little bit more information." [117:23–118:26 region / ~1573–1578]
- "Never too old for hormones" — explicitly stated no age cutoff (example given: a patient in their 80s), and no time-since-hysterectomy cutoff, for considering hormone therapy for bone/brain/cognition/heart protection. [1542–1547]
