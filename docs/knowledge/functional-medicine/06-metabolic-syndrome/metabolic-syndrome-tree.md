# Metabolic Syndrome — The Tree Model, Diagnostic Criteria & Insulin Resistance Physiology

Transcription of the "MET SYNDROME TREE" graphic (Phoenixville Nutrition Guide) plus the diagnostic-criteria and insulin-resistance content from Jenni Gallagher APRN's "Metabolic Syndrome and Insulin Resistance: A Functional Perspective" mentorship deck. Context: this is the conceptual map Derek uses to explain to patients why obesity is a downstream symptom, not the root cause.

## 1. The Tree Model (transcribed from MET SYNDROME TREE.jpg)

Source graphic: "Metabolic Syndrome & Beyond," Phoenixville Nutrition Guide. Structure is root → trunk → branches, with a "fertilizer" input feeding the roots.

**Headline fact on the graphic:** "FACT: Obesity doesn't cause metabolic syndrome. It's only one symptom of metabolic syndrome."

| Tree part | Element(s) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Fertilizer** (feeds the roots) | Starch, grain & sugars — depicted as beer, pretzels, cookies, French fries, a soda cup, cereal/rice bowl, pizza, bread, banana | The graphic includes an explicit "STOP FERTILIZING TREE" stop-sign icon pointing at these inputs |
| **Roots** | Insulin resistance in liver and muscle | The foundational lesion — everything above depends on this |
| **Trunk** | High insulin levels (hyperinsulinemia) | Insulin resistance at the roots forces compensatory hyperinsulinemia up the trunk |
| **Lower branches (left)** | Weight gain & abdominal obesity → prediabetes | Arrow shows prediabetes as a direct consequence, not a separate starting point |
| **Lower branches (right)** | Fatty liver | |
| **Mid branches (right)** | Type 2 diabetes & complications; PCOS | |
| **Mid branches (left)** | High blood pressure | |
| **Upper branches (right)** | High triglycerides, low HDL | |
| **Upper branches (left)** | Coronary artery disease | |
| **Crown (top)** | Cancer; dementia | Depicted as the furthest downstream / most severe consequences, growing out of a cloud above the tree |

**Clinical reading:** the graphic's logic is strictly hierarchical — insulin resistance (roots) is fed by dietary starch/grain/sugar (fertilizer), produces hyperinsulinemia (trunk), which branches into the clinically-recognized "symptoms" of metabolic syndrome (weight gain, prediabetes, fatty liver, hypertension, dyslipidemia, T2DM, PCOS), which in turn cascade into the highest-severity downstream diseases (coronary artery disease, cancer, dementia). Treatment logic follows the same hierarchy in reverse: stop fertilizing (carb restriction) before expecting the crown-level diseases to resolve.

## 2. ATP III / NCEP Diagnostic Criteria

Formal diagnosis requires **3 of the following 5** (per the mentorship slide, sourced to ATP III/NCEP):

| Criterion | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Elevated waist circumference | ≥ 40 inches (102 cm) men; ≥ 35 inches (88 cm) women |
| Hypertriglyceridemia | ≥ 150 mg/dL (1.7 mmol/L), or on medication for it |
| Reduced HDL cholesterol | < 40 mg/dL (1.0 mmol/L) men; < 50 mg/dL (1.3 mmol/L) women, or on medication for it |
| Hypertension | ≥ 130/85 mmHg, or on medication for it |
| Elevated fasting glucose | ≥ 100 mg/dL (5.6 mmol/L), or on medication for it |

Evidence quality: consensus clinical criteria (ATP III/NCEP), widely used but conservative relative to the functional-medicine "early identification" thresholds below.

## 3. Insulin Resistance — Core Definition

"A condition where the body's cells become less responsive to insulin, leading to a cascade of metabolic changes that can impact overall health."

Key mechanistic aspects taught:
1. **Reduced cellular response to insulin** — muscle, fat, and liver cells fail to take up glucose effectively despite insulin signaling.
2. **Increased insulin production** — the pancreas compensates by secreting more insulin to overcome cellular resistance (this is the hyperinsulinemia = "trunk" of the tree).
3. **Glucose intolerance → progression to diabetes** — if the pancreas can no longer compensate, fasting/postprandial glucose rises and T2DM develops.

Downstream systemic effects taught as connected to IR:
- Thyroid dysfunction
- Rising inflammation markers: CRP-hs, uric acid, WBC, homocysteine
- Reproductive hormone changes

## 4. Quantitative Markers of Insulin Resistance

| Marker | Definition / goal | Source note |
|---|---|---|
| **HOMA-IR** | Calculated from fasting glucose and insulin. Goal < 1; **> 1.9 = insulin resistant** | Clinical convention taught in mentorship |
| **GDR (Glucose Disposal Rate)** | Obtained via euglycemic hyperinsulinemic clamp studies — gold standard for insulin sensitivity, but impractical clinically (research tool) | |
| **Clinical indicators** | High blood pressure, elevated fasting glucose, increased waist circumference | |

### Kraft Insulin-Response Patterns (Dr. Joseph R. Kraft)

Referenced via video (jgerbermd.com) summarizing Kraft's oral glucose tolerance testing with insulin assay across **14,384 patients**:

- **More than 75% of people with "normal" glucose tolerance results failed Kraft's test** — i.e., showed an abnormal insulin response despite normal glucose. The slide's framing: "They were NOT ok."
- Four insulin response patterns identified:
  - **Pattern 1 — Euinsulin, non-diabetic** (normal)
  - **Pattern 2 — Hyperinsulin, "diabetes in situ"**
  - **Pattern 3 — Hyperinsulin, "diabetes in situ"**
  - **Pattern 4 — Hyperinsulin, "diabetes in situ"**
- Slide's verbatim framing of patterns 2–4: **"These people are Diabetic. Period."**

Evidence quality: Kraft's dataset is large (N=14,384) but is retrospective clinical data, not a peer-reviewed RCT; treat the "diabetic, period" framing as strong clinical opinion built on a large observational base, not established diagnostic consensus. Cited secondary source: DiNicolantonio et al. 2017, *Open Heart* — "Postprandial insulin assay as the earliest biomarker for diagnosing pre-diabetes, type 2 diabetes and increased cardiovascular risk."

## 5. Symptoms of IR & Metabolic Syndrome (patient-facing checklist)

- Fatigue
- Cravings for carbs/sugar
- Increased hunger despite eating; poor appetite control
- Weight gain / difficulty losing weight
- Elevated FBS, A1c, and/or insulin levels
- Acanthosis nigricans (dark, velvety skin patches)
- Elevated triglycerides & low HDL
- Brain fog
- Hypertension
- PCOS (in women)
- Facial/chest hair (women)
- Scalp hair thinning
- Skin tags, especially neck and armpits

## 6. Functional Medicine "Connections" — Insulin as Hormonal Disruptor

Per the mentorship's FM-lens framing (labeled explicitly as the functional medicine interpretation, not conventional endocrinology consensus):

- Insulin disrupts glucose homeostasis and increases adiposity, especially **visceral fat**, which further worsens metabolic dysfunction.
- Insulin is characterized as a **"big bully"**:
  - Drives **low testosterone in many men**
  - Drives **estrogen dominance in men and women**
  - Drives **estrogen down significantly in some menopausal women**
  - Interacts with thyroid dysfunction, raising glucose
- Broader FM framing: Carbs → Glucose → Insulin. "Insulin pushes inflammation" and "insulin drives hormone imbalances," including low T in males, high-or-low T in females, estrogen dominance in many females, the menopausal estrogen drop, and PMS/PMDD/endometriosis in many childbearing-age women.
- Mechanistic reference cited for the inflammation link: de Luca & Olefsky (2008), *FEBS Letters* — direct interaction of insulin signaling (IRS/PI3K/Akt) and inflammatory pathways (TLRs, NF-κB, JNK) at the cellular level; free fatty acids (FFAs) and ER stress both feed into inflammatory gene expression that impairs insulin signaling. This is peer-reviewed, established molecular biology — the strongest-evidence item in this section.

Evidence quality: the mechanistic insulin-inflammation crosstalk (de Luca & Olefsky) is peer-reviewed and well-established. The specific "insulin as big bully driving sex-hormone imbalance" claims are FM clinical framing supported by cited correlational literature (Ottarsdottir 2018; Cheung 2015; Meyer 2011; Rae 2025 blog) — reasonable associations, not proven causal mechanisms in every direction claimed.

## 7. Lifestyle Drivers of Insulin Resistance

- High intake of processed foods
- High intake of seed oils: canola/rapeseed, vegetable, corn
- High carbohydrate intake — grains: wheat, corn, rice, oats, barley, rye, millet
- Pseudograins: amaranth, quinoa, buckwheat, triticum, durum wheat
- Sedentary lifestyle
- Toxic exposures

## 8. Early Identification Thresholds (functional-medicine intervention level, stricter than ATP III)

These are the mentorship's own "catch it earlier than conventional criteria" thresholds — explicitly practitioner-level clinical convention, not a validated diagnostic standard.

| Marker | Threshold flagged as early IR/MetSynd |
|---|---|
| Blood pressure | > 135/85 (home readings preferred over office values) |
| Waist circumference | Elevated (specific FM cutoff not given beyond ATP III values above) |
| Insulin | Above 5 mcU/mL |
| A1c | Above 5.2% |
| Fasting blood sugar (FBS) | Trending upward, or > 100 mg/dL |
| Triglycerides | Above 90–100 mg/dL |
| HDL | Lower than 50 mg/dL |
| DHEA-S | Upper 1/3 or lower 1/3 of reference range |
| Testosterone (men) | Lower than 400 ng/dL |
| Testosterone vs. estradiol (women) | Testosterone levels higher than estradiol levels |
| Estradiol (women) | Higher than 200 |
| Progesterone | Lower than 5.0 ng/dL |
| Free T3 | Lower than 2.8 ng/dL |
| CRP-hs | > 1.0 mg/L |

| WBC | Higher than 7.0 cells/µL |

## 9. Interventions to Slow/Reverse Insulin Resistance

### 9.1 Macronutrient framing — carbohydrate restriction over fat restriction

Mentorship slide title: **"11 to 1, in favor of carbohydrate restriction."** Framing (quoted from slide): "Given that we're going to either restrict either dietary carbohydrate or fat, which is most likely, based on research, to have the greatest impact on lowering our risk of cardiovascular disease? Hmmmm, 11-1… But that 1 is LDL cholesterol, and we KNOW that's BAD, right?" (rhetorical — the slide's point is that LDL concentration is the only risk marker where low-fat "wins," while low-carbohydrate is favored on 11 other markers.)

Evidence quality: this comparison table is a secondary/advocacy graphic (Ivor Cummins/Grass Based Health), not a primary systematic review — directionally consistent with cited peer-reviewed sources (Volek et al. 2008, *Progress in Lipid Research*) but should be presented to patients as illustrative, not as a formal meta-analysis result.

### 9.2 Foundational optimization targets (named co-interventions)

- Optimize Vitamin D
- Optimize iron & ferritin
- Optimize protein levels (both intake and absorption)
- Optimize thyroid levels
- Optimize reproductive hormones

Institute of Medicine (2005) is quoted directly on the mentorship slide: *"The lower limit of dietary carbohydrate compatible with life apparently is zero, provided that adequate amounts of protein and fat are consumed."* (IOM, Dietary Reference Intakes for Energy, Carbohydrate, Fiber, Fat, Fatty Acids, Cholesterol, Protein, and Amino Acids, Chapter 6.) This is used in the curriculum to justify that no minimum carbohydrate intake is physiologically required — see [diet-carb-framework.md](diet-carb-framework.md) for the carb-gram tier definitions that follow from this framing.

## 10. Case Example from course_lesson_notes.md (worked patient, IR + inflammation)

41 y/o post-hysterectomy (no ovaries), presenting with high inflammation and insulin resistance:

| Category | Findings |
|---|---|
| Inflammation | CRP 6.0 (high), Homocysteine 12.1 (high), WBC 10.6 (high) |
| Insulin resistance | Fasting glucose 110, Triglycerides 176 (high), HDL 28 (low) |
| Hormones | Estradiol 51, Testosterone 11 (low), DHEA-S 78 (very low) |
| Thyroid | TSH 2.0, Free T4 1.0, Free T3 3.6 |
| Iron | Iron 87, Ferritin 67 |

**Root cause assigned:** inflammation and insulin resistance driving the metabolic dysfunction; low HDL and DHEA-S indicate insufficient cholesterol raw material for hormone synthesis; TSH 2.0 flagged suboptimal (suspect iodine deficiency) despite being within conventional "normal" range.

**Initial plan:** anti-inflammatory, grain-free, high-protein (red meat) diet; Osteopiex (D3/K2/Mg/Boron); high-dose vitamin C; low-dose iodine (<300 mcg/day); **berberine 1000 mg BID or dihydroberberine 200 mg/day** for insulin support (see [diet-carb-framework.md](diet-carb-framework.md) §Berberine for full dosing detail and a second, slightly different dosing reference from elsewhere in the same course notes); progesterone for sleep/irritability; DHEA 5–10 mg/day titrated to 20–30 mg. Follow-up labs at 4–5 months.

## 11. Note on "PCOS" Renaming

Course notes state: "The name change to 'Metabolic Ovarian Syndrome' highlights the metabolic root cause (insulin resistance) over the ovarian symptoms" — i.e., the mentorship frames PCOS as fundamentally a manifestation of insulin resistance rather than a primary ovarian disorder, consistent with the tree model's placement of PCOS as a mid-branch consequence of the hyperinsulinemia trunk.
