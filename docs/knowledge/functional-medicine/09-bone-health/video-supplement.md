## 1. Protein math and practical dosing (supports existing "protein/globulin" pillar)

- **Ounce-to-gram conversion rule of thumb:** to estimate ounces of meat from a target gram-of-protein number (or vice versa), divide grams of protein by 7. One egg ≈ 6.6–7 g protein. [29:19]–[30:25] ⚠️ (numbers given somewhat loosely/conversationally — verify against any handout) (not found in slides — remains unverified)
- **150 g/day protein is over-dosing for most metabolically dysfunctional adults.** This is presented as a common social-media/influencer target (from keto/carnivore-adjacent "diet gurus") that most patients with metabolic dysfunction don't need and can't realistically eat (≈21 oz of meat/day). Excess protein the body can't use undergoes gluconeogenesis. [20:50]–[23:03]
- **Relative conversion speed to glucose:** carbohydrate → glucose is roughly a 2-hour cycle (why mainstream advice says "eat every ~2 hours"); protein → glucose via gluconeogenesis is roughly a 4–5 hour cycle. Type 1 diabetic patients reportedly see measurable glucose spikes from high-protein meals via this slower pathway. [25:36]–[26:07]
- **Pediatric exception:** children/growing patients need proportionally more protein than adults because it's used for growth, not shunted into gluconeogenesis — don't apply adult protein-restriction logic to kids (relevant for T1D pediatric patients specifically called out). [26:18]–[26:52]
- **Weekly-average framing for patients:** teach patients to average protein intake over the week rather than hit a rigid daily number — "the body runs on a forever clock, not a 24-hour clock." Instructor's own example target ≈61 g/day at 5'7" (~8.5 oz meat/day), tracked via Chronometer. [33:44]–[34:55]
- Instructor has a protein-needs calculator/article on her site (joyfulhearthousecalls.com) built from actuarial-science life-sustaining protein data; ideal-body-weight-based targets are adjusted ±5–10 g by frame size. [28:13]–[29:17]
