# Bone Health

Functional-medicine approach to osteopenia/osteoporosis prevention: bone health as a systemic (not purely calcium) issue, the hormone replacement therapy (HRT)/SERM evidence base as taught in mentorship, and the core vitamin D3 / vitamin K2 / magnesium / boron bone-support stack. Built to train Derek's Atlas Medicine AI assistant and for use with PV Medispa & Weight Loss patients.

## Scope

This is a lean reference topic (README + one reference file), covering:
- Bone health as a systemic issue — protein/globulin status, thyroid function, inflammation control, and estrogen balance, not just calcium intake
- DEXA T-score interpretation and calcium supplementation risk/benefit as taught
- Hormone replacement therapy (HRT) and selective estrogen receptor modulators (SERMs): the evidence base for bone density and fracture outcomes, including the AHRQ Systematic Evidence Review this course cites and how it relates to the Women's Health Initiative (WHI)
- The D3 / K2 / magnesium / boron bone-support stack, including magnesium subtype selection and vitamin D cofactor requirements
- Osteopiex, the branded D3/K2/Mg/Boron combination product referenced in the course

It does **not** cover: bisphosphonates/denosumab/teriparatide pharmacology, fall-risk/balance programs, pediatric bone health, or a full current (post-2002) osteoporosis pharmacotherapy literature review. Supplement this topic with current guideline sources (AACE, ACOG, NAMS, USPSTF) before making treatment decisions.

## Key Takeaways

- Bone health is systemic, not just calcium: it depends on adequate protein (globulins specifically, not albumin), estrogen balance, thyroid function, and inflammation control.
- Excess calcium supplementation (>600 mg/day) is linked to atherosclerosis and does not reduce fracture rates or mortality; prioritize dietary calcium, and if supplementing use low doses (100–200 mg BID).
- DEXA T-score categories: Normal > -1.0; Osteopenia -1.0 to -2.5; Osteoporosis ≤ -2.5. Osteopenia should be treated as a serious warning sign, not a benign finding.
- As taught in mentorship: "the Women's Health Initiative (WHI) data supports estrogen's role in improving bone density and reducing fractures." The course-provided background PDF is a pre-WHI AHRQ systematic evidence review (2002), not the WHI trial report itself — see the sourcing note in bone-health-whi.md before citing WHI numbers to patients or colleagues.
- Vitamin D "cannot work on its own" — it needs magnesium, vitamin K2, iodine, and fatty food for absorption/utilization; optimal serum 25-OH-D is 60–80 ng/mL per the course's functional ranges.
- Magnesium form selection matters clinically: glycinate (absorption, neuropathy, leaky gut), malate (energy, fibromyalgia/muscle pain), threonate (crosses blood-brain barrier, anxiety/PTSD/seizures), taurate (cardiac, mitochondrial), sulfate (topical/Epsom salt), and chloride/citrate/oxide (laxative/detox use only, poor systemic absorption).
- Osteopiex (D3/K2/Mg/Boron combination) is referenced in the course as a bone-support supplement; the mentorship materials describe composition but do not specify per-dose amounts — treat exact dosing as [source unclear] and confirm against the product label before prescribing.

## When to Use

- Patient presents with osteopenia or osteoporosis on DEXA, or has risk factors (postmenopausal, early/surgical menopause, low estrogen, family history, chronic steroid use, low protein/globulin status)
- Deciding whether to discuss HRT or SERMs (raloxifene, tamoxifen) for bone protection and need the underlying evidence quality/context for that conversation
- Building a bone-support supplement stack and need dosing/form guidance for magnesium and vitamin D cofactors
- Patient is calcium-supplementing above 600 mg/day and needs counseling on cardiovascular risk without added fracture benefit
- Differentiating osteopenia (early warning, actionable) from osteoporosis (established disease) and setting monitoring/retest intervals
- Interpreting a DEXA T-score for a patient or explaining what "normal," "osteopenia," and "osteoporosis" mean functionally

## Clinical Disclaimers

1. This is a lean reference topic. It summarizes course teaching points plus one historical systematic evidence review (AHRQ Systematic Evidence Review #12, Nelson HD, August 2002) on HRT/SERMs and bone density/fracture outcomes. It is **not** a substitute for current osteoporosis screening/treatment guidelines.
2. The source PDF titled "bone health and the WHI results.pdf" is the AHRQ pre-WHI evidence review, not a WHI trial publication — see the sourcing distinction explained in bone-health-whi.md before representing its numbers as "WHI results" to a patient or in documentation.
3. HRT/SERM decisions require individualized risk assessment (breast cancer, venous thromboembolism, cardiovascular disease, endometrial cancer) that is outside the scope of this bone-focused file — see the AHRQ evidence review's Figure 2 (adverse effects framework) referenced in bone-health-whi.md for the categories to screen before recommending.
4. Osteopiex dosing was not specified in the available course materials; confirm exact label dosing before prescribing or recommending.
