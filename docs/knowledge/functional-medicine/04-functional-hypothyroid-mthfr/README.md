# Functional Hypothyroid Management & MTHFR — Overview

Functional-medicine approach to hypothyroid/Hashimoto's workup and treatment (full thyroid panel, T4→T3 conversion, reverse T3 as the "brake pedal," iodine dosing caution, liothyronine titration and cardiac safety) paired with the MTHFR/methylation/homocysteine biology the mentorship teaches alongside it.

The mentorship teaches these together for two reasons documented in the source material: (1) the PPT states directly that "MTHFR has some impact" on the FT4→FT3 conversion pathway, and (2) both modules share a common lab marker — elevated homocysteine and elevated reverse T3 are each taught as markers of underlying inflammation, and both are addressed with the same dietary/anti-inflammatory foundation (grain elimination, seed-oil elimination, high-dose vitamin C, methylated B-vitamins). ## Key Takeaways

- **Full panel required, not TSH alone:** TSH, Free T4, Free T3, Reverse T3, TgAb, and TPO on initial workup; follow-up labs can usually drop to FT3/FT4/RT3. Free T3 is the only thyroid hormone that enters the cell for energy production — TSH and Free T4 cannot.
- **Optimal Free T3 target is ~3.5–4.2 ng/dL** (top of range for most patients while on liothyronine, ~3.5 for patients over 65); **optimal Reverse T3 is 10–12 ng/dL on treatment** (≤15 ng/dL unmedicated). RT3 is the "brake pedal" — RT3 >15 signals a high likelihood of poor T4→T3 conversion and the need for T3 (liothyronine) therapy; the source's separate video-summary uses a tighter >12 action threshold for reducing T4 dose — both figures are preserved as taught rather than merged.
- **Iodine dosing caution:** the course explicitly says to "never recommend HIGH doses of iodine" for thyroid support. Figures taught across sessions range 150–300 mcg/day for thyroid purposes (one case specifies **<300 mcg/day**); the 1–3 mg/day higher-dose range is reserved for a different, non-thyroid indication (BPH/fibrocystic breast disease), not routine hypothyroid/Hashimoto's management.
- **Ferritin is a hard safety gate before any T3 medication:** liothyronine, NP thyroid, and Armour must not be started unless ferritin is above 45 ng/mL — T3 is a cardiac stimulant and low ferritin independently carries cardiac risk; the combination risks palpitations and tachyarrhythmia. A documented case shows new-onset AFib in a severely ferritin-deficient Hashimoto's patient.
- **MTHFR is inferred, not directly genotyped, in this mentorship's protocol:** the taught lab clues are elevated homocysteine (goal 5–8, flagged >8–9) and unexpectedly high B12 (>2000). The response is the same regardless of confirmed genotype — use methylated B-vitamin forms (methylfolate, methylcobalamin) and avoid cyanocobalamin.
- **Liothyronine carries real cardiac risk and requires structured monitoring:** dose to mimic the body's natural AM-high/PM-low FT3 release pattern, titrate slowly (1 tab/day, increase every 2–3 weeks), monitor HR/BP twice weekly with graphing, manage via in-person visits (not phone calls) every 8–10 weeks while titrating, and continue lifetime follow-up at 6 months or less once stable.

## When to Use This

Reference this topic when: interpreting a thyroid panel that shows "normal" TSH but persistent hypothyroid-pattern symptoms; deciding whether a patient needs T4 monotherapy, NDT, or added liothyronine based on their FT3/RT3 relationship; before prescribing or titrating liothyronine (check the ferritin gate and monitoring protocol first); working up a patient with elevated homocysteine or unexplained high B12 where MTHFR-linked methylation dysfunction is suspected; managing a Hashimoto's patient where antibody-driven inflammation needs a dietary and supplement protocol layered on top of standard thyroid dosing; or when using the JHHC symptom questionnaire for intake or longitudinal symptom tracking.

## Clinical Disclaimers

1. This is a functional-medicine framework taught by a single practitioner mentorship program (Joyful Heart Institute), corroborated against practitioner-advocacy books/blogs (Paul Robinson, Isabella Wentz, Westin Childs, Stop the Thyroid Madness) — it is not ATA/AACE conventional endocrinology guideline content. Conventional guidelines do not endorse routine reverse T3 testing or T3/NDT as first-line therapy; apply clinical judgment when reconciling the two frameworks for a given patient.
2. Liothyronine (T3) is a cardiac stimulant. Do not initiate without confirming ferritin >45 ng/mL, and do not continue management by phone alone — see [thyroid-management.md](thyroid-management.md) Sections 9, 11, and 12 for the full safety protocol.
3. General MTHFR/methylation biochemistry content in [mthfr-methylation.md](mthfr-methylation.md) beyond what the mentorship source documents is labeled as general clinical genetics background — verify specific enzyme-activity percentages or genotype-specific claims against current literature before patient-facing use.
4. Cross-reference the iron/ferritin repletion protocol at docs/knowledge/functional-medicine/02-iron-ferritin/ before treating the ferritin gate described here, and the inflammation/Hashimoto's markers protocol at docs/knowledge/functional-medicine/03-inflammation-hashimotos/ for the shared homocysteine/CRP/uric acid framework.
