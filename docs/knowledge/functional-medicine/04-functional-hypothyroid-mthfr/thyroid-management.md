# Functional Hypothyroid & MTHFR — Thyroid Panel Interpretation & Treatment

Full-panel functional thyroid workup, optimal target ranges, T4→T3 conversion physiology, iodine dosing caution, and the T4/NDT/liothyronine treatment algorithm as taught in the Spring 2026 Joyful Heart Institute mentorship.

## Source

Jenni Gallagher, APRN, Joyful Heart Health Care — "Functional Hypothyroid Management" PPT (mentorship video session, folder "VIDEO discussion regarding MTHFR and hypothyroidism-") and the corresponding course lesson notes (Week 3 "Hypothyroidism," the MTHFR/hypothyroidism video summary, Week 5 Fullscript protocols, and scattered case studies across Weeks 1, 2, 4, 5, and 8). Evidence quality throughout is **practitioner-taught functional-medicine protocol** (single-practitioner clinical experience + cited books/blogs), not RCT-graded evidence, unless otherwise noted. Numbers are reproduced exactly as taught; where two sessions gave different figures for the same marker, both are shown with their source context rather than merged into one number.

## 1. Full Thyroid Panel — What to Order

**Initial workup (always get):**
- TSH
- Free T4 (FT4)
- Free T3 (FT3)
- Reverse T3 (RT3)
- TgAb (thyroglobulin antibody)
- TPO (thyroid peroxidase antibody)

**Follow-up labs** likely only need FT3, FT4, and RT3 (antibodies not required at every recheck). *(PPT slide 3)*

A positive antibody test for **either** TPO or thyroglobulin antibody is sufficient to diagnose Hashimoto's. TPO/TgAb optimal is <100; levels in the hundreds-to-thousands confirm Hashimoto's, mild elevations (<100) are more likely general inflammation than a definitive diagnosis. Order TSI (thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulin) only for patients with a history of Graves' disease. *(course notes, Week 1 and Week 4 sections)*

**Framing taught throughout the course:** thyroid dysfunction = metabolic dysfunction, and insulin levels often influence thyroid hormone. *(PPT slide 3)* A full panel is essential because TSH and Free T4 cannot enter the cell for mitochondrial ATP production — only Free T3 can. *(course notes, Week 1 Key Takeaways)*

## 2. Optimal Targets — Unmedicated, Healthy Patient

| Marker | Optimal target | Source |
|---|---|---|
| TSH | Ideally <2 (course notes elsewhere specify <2.0, ideally <1.5) | PPT slide 9; course notes line 267 |
| Free T4 | Around 1 ng/dL | PPT slide 9 |
| Free T3 | Around 3.5–4 ng/dL (cheat-sheet slide gives 3.5–4.2 ng/dL) | PPT slides 9, 20 |
| Reverse T3 | 15 ng/dL or less (unmedicated); course notes separately say RT3 target "~15," with >18–19 indicating inflammation rather than normal physiology | PPT slide 9; course notes lines 269–270 |

**If FT3 and RT3 are both in the ideal range, the patient is NOT hypothyroid — neither are they hyperthyroid**, regardless of TSH. *(PPT slide 9, verbatim teaching point)*

## 3. Optimal Targets — Medicated Patient

- **Ideal Reverse T3 on treatment: 10–12 ng/dL** — associated with fewest symptoms and improved energy. *(PPT slide 10)*
- If RT3 is 15 or less, there is no need for T4 dosing. *(PPT slide 10)*
- RT3 is driven by T4 dosing (levothyroxine, Synthroid, Armour, NP/natural desiccated thyroid). Inflammation, illness, and injury can also raise RT3 independent of dosing. *(PPT slide 10)*
- While actively titrating a T3 (liothyronine) dose, continue reducing T4 dose until RT3 is **9–13 ng/dL**, and continue increasing T3 dose until FT3 is around 4. *(PPT slide 22)*
- Cheat-sheet figures: Optimal Free T3 = 3.5–4.2 ng/dL; Optimal Reverse T3 = 10–12 ng/dL; Free T4 goal during pregnancy is **>1.0 ng/dL**. *(PPT slide 20)*
- Separately, the MTHFR/hypothyroidism video summary frames RT3 as the "brake pedal": if rT3 is **>12**, the patient is "over-braking" from too much T4 and the T4 dose (Levo, NP) should be reduced. *(course notes lines 245, 300 — video-session Key Takeaways; note this >12 action threshold is a tighter cutoff than the PPT's >15 "high likelihood of poor conversion" language — both are reproduced as taught rather than reconciled into one number.)*

## 4. Free T3 — The Only Thyroid Hormone That Enters the Cell

Free T3 (thyronine) is taught as **the only thyroid ingredient that enters the cell for use in energy production** — i.e., the "gas pedal." *(PPT slide 11; course notes line 246)*

**Normal conversion process:**
- FT4 is converted to FT3 via a complex enzymatic (deiodinase) process.
- Deiodinase enzyme deficiencies will stop the normal conversion process.
- **MTHFR has some impact as well** — stated directly in the PPT without further mechanistic elaboration. *(PPT slide 11)* See [mthfr-methylation.md](mthfr-methylation.md) for the methylation-cycle background relevant to this claim.

## 5. Reverse T3 — The "Brake Pedal"

High reverse T3 acts as a brake on metabolism and causes hypothyroid symptoms even when TSH/FT4 look acceptable. *(course notes line 136, 168, 245)* Practically:

- **If RT3 is higher than 15**, the likelihood of poor/no T4→T3 conversion is HIGH → these patients will need T3 (liothyronine) prescribed. *(PPT slide 13)*
- **If RT3 is holding steady between 9–15, and FT3 is also above 3.5**, T4 alone may be all that's needed — these patients are "adequate converters." *(PPT slide 13)*
- **If FT3 is less than 3.5 AND RT3 is 15 or higher**, the patient is a poor "converter" and will benefit from liothyronine (T3) rx. *(PPT slide 14)*

## 6. Iodine — Dosing and Caution

**Explicit instruction from the course:** "Iodine is essential to a healthy thyroid. Healthy salts do NOT contain iodine. **Never recommend HIGH doses of iodine.**" *(course notes line 223, MTHFR/hypothyroidism video section)*

Dosing figures given across different sessions/cases (reproduced exactly as taught — figures vary by session, all stay under 300 mcg/day for thyroid support except the specific non-thyroid exception noted below):

| Context | Dose taught | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Case study (41 y/o perimenopausal, suspected iodine deficiency, TSH 2.0) | Low-dose iodine, **<300 mcg/day** | course notes line 55 |
| MTHFR/hypothyroidism video — "Three-Legged Stool" nutritional support | **150–180 mcg/day** | course notes line 274 |
| Same session — non-thyroid exception | Higher doses (**1–3 mg/day**, i.e., 1000–3000 mcg) *may help BPH and fibrocystic breast disease* — not a thyroid-dosing recommendation | course notes line 274 |
| Week 5 Fullscript "Core Protocols" thyroid optimization | **225–300 mcg/day** | course notes lines 421, 488 |
| Case study (myalgia, TSH 10.9, low vitamin D) | **200–300 mcg/day** | course notes line 984 |

**Clinical takeaway as taught:** keep thyroid-directed iodine dosing under 300 mcg/day; the 1–3 mg/day range is reserved for a different indication (BPH/fibrocystic breast disease), not routine thyroid support, and is not to be construed as license for high-dose iodine in hypothyroid/Hashimoto's patients.

## 7. Essential Nutritional Cofactors for Thyroid Function

Taught as the "Essential Ingredients for Thyroid": **Iodine, Vitamin D, Iron, Protein, healthy gut/digestion/absorption.** "Proper nutrient support is required for healthy thyroid function." *(PPT slide 5)*

**Grain intake blocks absorption of:** iron, zinc, T3, "some proteins," and various other nutrients — and grain intake also raises inflammation. *(PPT slide 4)* This is the mechanistic rationale repeated throughout the mentorship for grain elimination in thyroid patients (phytates block/impair T3 absorption specifically — PPT slide 15).

**"Foundation of Health" pyramid** (bottom to top): (1) dietary intake of essential nutrients — water, salt, protein, fats, iron, iodine, magnesium, potassium, calcium, vitamins A, Bs, C, D, E, K, and other trace minerals; (2) supplements to correct deficiencies identified by lab/provider; (3) lifestyle choices — no tobacco, no vaping, no soy or "chemical influencers"; (4) medication. *(PPT slide 2)*

**"Three-legged stool" framing:** food choices, vitamins/supplements, and medication prescriptions are each a MAJOR part of treatment — "Omit just one 'small' part, and your treatment fails." *(PPT slide 6)*

## 8. NDT (Armour / NP) vs Levothyroxine vs Liothyronine

- **NDT (Armour or NP):** natural desiccated thyroid, porcine-derived. Combination of T4 and T3 (levothyroxine + liothyronine components: "Levo = T4," "Lio = T3"). *(PPT slide 12)*
- **Levothyroxine/Synthroid (T4 only):** singular T4 treatment is "becoming less effective" as a general trend noted in the course. Levothyroxine must be taken alone, on an empty stomach, 1 hour before food/drinks/other meds. *(PPT slide 13; course notes line 281)*
- **Liothyronine (T3 only):** can be taken with food/meds, except other stimulants. *(course notes line 282)*

**Newly diagnosed hypothyroidism — initiation algorithm** (cheat-sheet, PPT slide 20):
- If RT3 is **10 or higher**, do not start a T4 medication (it will push RT3 higher) — start **liothyronine only**, and **only if ferritin is greater than 45 ng/mL**.
- If RT3 is **9 or less**, start **NP thyroid at 30–60 mg QD**.

## 9. Ferritin — The Safety Gate Before Any T3 Medication

**Do not prescribe liothyronine (or any T3-containing product — LIO, NP, Armour) unless ferritin is above 45 ng/mL.** Below that threshold, T3 is a cardiac stimulant layered on top of low-ferritin cardiac vulnerability, risking cardiac complications, palpitations, and heart racing. *(PPT slide 15; course notes lines 97, 247, 276)*

If ferritin is low, treating it becomes the priority **before** starting T3:
- Educate on grain elimination (phytates block/impair T3 absorption in addition to iron/zinc absorption). *(PPT slide 15)*
- See the iron/ferritin topic (docs/knowledge/functional-medicine/02-iron-ferritin/) for the full repletion protocol (Ferosolv/Ferrosolb dosing, IV infusion thresholds, follow-up timing).

**Case illustrating the risk of skipping this gate:** a Hashimoto's patient developed new-onset AFib (HR 120–150) after a ferritin infusion, with TSH 57 and ferritin only 11 at baseline — analyzed as likely triggered by the severe chronic low ferritin/high TSH combination, with the infusion itself possibly overwhelming the heart by increasing oxygen delivery too quickly. Managed with metoprolol 25 mg BID, delayed second infusion dose, and levothyroxine 25 mcg started despite labs not strictly requiring T4, because the body needed support while ferritin was being optimized. *(course notes lines 423–437)*

## 10. Liothyronine (T3) Titration Protocol

**Teach patients the physiology first:** the body's usual FT3 release is early AM, prior to/during awakening. The highest amount is released at that time and used over the early part of the day, resulting in a steady decline of FT3 in the bloodstream/cells over the day. *(PPT slide 16)*

**Starting dose and titration:**
- Rx 1 tab per day x 3 weeks.
- Increase dose every 3 weeks. *(PPT slide 16)*
- If FT3 is less than 3.0, add liothyronine **5 mcg QD** and increase 1 tab every 3 weeks (Q3WK), building a dosing pattern that is highest in the early AM and lowest/last dose by 2 pm. *(PPT slide 21)*
- General titration rule stated elsewhere in the course: start with 1 tablet daily and increase by 1 tablet every 2–3 weeks. *(course notes line 284)*

**Dose pattern to mimic the body** (example distribution given in the PPT):
- 3 tabs early AM
- 2 tabs 10–11 am
- 1 tab around 1–2 pm
- **No max dose, no target dose — this must be taught explicitly to the patient.**
- Keep the lowest dose of liothyronine in the early afternoon; late-in-day doses will impair sleep for most patients.
- This pattern also helps align with lab-draw follow-up timing. *(PPT slide 17)*

**Goal FT3 while titrating liothyronine:**
- No target dose, no goal dose of medication itself — **treat symptoms, monitor labs for safety.**
- Aim for FT3 around **4 ng/dL, or slightly above** — goal FT3 is the very top of the reference range for most patients.
- **Elders, over age 65:** goal may be around **3.5** instead.
- Use symptoms to guide the dosing regimen. *(PPT slide 18)*
- Elsewhere in the course, the optimal FT3 target while on liothyronine is stated as **~4.0**, though "some patients feel great at lower levels (e.g., 3.4–3.5)." *(course notes line 285)*
- If FT3 is **3.1–3.5**, consider correcting deficiencies (vitamin D, iron/ferritin, protein; eliminate grains) and recheck labs in **4–6 months** rather than immediately increasing dose further. *(PPT slide 21)*

## 11. Safety Monitoring While on Liothyronine

Patients must be taught that liothyronine **is a cardiac stimulant**:
- Check heart rate and blood pressure **about twice weekly**; **plot on graph paper** for optimal patient understanding.
- Notify the clinician of any signs/symptoms of hyperstimulation.
- **Drop the dose back ASAP, even before calling the office**, if hyperstimulation symptoms occur.
- If the patient follows instructions to increase gradually, hyperstimulation symptoms should NOT occur. *(PPT slide 19)*

## 12. Lab Follow-up Cadence

- **Lio management requires in-person VISITS, not phone calls**, due to the cardiac risk profile. *(PPT slide 22)*
- **TSH will be suppressed by liothyronine — there is no need to continue drawing TSH once liothyronine is prescribed.** Monitor FT3, FT4, and RT3 instead. *(PPT slide 22; course notes line 286)*
- Repeat thyroid studies (FT3, FT4, RT3) every **8–10 weeks** while titrating liothyronine dose. *(PPT slides 21–22)*
- Continue reducing T4 dose until RT3 is 9–13 ng/dL; continue increasing T3 dose until FT3 is around 4. *(PPT slide 22)*
- **Long-term follow-up:** once labs are optimized and the patient feels good, spacing between visits can increase, but **always see these patients every 6 months or less** — cardiac risk accumulates without adequate follow-up and assessment. *(PPT slide 23)*
- Once ferritin is above ~45, start the same T3 treatment plan; recheck iron every 4–6 months until levels remain stable; **decrease the liothyronine dose if ferritin falls back down.** *(PPT slide 23)*
- General optimal-management follow-up cadence taught: **Q8 weeks → Q4 months → Q6 months** as the patient stabilizes. *(PPT slide 24)*

## 13. What Optimal Thyroid Management Requires (Summary Framing)

- Thorough nutritional assessment, with treatment and lab follow-up.
- Required cofactor hormones/nutrients: iron, vitamin D, protein.
- Adequate patient education and CYA documentation.
- Patient willingness to cooperate with all recommendations and follow-up.
- Frequent follow-up on the Q8wk–Q4mo–Q6mo cadence above. *(PPT slide 24)*

## 14. Hashimoto's — What's Different

Same treatment approach as above, **EXCEPT** inflammation MUST be addressed:
- Eliminate all sources of dietary inflammation: grains, seed oils, dairy, oxalates, uric acid, tobacco, vaping, etc.
- Adjunct supplements taught: vitamin C, black cumin seed, CoQ10, luteolin, chlorella, berberine, B-complex (methylated B — "methylB"). *(PPT slide 25)*
- Monitor antibodies (TPO/TgAb) every **6 months** — antibody response is slow.
- Recommended patient reading: *The Thyroid Pharmacist* (Isabella Wentz). *(PPT slide 25; also cited as "The Hashimoto's Protocol" in the References slide and in course notes line 345)*

**Illustrative severe case:** ferritin 6→11 ng/mL after 5 months of oral iron (interpreted as non-compliance with the grain-free diet, critical for iron absorption); TSH 57.3 mIU/L; Free T4 0.4 ng/dL; Free T3 3.9 pg/mL (**at goal — T3 medication not needed** despite the severity of TSH/FT4 derangement); Reverse T3 8 ng/dL; TPO Ab 386 → >1100 IU/mL. Plan: Ferraheme infusion (510 mg x 2 doses, 1 week apart), retest iron/ferritin in 6–8 weeks; start levothyroxine 25 mcg daily; retest thyroid labs with the post-infusion iron panel; reiterate grain-free diet education. *(course notes lines 324–345)*

## 15. LDN (Low-Dose Naltrexone) as an Adjunct

- Possible aid for Hashimoto's, but **not first-line** — "fighting a losing battle" (a "round robin of inflammation and anti-inflammation") if dietary inflammation triggers aren't eliminated first; **patients must be grain-free for LDN to be effective**, since grains cause an inflammatory spike that negates LDN's overnight anti-inflammatory effect. *(PPT slide 26; course notes lines 390–392)*
- **Titration (Dr. Isabella Wentz protocol):** start **0.5 mg HS x 30 days** (course notes: "for two weeks" is stated in one summary — PPT explicitly says 30 days; both reproduced as taught); increase by half to whole mg every 30 days (course notes: "every two weeks" — see discrepancy note below).
- Most patients do well around **4–7 mg QD**. *(PPT slide 26; course notes line 398 gives 4.5–7 mg daily)*
- **Note on source discrepancy:** the PPT (primary session material) states a 30-day interval between increases; the AI-generated lesson-notes summary of the Week 4 inflammation session states a 2-week interval (0.5 mg → 1.0 mg → 1.5 mg every two weeks). Both are reproduced here rather than reconciled — confirm against Dr. Wentz's published protocol or Jenni Gallagher directly before prescribing.
- Must be compounded — do not instruct patients to crush and dilute standard naltrexone tablets. *(course notes line 401)*

## 16. Worked Case Examples (Reverse T3 / Free T3 Titration)

**Case 1 ("Keydella"), 48 y/o on 240 mg NP thyroid, RT3 = 17.**
Problem: high RT3 indicates over-braking from excessive T4. Plan: reduce NP to 120 mg to lower T4; immediately replace the lost T3 by adding liothyronine (e.g., 15 mcg AM, 25 mcg lunch) to maintain the current T3 level. *(course notes lines 290–293)*

**Case 2 ("Tracy"), 57 y/o on 75 mcg levothyroxine, RT3 = 38.**
Problem: extremely high RT3 indicates severe over-braking from T4. Plan: drastically reduce levothyroxine to 25 mcg to remove the brake; start liothyronine titration to provide energy. *(course notes lines 295–298)*

## Evidence Quality Note

All target ranges, dosing figures, and titration schedules above are **taught clinical protocol from a single functional-medicine practitioner's mentorship program**, cross-referenced against a small number of practitioner blogs/books (Paul Robinson, Isabella Wentz, Westin Childs, Stop the Thyroid Madness) that are themselves patient-advocacy/practitioner sources rather than peer-reviewed guidelines. This differs materially from ATA/AACE conventional endocrinology guidance (which does not endorse routine reverse T3 testing or T3 monotherapy/NDT as first-line therapy). Use this content as the taught functional-medicine framework Derek is applying in practice, not as a substitute for conventional endocrine guideline review — apply standard clinical judgment, informed consent, and cardiac risk screening (ferritin, baseline EKG where indicated) before prescribing T3-containing products.
