## 2. Iodine & Salt — New Practical Detail (pt1)

- **New specific mechanism/rationale for the 150–180 mcg RDA figure:** two teaspoons of *iodized* salt, taken consistently every day, supplies approximately the RDA of iodine *and* a decent amount of sodium/chloride for muscle function and stomach acid — described as a simple "two teaspoons" rule of thumb. [pt1 25:57–26:25]

## 4. Reverse T3 / Free T3 — New Nuances (pt1)

## 5. Liothyronine (Lio) Prescribing — New Practical/Operational Pearls (pt1)

- **New mnemonic:** "levo" (4 letters) = T4; "lio" (3 letters) = T3 — used as a quick mental shorthand to keep the two straight. [pt1 49:24–49:41]
- **New specific tablet-strength/format constraint:** liothyronine only comes in **5, 25, and 50 mcg** tablet strengths commercially; anything else requires compounding (e.g., a 10 or 12 mcg tablet). [pt1 87:38–87:57]
- **New pharmacy-workaround pearl:** when prescribing multiple 5 mcg tablets per day, write the exact split on the script itself (e.g., "take 3 tablets in the morning and 2 tablets at lunch") rather than just a total daily quantity — otherwise the pharmacy may try to substitute/consolidate into fewer, larger-dose tablets that don't match the intended split-dosing pattern. [pt1 86:53–87:38]
- **New titration mechanics — where to add the next tablet:** when increasing the Lio dose, the new tablet can be added to *any* time slot **as long as the morning (first) dose remains the highest**. Concrete example given: patient on 3-2-1 (AM-midday-2pm) can only add to the AM dose (since 3-2-1 has no slot with >1 tablet of headroom before violating the "highest AM dose" rule); a patient on 5-3-1 has more flexibility since multiple slots differ by >1 tablet. [pt1 90:00–91:30]
- **New Rx/refill policy detail:** while actively titrating, she prescribes Lio for **90 days with zero refills**; once the patient is stable, **90 days with one refill** — and never more than that one refill, ever. [pt1 75:08–75:20]
- **New: never prescribe/refill outside of a visit** — described explicitly as a safety/liability practice (except rare travel-related exceptions) and reinforced by the same logic applied to the "why can't mainstream use this drug" discussion (it "comes with baggage" requiring in-person follow-up). - **New — osteoporosis/thyroid-medication myth explicitly debunked with mechanism:** she describes the origin of the "T3/thyroid meds cause osteoporosis" concern as flawed methodology — endocrinology researchers studied *hyperthyroid* patients (who have excess FT3/FT4) and found elevated osteoporosis risk, then extrapolated this to hypothyroid patients placed on medication to reach "normal" labs, incorrectly treating appropriately-dosed replacement as equivalent to endogenous hyperthyroidism.
