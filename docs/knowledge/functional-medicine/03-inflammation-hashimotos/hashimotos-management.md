# Inflammation & Hashimoto's — Hashimoto's Thyroiditis Management

Functional diagnosis and management of Hashimoto's thyroiditis as autoimmune/inflammation-driven disease, as taught in Spring 2026 Mentorship Weeks 3–4 and reinforced in Weeks 5 and 9 case discussions.

**Evidence quality:** Practitioner-taught approach from Jenni Gallagher, APRN (mentorship slides and session notes) plus one referenced patient-education text (Dr. Isabella Wentz). Diagnostic thresholds and dosing are reproduced exactly as taught — this is expert-opinion/practitioner-protocol level unless otherwise noted, not a professional-society guideline.

## 1. Core Framing

**Hashimoto's and Graves' disease are immune dysfunction and inflammation-driven conditions**, not purely thyroid-hormone problems. Some patients have "pretty normal" thyroid hormone levels even when immune dysfunction and inflammation are clearly present — meaning a normal TSH/free T4/free T3 does not rule out autoimmune thyroid disease. Antibody testing is required to catch these cases.

## 2. Antibody Interpretation

| Antibody | Typically elevated in | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Thyroid Stimulating Antibody (TSI) | Graves' disease (hyperthyroidism) | Taught as RARE; order when history suggests Graves' (tachycardia, nervousness, anxiety, sleeplessness/insomnia, and weight **loss**) |
| TPO (thyroid peroxidase) Ab | Hashimoto's (hypothyroidism) | |
| Thyroglobulin (Tg) Ab | Hashimoto's **OR** Graves' disease | Not specific to one direction of thyroid dysfunction |

**Recommended initial workup:** draw **both TPO and TgAb** in all patients initially when hypothyroidism is suspected. A positive antibody test for **either** TPO or thyroglobulin is sufficient to diagnose Hashimoto's — you do not need both positive.

### Magnitude matters
- TPO/TgAb levels in the **hundreds or thousands** confirm Hashimoto's.
- **Mild elevations (<100)** are likely general inflammation, not necessarily a definitive Hashimoto's diagnosis — interpret in clinical context rather than treating any positive as automatically diagnostic.
- Course-notes optimal target for TPO/TgAb: **<100**.
- Slight elevations may not mean anything on their own — correlate with symptoms.

## 3. Full Thyroid Panel — Ordering and Targets

Order the **full panel**, not TSH alone: TSH, Free T3, Free T4, Reverse T3, TPO Ab, and Thyroglobulin Ab.

| Marker | Target (unmedicated) | Role |
|---|---|---|
| TSH | <2.0 (ideally <1.5) | |
| Free T4 (fT4) | — | Less critical on its own than conversion to fT3 |
| Free T3 (fT3) | ~3.5, optimal target ~4.0 | The "gas pedal" — active hormone for energy. Some patients feel best at 3.4–3.5. If fT4 is normal but fT3 is low, conversion is impaired. |
| Reverse T3 (rT3) | ~15; >18–19 indicates inflammation (not normal physiology) | The "brake pedal." High rT3 = over-braking from too much T4 |

**rT3 dictates T4 dose; fT3 dictates T3 dose.** If rT3 is high (>12 per Week 3 teaching, >18-19 per Week 4 teaching — reproduced as taught, exact single cutoff is "[source unclear]" across sessions), reduce T4 dose (Levo/NP) to remove the brake. If fT3 is low (<3.5), add liothyronine (LIO/T3) for energy.

### Safety gate before starting T3
**Do not start any T3 medication (LIO, NP, Armour) if ferritin is low (<45).** Low ferritin causes cardiac symptoms; T3 is a cardiac stimulant. Combining the two is dangerous. Correct ferritin first — see the iron/ferritin protocol elsewhere in the functional-medicine knowledge base (Ferosolv/ferrous bisglycinate 45 mg BID + high-dose vitamin C 2–4g TID/QID) before adding T3.

### Medication practicalities (as taught)
- **Levo (T4):** must be taken alone on an empty stomach, 1 hour before food/drinks/other meds.
- **LIO (T3):** can be taken with food/meds (except other stimulants).
- **Dosing pattern:** mimic natural rhythm — highest dose on waking, mid-day dose, final dose by 2 PM to protect sleep.
- **Titration:** start with 1 tablet daily, increase by 1 tablet every 2–3 weeks; titrate to symptoms and labs.
- TSH will be suppressed by LIO — monitor fT3, fT4, and rT3 instead of relying on TSH alone once T3 is started.

## 4. Triggers / Drivers of Hashimoto's

Same general inflammation-driver framework applies (see [inflammation-drivers.md](inflammation-drivers.md) Section 3), with grains singled out specifically for thyroid autoimmunity:
- Grains — both as a direct inflammatory/autoimmune trigger and as a toxin carrier from planting/harvesting/processing
- Seed oils (canola, corn, vegetable)
- Dairy
- Gut dysbiosis, SIBO, yeast overgrowth
- Chronic stress & poor sleep
- Environmental toxins (mold, pesticides, plastics)
- Blood sugar dysregulation / insulin resistance
- Hormonal imbalance
- Chronic infections / stealth pathogens

Nutritional cofactor drivers specific to thyroid function ("three-legged stool," Week 3):
- **Iodine:** 150–180 mcg/day. Higher doses (1–3 mg/day) may help BPH and fibrocystic breast disease. **Never recommend HIGH doses of iodine** — this is stated explicitly as a caution given iodine can precipitate or worsen autoimmune thyroid flares.
- **Vitamin D:** ensure adequate intake with cofactors (K2, magnesium) and fat (e.g., butter) for absorption.
- **Iron & ferritin:** correct low iron/ferritin before starting T3 (see Section 3 safety gate).
- **Protein:** essential for cellular function; ensure adequate intake and absorption.

## 5. Functional Management Sequence

1. **Grain-free diet is foundational and explicitly reiterated as necessary** — patient education point repeated across multiple sessions. Non-compliance with a grain-free diet is called out directly as a driver of poor treatment response (see Case 1 below).
2. **Gut healing** — same protocol as general inflammation: complete bowel emptying, remove irritants (grains, roughage, NSAIDs, alcohol), the "2 and 2 Rule" (2 tsp salt/day + 2 tbsp butter/day), digestive enzyme support.
3. **Stress/sleep regulation** — vagus nerve activation, sleep prioritization; progesterone 200 mg at bedtime noted as improving sleep in this population.
4. **Foundational supplementation:** high-dose vitamin C (titrate to bowel tolerance), vitamin D with cofactors, L-glutamine, zinc-carnosine, collagen, bone broth, digestive enzymes (e.g., EnzymeX Pro / Enzymix Pro by Apex Energetics), NAC and milk thistle (liver support), methylated B vitamins (avoid cyanocobalamin).
5. **Correct iron/ferritin and thyroid hormone** as above, once the foundational diet/gut/stress work is underway.
6. **LDN as an adjunct** once the patient is grain-free and foundational work is in place — see Section 6.

**Patient education resource named in source:** Dr. Isabella Wentz, *The Hashimoto's Protocol* — recommended to reinforce the grain-free diet message with non-compliant patients.

## 6. LDN in Hashimoto's — Brief Note

LDN (low-dose naltrexone) is named specifically among the anti-inflammatory benefits/uses of naltrexone for **Hashimoto's**, alongside RA, Sjögren's, SLE, IBD, Lyme, long-haul COVID, fibromyalgia, and MS. It works on opioid receptors to modulate the immune system and reduce inflammation.

**Prerequisite taught repeatedly: the patient must be grain-free for LDN to work.** Grains cause an inflammatory spike that negates LDN's overnight anti-inflammatory effect — "the see-saw fluctuations of inflammation and LDN will prevent adequate inflammation reduction" if diet isn't addressed first. LDN is described as a poor value for the patient's cash-pay cost if this prerequisite isn't met (LDN is compounded; insurance does not cover it).

**Full dosing, titration schedule (0.5 mg → 1.0 mg → 1.5 mg → maintenance 4.5–7 mg, up to 8–10 mg PRN), and prescribing details are covered in [08-methylene-blue-ivm-ldn](../08-methylene-blue-ivm-ldn/README.md) — not duplicated here.**

## 7. Lab Monitoring Cadence

- Inflammatory/antibody markers (TPO, TgAb, CRPhs, uric acid, ferritin, WBC, complements, RA markers) are rechecked roughly **every 9–18 months** — inflammation and antibody levels take a long time to fall.
- Elsewhere in course notes, LDN-specific inflammatory marker recheck (e.g., CRP) is timed to **8–9 months** post-titration "to avoid patient frustration from slow-moving results."
- Iron/ferritin: recheck no sooner than **4 months** post oral therapy start (100-day RBC lifecycle), or **6–8 weeks** post-infusion for safety (to ensure levels aren't dangerously high).

## 8. Illustrative Case Data (De-identified, from mentorship case discussions)

### Case 1 — Severe Hashimoto's, dietary non-compliance
- Ferritin: 6 → 11 ng/mL after 5 months of oral iron (minimal rise, consistent with non-compliance with grain-free diet, which is critical for iron absorption)
- TSH: 57.3 mIU/L
- Free T4: 0.4 ng/dL
- Free T3: 3.9 pg/mL (at goal — T3 supplementation not needed)
- Reverse T3: 8 ng/dL
- TPO Ab: 386 → >1100 IU/mL
- **Plan:** Ferraheme infusion (510 mg x 2 doses, 1 week apart; pre-medication rarely needed, PRN cetirizine/diphenhydramine if required; retest iron/ferritin in 6–8 weeks). Start levothyroxine 25 mcg daily; retest thyroid labs with the post-infusion iron panel. Reiterate grain-free diet; recommend *The Hashimoto's Protocol* (Wentz).

### Case 2 — Hashimoto's with new-onset AFib after ferritin infusion
- TSH 57, ferritin 11
- New-onset AFib (HR 120–150), referred abdominal pain
- **Analysis:** AFib was attributed to severe chronic low ferritin + high TSH rather than the infusion itself; the infusion may have overwhelmed the heart by rapidly increasing oxygen delivery capacity.
- **Plan:** Metoprolol 25 mg BID for rate control (monitor for fatigue; switch to carvedilol if needed); delay second infusion half by a few days to let the beta-blocker take effect; consider switching to Vinifer (fewer side effects than Feraheme); start levothyroxine 25 mcg despite labs not strictly requiring T4, because the body needs support while ferritin is optimized; recheck EKG and symptoms in 48 hours.

### Case 3 — Acute gastritis/diarrhea in an existing Hashimoto's patient
- Patient with known Hashimoto's developed acute gastritis and diarrhea for two weeks; GI endoscopy normal, symptoms persisted.
- Initial pantoprazole prescription is flagged as a likely contributor to the diarrhea (microbiome disruption).
- Differential raised: new parasite outbreak, *Cyclospora cayetanensis* (reported in 20 US states at the time of teaching) — see the parasite-protocol knowledge base for full workup.
- **Plan:** IV hydration, bone broth/collagen-fortified broth for symptomatic relief; empirical 5-day course of ivermectin; order ova & parasite stool test (patient may start ivermectin immediately after sample collection).
