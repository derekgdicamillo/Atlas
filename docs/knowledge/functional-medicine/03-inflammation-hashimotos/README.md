## Scope

This topic covers:
- **General inflammation markers**: CRP/hs-CRP, WBC, homocysteine, uric acid, ferritin (as an acute-phase reactant), and how they're interpreted together
- **Functional root-cause framing** for chronic low-grade inflammation (diet, gut, stress, toxins, blood sugar, hormones, infection)
- **Hashimoto's thyroiditis**: TPO Ab / thyroglobulin Ab / TSI interpretation, diagnostic thresholds, and the functional management sequence (diet → gut → stress → metabolic → thyroid hormone)
- Brief mention of **low-dose naltrexone (LDN)** as an autoimmune/anti-inflammatory adjunct — full dosing and titration protocol lives in **08-methylene-blue-ivm-ldn**, not here

## Key Takeaways

1. **Diagnose inflammation with functional (not just "normal range") labs**: WBC goal <6.5–7.0, hs-CRP goal <1.0, uric acid goal <5, homocysteine goal 5–8 (target ~7). A ferritin significantly higher than the iron number (1.5–2x or more) signals an inflammatory acute-phase response rather than true iron overload.
2. **Root-cause sequence, taught as a chain**: Remove triggers → Repair gut → Regulate stress → Restore metabolic balance → Support resolution. This is foundational and non-negotiable before medication is added.
3. **Inflammation resolves slowly.** Recheck inflammatory markers (CRP, homocysteine, uric acid, ferritin, WBC, complements, RA markers, thyroid antibodies) roughly every 9–18 months, not every 2–3 months — the teaching is explicit that inflammation "takes a LONG time to fall."
4. **Hashimoto's is diagnosed by a single positive antibody.** A positive TPO Ab OR thyroglobulin Ab is sufficient; levels in the hundreds-to-thousands confirm the diagnosis, while mild elevation (<100) is more likely general inflammation than a definitive Hashimoto's diagnosis. Draw both TPO and TgAb whenever hypothyroidism is suspected.
5. **Hashimoto's and Graves' are immune/inflammation-driven diseases, not purely thyroid-hormone diseases** — some patients have near-normal thyroid hormone levels while immune dysfunction and inflammation are clearly present.
6. **LDN is an autoimmune-inflammation adjunct, not a substitute for diet.** It is taught as ineffective (or much less effective) without a grain-free diet, because grain-driven inflammatory spikes counteract its overnight anti-inflammatory action. See [08-methylene-blue-ivm-ldn](../08-methylene-blue-ivm-ldn/README.md) for the full titration protocol and dosing.

## When to Use

- Patient presents with fatigue, joint pain, "various aches and pains," plantar fasciitis, frozen shoulder, unexplained swelling, or is overweight with no clear single diagnosis — screen general inflammation markers.
- Hypothyroid symptoms are present or TSH is abnormal — order a full thyroid panel including TPO and TgAb to rule in/out Hashimoto's, per [hashimotos-management.md](hashimotos-management.md).
- CRP, homocysteine, uric acid, ferritin, or WBC come back elevated on a routine panel and the driver isn't obvious — work through [inflammation-drivers.md](inflammation-drivers.md) for differential and root-cause questions to ask.
- Considering LDN for an autoimmune or chronic inflammatory condition — confirm the patient is willing to commit to a grain-free diet first (see Key Takeaway 6), then hand off to 08 for dosing.

Numeric thresholds in this topic come from a single mentor's teaching slides, a patient-facing graphic, and session notes — they are practitioner-taught functional/optimal ranges, not laboratory reference ranges or society guideline cutoffs.
