# Inflammation & Hashimoto's — Inflammation Drivers & Markers

Functional interpretation of the common inflammatory lab markers (WBC, hs-CRP, uric acid, homocysteine, ferritin, and the autoimmune panel), what drives chronic inflammation, and the root-cause treatment sequence, as taught in Spring 2026 Mentorship Week 4.

**Evidence quality:** Practitioner-taught functional/optimal ranges from Jenni Gallagher, APRN (mentorship slides, patient handout, session notes). These are clinical teaching points from one practitioner's protocol, not laboratory reference ranges or formal guideline cutoffs. Five PMC/journal references were listed in the source slide deck without being mapped to specific claims — see References below.

## 1. Core Inflammation Markers — Optimal/Goal Values

These are the "common inflammation markers" as taught, with goal values used to flag functional (subclinical) inflammation before it reaches standard-lab "abnormal" flags:

| Marker | Goal / Optimal | Notes |
|--------|-----------------|-------|
| WBC | <6.5–7 (x10⁹/L) | Chronically high-normal WBC = signal of constant inflammation, not just infection |
| hs-CRP (CRPhs) | <1.0 mg/L | Use **high-sensitivity** CRP, not plain CRP — "plain CRP is not quantitative enough" |
| Uric acid | <5 | Taught as "the newest inflammation marker" — not just a gout marker |
| Homocysteine | 5–8 (target ~7) | Elevated >8–9 indicates inflammation, often linked to MTHFR variants and B-vitamin deficiency |
| Ferritin | Ferritin should not be >1.5–2x the iron number | Ferritin >1.5–2x iron (e.g., iron 67 mcg/dL, ferritin 100 ng/mL) = most likely an inflammation/acute-phase response, not iron overload |
| Reverse T3 | — | Elevated "sometimes" tracks with inflammation; see thyroid section |
| Thyroid antibodies (TPO, TgAb) | — | Levels "just barely above range" may reflect general inflammation rather than confirmed Hashimoto's — see [hashimotos-management.md](hashimotos-management.md) |

Additional autoimmune/inflammatory panel taught alongside the above (order based on clinical suspicion, not routinely on everyone):
- Thyroglobulin Ab, Thyroid peroxidase (TPO) Ab
- ANA / ANA IFA (**order the immunofluorescence assay for higher specificity; never order an ANA reflex panel — it can miss other positive markers**)
- Sed rate (ESR)
- Rheumatoid factor (RF)
- Anti-CCP (anti–cyclic citrullinated peptide) — order alongside RF for rheumatoid arthritis workup
- Complements C3 & C4 — order for lupus workup
- Anti-dsDNA, Smith Ab (lupus-specific)
- Anti-SSA (Ro) and Anti-SSB (La) — order for Sjögren's workup
- TSI (thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulin) — order when history suggests Graves' disease (tachycardia, nervousness, anxiety, insomnia, weight **loss**)

**Retesting cadence:** Order hs-CRP (and the general inflammation panel) roughly **every 9–18 months**, because inflammation takes a long time to resolve/fall. Retesting more frequently (e.g., every 2–3 months) is taught as generally not useful — the physiology hasn't had time to change. This mirrors the guidance for retesting iron/ferritin after infusion (4 months, aligned to the 100-day RBC lifecycle) — give the underlying physiology time before redrawing.

## 3. Drivers of Chronic Inflammation (Root-Cause Framing)

Taught driver list:
- Ultra-processed foods, excess sugar, industrial seed oils
- Food sensitivities (gluten, dairy, eggs, soy are the most common)
- Dysbiosis, SIBO, yeast overgrowth
- Chronic stress & poor sleep
- Environmental toxins (mold, pesticides, plastics)
- Blood sugar dysregulation / insulin resistance
- Hormonal imbalance
- Chronic infections or stealth pathogens

Insulin is repeatedly taught as a root driver: "High insulin (>5) drives inflammation (CRP >1) and hormone imbalance. Correcting insulin is the first step to resolving most chronic issues" (Week 1 notes). This is echoed in the Week 4 reducing-inflammation framing: "Reduce insulin — driver of inflammation."

## 4. WBC Interpretation

### Conditions associated with elevated WBC
Acute or chronic infection; insulin resistance, metabolic syndrome, diabetes; stress; trauma/surgery; smoking; chronic kidney disease; some autoimmune disorders; exercise; mild/subtle/chronic inflammation; cancers.

**Medications that elevate WBC:** corticosteroids, beta-agonists, lithium, epinephrine.

### Differential by WBC subtype (Key Takeaways from source)
- Mild elevation + normal differential = usually reactive
- Neutrophilia → infection, steroids, stress
- Lymphocytosis → viral illness
- Eosinophilia → allergy, asthma, **parasites** (cross-reference: see the parasite-protocol knowledge base for full eosinophilia workup)
- Persistent elevation (>3 months) → evaluate further

## 5. hs-CRP Interpretation

**Note taught explicitly: plain CRP is not quantitative enough — always use hs-CRP.**

### Conditions associated with elevated hs-CRP
- Cardiovascular disease: atherosclerosis, coronary artery disease, prior MI or stroke risk
- Obesity & metabolic syndrome
- Type 2 diabetes / insulin resistance
- Chronic inflammatory disease: rheumatoid arthritis, lupus (often mild–moderate), psoriasis, inflammatory bowel disease
- Acute or chronic infection
- Smoking
- Chronic kidney disease
- Malignancy
- Obstructive sleep apnea

### Key clinical point
hs-CRP >10 is taught as "likely NOT CVD" — i.e., very high CRP points away from routine cardiovascular risk toward an acute/other inflammatory process (infection, autoimmune flare, malignancy) and should prompt broader workup rather than a cardiovascular-only interpretation. A level of 3–10 is taught as a strong cardiovascular risk factor.

hs-CRP reflects inflammation and CV risk; uric acid reflects metabolic and renal stress. **When both are elevated together, think cardiometabolic disease first.**

## 6. Uric Acid Interpretation

### Conditions associated with elevated uric acid
Gout; chronic kidney disease; hypertension; metabolic syndrome/insulin resistance; type 2 diabetes; obesity; heart failure; atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease; diuretic use (thiazides, loops); cancers; psoriasis.

Uric acid >5 is also taught elsewhere (CKD teaching, Week 8) as "a strong predictor of hypertension, insulin resistance, and CKD progression" — reinforcing its role as a general inflammatory/metabolic marker, not just a gout marker.

## 7. Homocysteine Interpretation

### Conditions associated with elevated homocysteine
Cardiovascular/thrombotic group: CVD, atherosclerosis, CAD, stroke/TIA, peripheral vascular disease, thrombotic disorders, venous thromboembolism, recurrent pregnancy loss, hypercoagulable states.

Nutritional/other group: vitamin B12 deficiency, folate deficiency, vitamin B6 deficiency, dementia, cognitive decline, hypertension, depression, peripheral neuropathy (often B12-related), CKD, homocystinuria, MTHFR variants.

### Workup when homocysteine is elevated
1. **Think B12/folate deficiency first.**
2. Normal methylmalonic acid (MMA) + elevated homocysteine → **folate deficiency**.
3. Elevated MMA + elevated homocysteine → **B12 deficiency**.

### Supplementation note
Use methylated B-vitamin forms (methylcobalamin, methylfolate); avoid cyanocobalamin when possible.

## 8. Ferritin as an Inflammatory (Acute-Phase) Marker

- Ferritin rises easily with illness, trauma, or surgery because of the body's inflammatory response — it is an acute-phase reactant, not purely an iron-storage number.
- Compare ferritin to the iron number, but note the **different units**: ferritin is measured in ng/mL, iron in mcg/dL — they are not directly comparable 1:1 by number alone, only by the ratio rule below.
- **Ratio rule:** Ferritin that is **1.5–2x (or higher) the iron number** most often reflects an inflammation response rather than true iron loading.
  - Worked example from source: Iron 67 mcg/dL, Ferritin 100 ng/mL → most likely an inflammation response.
- Other causes of an elevated ferritin-to-iron ratio taught: vitamin D deficiency; gut dysfunction (Candida, SIBO, IBS/Crohn's).
- Ferritin and hepcidin are "intricately linked" — hepcidin is the hormone that blocks iron absorption. **Lower hepcidin by raising vitamin D level/absorption.**
- **High ferritin threshold requiring further workup:** HFE gene testing and/or liver imaging is required if ferritin is above 500/600 ng/mL, and/or there is a mild elevation WITH transferrin saturation greater than 45% — evaluate for hereditary hemochromatosis.
- Vitamin C, taken multiple times a day, is taught as helpful for moving iron and ferritin and for GI/absorption effects — see high-dose vitamin C dosing in Section 9.

## 9. Reducing Inflammation — The Functional Approach

### Diet (primary tool)
- Eliminate seed oils: vegetable oil, canola oil, corn oil
- Eliminate grains — both as a direct inflammatory agent and as a carrier for planting/harvesting/processing toxins
- Reduce insulin (a driver of inflammation) — diets taught as options: Whole30, Paleo, AIP (autoimmune protocol), low-carb Mediterranean, Keto, Carnivore, Keto/Carnivore
- Cook with butter, lard, tallow, or avocado oil; add olive oil **after** cooking to preserve its integrity
- In sensitive/inflamed patients, avoid raw, fibrous vegetables (e.g., large salads) that can irritate the gut lining during active gut healing
- **"2 and 2 Rule"**: 2 tsp salt/day (supports HCl production) + 2 tbsp butter/day (feeds healthy gut bacteria)

### Gut repair
- Complete bowel emptying is recommended as a starting step. Laxative of choice: Milk of Magnesia (MoM), high-dose Senna, salt water flush, coconut oil, coffee with coconut oil, or high-dose vitamin C.
- Remove gut irritants: grains, roughage, NSAIDs, alcohol, certain medications.
- Support digestion: increase salt intake (for HCl production), increase butter (hydroxybutyrate) intake, adequate water intake — approx. **2–3 L/day with electrolytes**.
- **Post-cholecystectomy patients:** consider digestive enzymes 15 minutes prior to all meals (not necessarily snacks) — betaine, HCl, pepsin, proteases, lipases. Named products from source: Advanced Digestive Enzymes (Thorne), Enzymix Pro / EnzymeX Pro (Apex Energetics). Also: L-glutamine, collagen, bone broth, zinc carnosine, omega-3s.

### Lifestyle
- Sleep optimization — taught as non-negotiable
- Breathwork (vagus nerve activation)
- Mindfulness / meditation
- Gentle movement (walking, yoga, mobility)
- Manage stress; progesterone 200 mg at bedtime is mentioned elsewhere in course notes as a sleep aid that can help this population (see hormone-health topic for full dosing context — not detailed here)
- Hydration with electrolytes to prevent mineral depletion

### Detox / liver support
- Glutathione or its precursors: NAC or ALA, 250–1000 mg QD, may divide
- NAC 600 mg QD — **caution with sulfa allergy**
- Milk thistle 150–600 mg QD, usually divided
- B vitamins — avoid cyanocobalamin when possible
- Red light therapy; sauna/hot tub; Epsom salt soaks

### LDN cross-reference
LDN (low-dose naltrexone) is taught as an effective anti-inflammatory/immune-modulating adjunct for autoimmune conditions (RA, Sjögren's, Hashimoto's, SLE, IBD, Lyme, long-haul COVID, fibromyalgia, MS) but **requires a grain-free diet to work** — grain-driven inflammatory spikes counteract its overnight anti-inflammatory action. **See [08-methylene-blue-ivm-ldn](../08-methylene-blue-ivm-ldn/README.md) for full LDN dosing/titration** — not duplicated here.

## 10. Root-Cause Sequence (Key Takeaway)

> Remove triggers → Repair gut → Regulate stress → Restore metabolic balance → Support resolution
>
> Follow labs over LONG periods of time.

## 11. Illustrative Case Data (De-identified, from mentorship case discussion)

41 y/o perimenopausal, post-hysterectomy (no ovaries), presenting with high inflammation and insulin resistance:
- Inflammation: CRP 6.0 (high), Homocysteine 12.1 (high), WBC 10.6 (high)
- Insulin resistance: Fasting glucose 110, Triglycerides 176 (high), HDL 28 (low)
- Hormones: Estradiol 51, Testosterone 11 (low), DHEA-S 78 (very low)
- Thyroid: TSH 2.0, Free T4 1.0, Free T3 3.6
- Iron: Iron 87, Ferritin 67
- **Root-cause read:** inflammation and insulin resistance are driving the metabolic dysfunction; low HDL and DHEA-S indicate a lack of raw material (cholesterol) for hormone synthesis; TSH of 2.0 is suboptimal, suspect iodine deficiency.
- **Initial plan:** anti-inflammatory grain-free high-protein (red meat) diet; OsteoPX (D3/K2/Mg/Boron); high-dose vitamin C; low-dose iodine (<300 mcg/day); berberine 1000 mg BID or dihydroberberine 200 mg/day; progesterone for sleep/irritability; DHEA 5–10 mg/day (titrate to 20–30 mg). Retest labs in 4–5 months.
