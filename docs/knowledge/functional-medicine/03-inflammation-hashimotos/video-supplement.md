## 1. Thyroid Antibody & Natural History Nuances

- **High ferritin is a clotting risk**: taught mechanism is that the ferritin molecule itself is a "sticky kind of aggregate," not a smooth molecule, so high ferritin poses an elevated clot risk independent of iron overload status. [33:52]
- **Graves' often progresses to Hashimoto's over time**: "most of the time, Graves patients become hypothyroid patients... Graves hyperthyroidism most often becomes Hashimoto's hypothyroidism at some point." Rarely, a patient can have both simultaneously (positive TSI + positive TPO/TgAb). [35:57–36:29]
- **Epidemiologic ratio**: roughly 1 Graves'/hyperthyroid patient for every 80–100 hypothyroid patients in practice; many prescribers never see one in their career. [672–676]
- Any patient with a personal history of Graves'/hyperthyroidism should get a **TSI on the first workup**, even if currently presenting as hypothyroid — Graves' and Hashimoto's antibodies can coexist or flip. If TSI is negative/very low, it does not need to be repeated for a long time. [702–707]
- **Hashimoto's patients are taught to be "almost always" also MTHFR-positive or COMT-positive** (cross-referenced to a separate MTHFR/thyroid video posted in Week 3, not this session). [647–649]
- Mild thyroid antibody elevation (<100) is *not* automatically dismissed as "just inflammation" if the patient has **previously documented much higher values and has been actively working to bring them down** — in that case still consider it Hashimoto's in remission/response, not ruled out. [806–809]
- New actionable step not in current KB: when TPO/TgAb are elevated even mildly (e.g., "30–40 range, barely abnormal"), **guidelines are cited as recommending a thyroid ultrasound** — may reveal nodules/goiters and can support catching autoimmune disease early. [67:05–68:30 → lines 1267–1280]
- Practice caution: be careful labeling a patient with an autoimmune diagnosis code when not fully certain — it becomes visible in insurance/provider databases and "causes problems with other providers... their insurers." [1261–1266]

## 3. General Inflammation Markers — Added Practicalities

- **hs-CRP has an alternative: "cardiac CRP."** Either a cardiac CRP or hs-CRP is acceptable — both are described as better than plain CRP; just be consistent about which one you order. [605–607]
- Operational tip for recurring lab-ordering errors: if a lab keeps running plain CRP instead of hs-CRP/cardiac CRP, add a note to the next order for that patient/company: "please double check CRP order — last time it was the incorrect test." [621–627]
- Uric acid's role as an inflammation marker (not just gout/kidney stones) is attributed specifically to **Dr. Perlmutter's book "Drop Acid."** [628–637]
- **CRP 3–10 → consider a coronary artery calcium (CAC) scan** as a follow-up workup step for cardiovascular risk (not previously specified in KB). [953–954]
- **"Autoimmune diseases run in packs"**: a patient with Type 1 diabetes is taught to be more likely to develop Hashimoto's, lupus, or rheumatoid arthritis than the general population — explicit teaching point not currently in the KB. [958–961]
- Statistic cited: roughly 8–9 out of 10 people have insulin resistance. [858]
- **Interstitial cystitis is named as an inflammatory condition**, often responsive to hormone/estrogen therapy — not currently listed among inflammatory conditions in the KB. [866–869]
- **A1/A2 milk protein distinction**: some patients tolerate farm-raised, local raw milk with minimal issues, framed as an A1/A2 casein consideration for dairy-sensitive patients. [829–832]

## 4. MTHFR / Homocysteine — Added Practice Points

- **Practice philosophy stated explicitly**: treat every patient as if they are MTHFR-positive — i.e., prescribe methylated folate/B-vitamins even when homocysteine is normal or genetic status is unknown, specifically to avoid having to track who's positive vs. negative. [1041–1050]
- Elevated homocysteine + a history of **2–3 pregnancy losses** → consider genetic counseling and/or an MMA + MTHFR genetic panel. Can be run cash-pay through **RUPA** relatively inexpensively if insurance won't cover genetic testing. [1054–1065]
- **Billing pearl**: Medicare will *not* cover a homocysteine level unless a hypertension diagnosis code is used on the order — remove homocysteine from Medicare orders without an HTN diagnosis, or let the patient refuse it at the draw. [1070–1076]
- General patient-autonomy teaching point: explicitly tell patients they have permission to refuse any lab at the draw that turns out to be unexpectedly expensive. [1079–1087]

## 6. Absorption & Supplement Counseling (new topic area)

- **Coffee impairs supplement absorption**: caffeine specifically impairs iron, magnesium, and calcium absorption. Counsel patients *against* taking vitamin D (or other supplements) with morning coffee-and-butter; move dosing to a real meal instead (e.g., lunch) if that's when they actually eat. [1137–1152, 1180–1186]
- General absorption principle: the body preferentially digests/absorbs nutrients delivered with actual food — taking a pill with only a drink triggers little digestive (acid/bile) response, whereas taking it with a meal or even a small snack improves absorption. "Hide" supplements in bites of food. [1189–1202]
- **Vitamin C pharmacokinetics pearl**: a single dose of vitamin C is used up by the body in roughly **60–90 minutes**. This is given as the rationale for why a single small daily dose (studies cited showing ~50–60 mg is enough for basic iron absorption) is inadequate for Hashimoto's/anti-inflammatory antibody effects — high-dose vitamin C needs to be spread across the day, dosed as many times as tolerated up to loose stool. [1213–1239]
- Preferred vitamin C form: **plain ascorbic acid** (easiest to dose). Patient compliance hack mentioned: buy bulk ascorbic acid powder plus a capsule-filling tray so patients can pack more than 1,000 mg per capsule for easier high-dose compliance. [1362–1391]

Not previously called out as its own scenario in the KB. [1493–1505]
- Pushes back directly on standard "add fiber/vegetables" advice given to IBS/Crohn's patients — describes roughage (broccoli, corn, raw vegetables) as acting like a "bottle brush" scrubbing an already-inflamed gut lining. Illustrative case: a new-intake patient's daily "huge salad," adopted after seeing a TikTok/YouTube claim it would fix her IBS, was actually worsening her IBS. [1506–1548]
- Salt guidance nuance beyond the existing "2 tsp/day" rule: should be **spread across the day, never all at once**; manual laborers, yard workers, or anyone sweating heavily in summer need more than 2 tsp/day. [1560–1573]
- **Pink Himalayan salt contains no iodine** — confirm with patients whether their salt source is iodized before assuming adequate dietary iodine intake. [1550–1554]
- Specific electrolyte brand recommendations named: **Element** and **Redmond's** (Redmond's discount code "joyful," ~10–15% off). [1654–1660]

## 8. Hyperthyroid/Graves' Symptomatic Management (new topic area — not in current Hashimoto's KB file)

Rationale given: propranolol causes significant fatigue and requires multiple daily doses, which patients don't tolerate well. [1293–1303] (not found in slides — remains unverified: this deck does not cover hyperthyroid/Graves' medication management)
- Methimazole: typical starting dose **10–15 mg once daily**; can move to BID dosing if insufficient; recheck thyroid labs in **6–8–10 weeks** while titrating; often reducible to 5–10 mg once daily after initial control is achieved. [1303–1325]
- **Safety point**: get symptomatic hyperthyroid patients on a beta blocker as soon as possible regardless of comfort prescribing methimazole — hyperstimulation carries cardiac dysfunction/MI risk. If uncomfortable prescribing methimazole, refer to endocrinology, but don't delay starting the beta blocker. [1310–1318]
- Reminder pearl: **weight loss (not weight gain)** is associated with hyperthyroidism; patients frequently misuse "hyper" vs. "hypo" terminology, so verify rather than assume based on the patient's own word choice. [1338–1344]

## 9. LDN — Items Not Duplicative of the Existing Dosing Protocol (cross-referenced to 08-methylene-blue-ivm-ldn)

- **Duration-of-therapy expectation**: ideal LDN use is described as being on the order of a **couple of years**; an 8–10 week course "is not going to fix anything." [1876–1881]
- **New contraindication/caution**: patients on chronic opioid pain management are poor LDN candidates because LDN and opioids act on the same mu/kappa receptors — unless the patient is actively trying to wean off opioids. [1795–1799]
- **Pregnancy/fertility caution**: LDN is not used for fertility treatment due to insufficient safety data in pregnancy. Safer options mentioned for fertility/weight concerns in this population: grain-free diet, seed-oil elimination, inositol, progesterone, and ⚠️ (garbled — possibly "metformin") for OB patients. [1980–2002] (not found in slides — remains unverified: the deck does not cover LDN/fertility topics)
- **LDN for weight loss / Contrave nuance**: Contrave = LDN + bupropion (Wellbutrin) at a fixed 8 mg dose; the lack of a slow titration (vs. compounded LDN) is given as the reason patients on Contrave report feeling "loopy"/drugged (opioid receptor activation). Preferred approach: compound LDN separately with gradual titration (1 mg/2 mg/4 mg) before transitioning to Contrave — cheaper and better tolerated. [1891–1915]
- Weight-loss LDN compounding combinations mentioned: LDN + inositol + B12 + ALA, or LDN + saffron + inositol. Practitioner has moved away from combining LDN with **topiramate** after 2–3 patients had significant side effects. [1920–1934]
- **LDN should not be added simply because a patient has plateaued on a GLP-1** — investigate root cause first (diet, gut function, thyroid); if plateau lasts longer than 4–6 weeks, re-run labs (thyroid, iron, protein status) rather than adding LDN. [1940–1955]
- **GLP-1 plateau troubleshooting pearl**: plateaus are often due to constipation from severely slowed gut motility on a GLP-1 — a short laxative "reset" (e.g., Milk of Magnesia) can produce a 2–3 lb "whoosh" and restart progress. Also watch for appetite suppression so severe that inadequate protein/fat intake triggers metabolic "hoarding" — ensure adequate protein/fat intake while on a GLP-1. [2014–2035]
- Additional GLP-1-adjacent probiotic products named (not previously in KB): a GLP-1-supportive probiotic, and **Akkermansia by Pendulum**, used interchangeably depending on the patient. [2009–2012]
- LDN's condition list is expanded beyond what's cross-referenced in the 03 KB: also mentioned here for **gut problems, multiple sclerosis, Parkinson's, depression, and PTSD/TBI** (cited to VA research). [1737–1743]
